01

Efficient Portfolios with Many Stocks

With two stocks, the efficient frontier is a single curve in risk-return space. When we add more stocks, the frontier expands — every new asset, even one with an inferior standalone risk-return profile, potentially improves the frontier by offering additional diversification. This insight leads to a powerful conclusion: to achieve the best possible risk-return trade-off, we should hold every available risky asset. The efficient frontier ultimately encompasses all tradable investments.

Consider the three-stock universe of Intel, Coca-Cola, and Bore Industries. Bore Industries has a 2% expected return and 25% volatility — inferior to Coca-Cola in expected return, with the same risk. Should any rational investor hold Bore Industries? The initial intuition says no — but it is wrong.

Three-stock universe — assumptions
StockE[R]σρ_Intelρ_Cokeρ_Bore
Intel26%50%1.000.000.00
Coca-Cola6%25%0.001.000.00
Bore Industries2%25%0.000.001.00
Bore Industries has the same risk as Coca-Cola but far lower return. All three are uncorrelated with each other (ρ=0). Yet because Bore is uncorrelated with everything else, adding even a small allocation provides pure diversification benefit with no correlation drag.
Why Bore Industries still improves the frontier
  • Adding Bore to a Coca-Cola + Intel portfolio creates a new set of feasible combinations — the frontier shifts left (lower volatility achievable)
  • Even though Bore's own return is only 2%, its zero correlation with both Intel and Coke means it adds pure diversification — reducing portfolio variance without the constraint of correlated movements
  • A tiny allocation to Bore (e.g., 5%) can reduce portfolio variance noticeably while only slightly reducing expected return
  • The general principle: every asset with imperfect correlation with the existing portfolio improves the frontier, regardless of its standalone Sharpe ratio
Key: the question is never "is this asset good in isolation?" but "does it improve the portfolio?"
Efficient frontier — expanding from 2 stocks to 3 stocks (Intel + Coke + Bore)
All correlations = 0 · Short sales allowed
Intel + Coke only 3-stock efficient frontier Individual stocks
The 3-stock frontier (green) lies to the left of the 2-stock frontier (blue) — adding Bore achieves lower volatility for the same expected return. The shaded region represents all feasible 3-stock portfolios including short sales.
The expanding frontier principle
Each time we add a new stock (even an apparently inferior one), the efficient frontier either stays the same or improves. It can never worsen, because we can always assign zero weight to the new asset and recover the old frontier exactly.
Adding N stocks creates a region of possibilities, not just a curve
From 3 stocks to the whole market
With 10 stocks, the frontier improves significantly over 3 stocks. With 500 S&P stocks, it improves further. The logical endpoint: include all risky assets in the world. That frontier is the best possible risk-return combination achievable through diversification.
Optimal diversification → include every available risky investment
Practical implication
No single stock, on its own, is ever on the efficient frontier when many stocks are available. The relevant question for any asset is not "what is its Sharpe ratio?" but "what is its marginal contribution to portfolio risk?" — measured by its beta with the portfolio.
Firm-specific risk can always be diversified away at no cost
02

Risk-Free Saving, Borrowing & Margin Investing

The efficient frontier we have built considers only risky assets. But investors also have access to a risk-free asset (e.g., T-Bills) — one whose return is known with certainty, has zero volatility, and is uncorrelated with every risky asset. Including the risk-free asset changes the entire shape of the investment opportunity set: the efficient frontier becomes a straight line. This has profound implications for how all investors should structure their portfolios.

Expected Return — Risky + Risk-Free Blend
E[R_xP] = (1−x)rƒ + x·E[Rₚ] = rƒ + x(E[Rₚ]−rƒ)
x = fraction invested in risky portfolio P · (1−x) = fraction in risk-free
Expected return = risk-free rate + x × portfolio risk premium
x > 1 means borrowing at rƒ to invest more than 100% in P (margin/leverage)
Volatility — Risky + Risk-Free Blend
SD(R_xP) = x · SD(Rₚ)
Because Var(rƒ) = 0 and Cov(rƒ, Rₚ) = 0:
SD = √(x²·Var(Rₚ)) = x·SD(Rₚ)
Risk scales linearly with fraction x invested in the risky portfolio

Both expected return and volatility are linear functions of x. This means every combination of the risk-free asset and any risky portfolio traces a straight line in risk-return space — from the risk-free rate (when x=0) through the risky portfolio (when x=1) and beyond (when x>1, leveraged). The key insight: combining the risk-free rate with any risky portfolio traces a line, and we want the line with the steepest possible slope.

Margin investing — Example 1
$10k cash + borrow $10k at 5% → invest $20k in portfolio Q (E[R]=10%, σ=20%). So x=200%.
E[R] = 5% + 2×(10%−5%) = 15%
SD = 2×20% = 40%
If Q rises 30%:Return = +55%
If Q falls 10%:Return = −25%
The range of outcomes doubles: 55%−(−25%) = 80% vs 30%−(−10%) = 40% for unlevered Q. Leverage doubles both expected return above rƒ and volatility. The risk-return trade-off is linear and symmetric.
Margin investing: higher expected return but proportionally higher risk
Why the volatility formula simplifies
  • T-Bills have a known, fixed return — their variance is literally zero
  • T-Bills are uncorrelated with risky assets — their covariance with any risky portfolio is zero
  • These two properties eliminate the last two terms of the two-asset variance formula: Var(R_xP) = x²·Var(Rₚ)
  • Taking the square root: SD = x·SD(Rₚ) — risk scales one-for-one with the fraction invested in risky assets
Risk-return combinations — risk-free asset + risky portfolio P (straight line)
rƒ=5% · E[Rₚ]=15% · σₚ=25% · x ranges from 0 to 200%
x ∈ [0,1]: saving (lending at rƒ) x > 1: borrowing (margin investing) Portfolio P (x=100%)
The combination line is perfectly straight because both return and risk are linear in x. The slope of the line is the Sharpe Ratio of portfolio P: (E[Rₚ]−rƒ)/SD(Rₚ).
03

The Tangent Portfolio & Sharpe Ratio

Different risky portfolios on the efficient frontier, when combined with the risk-free asset, produce different straight lines. The steeper the line, the better — for any level of volatility, a steeper line means a higher expected return. The optimal risky portfolio is the one that maximises the slope of this line: the portfolio with the highest Sharpe Ratio. Geometrically, it is the point where a line from the risk-free rate is tangent to the efficient frontier.

Sharpe Ratio — the Slope of the Capital Market Line
Sharpe Ratio = (E[Rₚ] − rƒ) / SD(Rₚ)
= portfolio excess return / portfolio volatility
= reward per unit of risk taken
The tangent portfolio maximises the Sharpe Ratio over all risky portfolios

The Sharpe Ratio answers: how much excess return do I earn per unit of risk I bear? Maximising it is equivalent to finding the steepest line from the risk-free rate to the efficient frontier — the tangent point. All investors should hold this portfolio of risky assets, regardless of their risk tolerance. Their risk preference determines only how much they invest in it versus the risk-free asset.

Universal portfolio choice — the separation theorem
  • Conservative investor: invests fraction x < 1 in the tangent portfolio + (1−x) in T-Bills → low volatility, moderate return (to the left of T on the CML)
  • Aggressive investor: invests fraction x > 1 in the tangent portfolio (borrowing at rƒ) → high volatility, high return (to the right of T on the CML)
  • Both hold the same risky portfolio — they only differ in how much leverage they use
  • This is the two-fund separation theorem: every efficient portfolio is a combination of the risk-free asset and the unique tangent portfolio T
Tangent portfolio T is the unique optimal portfolio of risky assets — valid for ALL investors
Example 2 — Optimal portfolio choice
Uncle's current portfolio P: E[R]=10.5%, σ=8%, rƒ=5%. Tangent portfolio T: E[R]=18.5%, σ=13%.
Goal 1: Maximise return, keep σ=8%
x = 8%/13% = 61.5% in T
→ $61,500 in T + $38,500 in T-Bills
→ E[R] = 5% + 0.615×(13.5%) = 13.3% (up from 10.5%)
Goal 2: Minimise risk, keep E[R]=10.5%
5% + x×13.5% = 10.5% → x = 40.7%
→ $40,700 in T + $59,300 in T-Bills
→ σ = 40.7%×13% = 5.29% (down from 8%)
In both cases, the optimal portfolio is a blend of T-Bills and the tangent portfolio. Portfolio P is dominated — it lies below the capital market line.
Identifying the tangent portfolio — multiple Sharpe ratio lines vs efficient frontier
rƒ=5% · Tangent point maximises the slope (highest Sharpe Ratio)
Efficient frontier (risky only) Capital Market Line (CML) — steepest line through rƒ Sub-optimal lines from rƒ through other portfolios Tangent portfolio T
Every line from rƒ that passes through a portfolio on the efficient frontier has the same slope as that portfolio's Sharpe Ratio. The CML is the one tangent line that touches — and doesn't cross — the frontier. All other lines are less steep.
04

The Capital Asset Pricing Model

The tangent portfolio maximises the Sharpe Ratio — but to identify it, we need expected returns, volatilities, and correlations for every asset. These are nearly impossible to estimate precisely. The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) sidesteps this problem entirely: under three key assumptions, rational investors collectively drive the market to an equilibrium where the tangent portfolio equals the market portfolio — the value-weighted portfolio of every tradeable security.

Three Assumptions
The Market Portfolio
CAPM Derivation
Assumption 1 — Perfect markets
Investors can buy and sell all securities at competitive market prices without taxes or transaction costs. They can borrow and lend at the same risk-free rate rƒ. This means capital flows frictionlessly to wherever it earns the highest risk-adjusted return.
No taxes · No transaction costs · Same borrowing/lending rate for all
Assumption 2 — Efficient portfolios only
Every investor holds only efficient portfolios — the combination of the risk-free asset and the tangent portfolio that maximises their expected return for a given level of volatility. No investor knowingly holds an inefficient portfolio. This is what the previous section established as the rational choice.
All investors are mean-variance optimisers · No irrationality
Assumption 3 — Homogeneous expectations
All investors have the same estimates of expected returns, volatilities, and correlations for every security. Because everyone uses identical inputs, everyone computes the same efficient frontier and identifies the same tangent portfolio. There is no "agreeing to disagree."
Same beliefs → same tangent portfolio → same market outcome
These assumptions are strong and don't fully describe real investor behaviour. But the CAPM's qualitative insights — that market risk is the only priced risk, and beta is its measure — are so compelling that the model remains the most widely used risk-return framework in finance.
— Capital Asset Pricing Model · Foundations

Under CAPM assumptions, every investor holds the same risky portfolio — the tangent portfolio. But when we aggregate all investors' holdings, the total holdings of each security must equal its total outstanding supply (its market capitalisation). Therefore, the tangent portfolio must be the value-weighted market portfolio.

Example 3 — market cap implication
You hold $10k in Microsoft and $5k in Pfizer. Your friend holds $2k in Pfizer. Both hold efficient portfolios.
  • Your ratio: Microsoft/Pfizer = $10k/$5k = 2:1
  • Your friend must also hold 2:1 → so $4k in Microsoft
  • If all investors hold the same proportions, then Microsoft's market cap must be 2× Pfizer's market cap
  • Generalising: the proportion of each stock in the tangent portfolio equals its share of total market capitalisation
Tangent portfolio = market portfolio (by aggregation of all investors' portfolios)
What the market portfolio contains
  • In theory: all tradeable risky assets — stocks, bonds, real estate, commodities, human capital
  • In practice: approximated by broad stock market indices (S&P 500, MSCI World, FTSE All-World)
  • Weight of each asset = market cap / total market cap
  • Large-cap stocks (Apple, Microsoft) have larger weights; micro-caps have tiny weights
  • The market portfolio is always fully invested — no cash holding
β_market = 1.0 by definition · β_risk-free = 0 by definition
Why beta on the CML equals x (the fraction in market portfolio)
On the CML, a portfolio holds fraction x in the market (β=1) and (1−x) in T-Bills (β=0). The portfolio's beta = x×1 + (1−x)×0 = x. This is why beta and the CML fraction are interchangeable — investing x% in the market gives β=x. This is the bridge between the CML and the SML.

With the market portfolio as the efficient portfolio, we can price any individual security. The CAPM says: the expected return of any security equals the risk-free rate plus a risk premium proportional to its beta. Beta measures how much market risk the security carries — the only risk investors need to be compensated for, since all firm-specific risk can be diversified away.

CAPM Equation — Security Expected Return
E[Rᵢ] = rƒ + βᵢ × (E[Rmkt] − rƒ)
rƒ = risk-free rate · βᵢ = security's beta with the market
E[Rmkt] − rƒ = market risk premium (historically ~5–8%)
Beta Definition
βᵢ = Cov(Rᵢ, Rmkt) / Var(Rmkt)
= SD(Rᵢ) × Corr(Rᵢ, Rmkt) / SD(Rmkt)
Measures the fraction of security i's volatility that is correlated with the market
Only this "systematic" portion is priced — firm-specific risk earns no premium
Why only market (systematic) risk is priced
  • Systematic risk (market risk) — moves with the overall economy; cannot be diversified away no matter how many stocks you hold (e.g., recessions affect all firms)
  • Idiosyncratic risk (firm-specific) — affects only one company (e.g., a CEO scandal, a product recall); disappears as you hold more stocks
  • In a competitive market, investors are only rewarded for risks they must bear. Since idiosyncratic risk can be diversified at zero cost, no risk premium is paid for holding it
  • Beta measures the systematic component only — the correlation with the market weighted by relative volatility
Firm-specific risk → diversifiable → zero risk premium · Systematic risk → non-diversifiable → priced via beta
05

Beta — Measuring Systematic Risk

Beta is the single most important number in the CAPM. It quantifies a security's sensitivity to market-wide movements — for every 1% the market moves, a stock with β=1.5 tends to move 1.5%, and one with β=0.5 tends to move only 0.5%. Beta determines the security's required return: high-beta stocks must offer higher expected returns to compensate investors for their amplified exposure to market risk.

Beta interpretation guide
Beta (β)InterpretationExample sectors
β = 0Uncorrelated with market (risk-free)T-Bills
0 < β < 1Less volatile than marketUtilities, Consumer staples
β = 1Moves exactly with marketMarket portfolio
β > 1More volatile than marketTech, Financials, Cyclicals
β < 0Moves opposite to marketGold (sometimes), short positions
Example 4 — Campbell Soup beta & CAPM return
σ_Campbell26%
Corr(Campbell, Mkt)0.33
σ_Market16%
β = (26% × 0.33) / 16% = 0.54
rƒ4%
E[Rmkt]10%
E[R_CPB] = 4% + 0.54×(10%−4%) = 7.24%
Same as investing 54% in market + 46% in T-Bills = same β = 0.54
Campbell Soup moves only 54% as much as the market — it is a defensive stock. The required return (7.24%) reflects only this systematic exposure. Any additional firm-specific risk (food safety scares, management changes) earns no extra return because it can be diversified away.
Beta — stock return vs market return (scatter with regression line)
β = slope of regression line
β=1.5 (aggressive — amplifies market) β=1.0 (market) β=0.5 (defensive — dampens market)
CAPM expected return vs beta (rƒ=4%, E[Rmkt]=10%)
E[R] = 4% + β×6% · Market risk premium = 6%
This IS the Security Market Line (SML). Every security should plot on this line under CAPM. Securities above the line are underpriced (positive alpha); below are overpriced (negative alpha). The line passes through rƒ at β=0 and through the market return at β=1.
06

The Security Market Line & Alpha

The Security Market Line (SML) is the graphical representation of the CAPM equation. It plots every security's expected return against its beta. Under the CAPM, all securities and portfolios must lie on this line — if they don't, an arbitrage opportunity exists. The SML is the second major diagram in portfolio theory (alongside the CML), and the two are fundamentally different: the CML uses volatility on the x-axis; the SML uses beta.

The CML shows the highest attainable expected return for each level of total volatility — it only applies to efficient portfolios (combinations of the market and risk-free asset). The SML applies to every individual security and portfolio: it shows the required expected return for a given level of systematic risk (beta). Individual stocks lie to the right of the CML (they carry diversifiable risk too), but they should all lie on the SML.

Alpha (α) — deviations from the SML
In practice, securities often deviate from the SML. The deviation is called alpha (α):
α = Actual return − CAPM expected return
= E[Rᵢ] − [rƒ + βᵢ(E[Rmkt]−rƒ)]
α > 0 (above SML)Underpriced → buy
α = 0 (on SML)Fairly priced
α < 0 (below SML)Overpriced → sell/avoid
Under the CAPM, in equilibrium no security can persistently have α≠0 — investors would immediately exploit the mispricing. In practice, finding securities with consistent positive alpha is the goal of active management (and extremely difficult).
CAPM implies all α = 0 in equilibrium · Active managers seek α > 0

rƒ=4%, E[Rmkt]=10%, market risk premium=6%
Security Market Line (SML) Securities on SML (fairly priced) Above SML (positive α — underpriced) Below SML (negative α — overpriced)
Example 5 — Portfolio beta & expected return
Equal-weight portfolio: Pfizer (β=0.50) + Home Depot (β=1.25). rƒ=4%, E[Rmkt]=10%.
Method 1: Via individual stock expected returns
E[R_PFE] = 4%+0.50×6% = 7.0%
E[R_HD] = 4%+1.25×6% = 11.5%
E[R_P] = ½×7.0% + ½×11.5% = 9.25%
Method 2: Via portfolio beta
β_P = ½×0.50 + ½×1.25 = 0.875
E[R_P] = 4% + 0.875×6% = 9.25% ✓
Both methods give exactly the same answer — this confirms that the SML applies to portfolios just as it does to individual securities. Portfolio beta is the weighted average of component betas.

All formulas — exam reference
  • E[R_xP] = rƒ + x(E[Rₚ]−rƒ) — expected return of x fraction in risky portfolio + (1−x) in T-Bills
  • SD(R_xP) = x·SD(Rₚ) — volatility scales linearly with fraction in risky portfolio (T-Bills add no risk)
  • Sharpe Ratio = (E[Rₚ]−rƒ)/SD(Rₚ) — reward per unit of volatility; tangent portfolio maximises this
  • CAPM: E[Rᵢ] = rƒ + βᵢ(E[Rmkt]−rƒ) — required return = rƒ + scaled market risk premium
  • βᵢ = Cov(Rᵢ,Rmkt)/Var(Rmkt) — beta = systematic risk relative to the market variance
  • βᵢ = SD(Rᵢ)×Corr(Rᵢ,Rmkt)/SD(Rmkt) — alternative beta formula using correlation
  • βₚ = Σxᵢβᵢ — portfolio beta is the weighted average of component betas
  • α = Actual return − CAPM return — deviation from SML; positive α = underpriced; negative α = overpriced
  • CML: E[R] = rƒ + (E[Rmkt]−rƒ)/σ_mkt × σ — the efficient frontier with risk-free asset; applies only to efficient portfolios
  • On CML: β = x (fraction in market portfolio) — because β_mkt=1 and β_rƒ=0, blending them gives β=x
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • CML ≠ SML — CML uses total volatility (σ) on x-axis and applies only to efficient portfolios; SML uses beta and applies to every security
  • High total volatility ≠ high required return — only systematic risk (beta) is priced; a stock with high σ but low β earns a low premium because its risk is diversifiable
  • Adding a low-return stock can still improve your portfolio — if it has low/negative correlation with existing holdings, it reduces portfolio variance without a proportional return cost
  • The market portfolio is NOT the S&P 500 — theoretically it includes all risky assets worldwide; in practice the S&P 500 or MSCI World is used as an approximation
  • All investors hold the same risky portfolio (under CAPM) — not the same total portfolio; risk aversion determines how much goes in the risk-free asset, but everyone's risky component is the market portfolio
  • β = 1 does NOT mean 0% risk premium — β=1 earns the full market risk premium E[Rmkt]−rƒ; only β=0 earns zero risk premium (the risk-free rate)
  • CAPM gives a required return, not a guaranteed return — it says what investors must expect to hold the stock in equilibrium; actual realised returns will differ
  • Negative beta is possible — gold and volatility instruments sometimes have negative betas; they earn an expected return BELOW rƒ because they provide insurance against market downturns
STEP 1 — Expand the efficient frontier with all risky assets
Adding any imperfectly correlated asset improves the frontier
No matter how inferior a stock looks in isolation, if its correlation with the existing portfolio is below 1, it can reduce volatility. The optimal set of risky investments is all available securities — not just the highest Sharpe Ratio stocks. This leads naturally to the concept of the market portfolio.
STEP 2 — Include the risk-free asset; the efficient frontier becomes a line
Every combination of T-Bills and a risky portfolio traces a straight line
Because T-Bills have zero variance and zero covariance with risky assets, SD scales linearly with x. The investor's problem reduces to: which risky portfolio gives the steepest line from rƒ? That portfolio is the tangent portfolio — unique for all investors.
STEP 3 — Identify the tangent portfolio; it maximises the Sharpe Ratio
All investors hold the same risky portfolio (separation theorem)
Risk aversion determines only the mix between T-Bills and the tangent portfolio, not which risky portfolio to hold. Under CAPM assumptions, collective market equilibrium forces the tangent portfolio to equal the value-weighted market portfolio.
STEP 4 — Use CAPM to price any security's required return
E[Rᵢ] = rƒ + βᵢ(E[Rmkt]−rƒ) — one formula for every security
Beta measures systematic risk. Only systematic risk earns a premium — firm-specific risk is diversifiable and freely eliminable. The CAPM gives a practical, single-number cost of equity for any company or project, making it indispensable for capital budgeting (WACC) and equity valuation.
STEP 5 — Plot the SML; identify alpha to find mis-pricings
Every security should lie on the SML in equilibrium · α = deviation
Under CAPM, α=0 for all securities in equilibrium. In practice, securities deviate: positive alpha stocks appear undervalued (buy), negative alpha stocks overvalued (avoid). Active management is the pursuit of positive alpha — and CAPM is the benchmark against which that alpha is measured.
One-line synthesis — CAPM
The CAPM delivers one profound conclusion: in equilibrium, the only risk that earns a return is market risk — systematic, non-diversifiable exposure to the economy as a whole. Every other risk is idiosyncratic, eliminable at zero cost through diversification, and therefore unrewarded. Beta quantifies exactly how much market risk a security carries, and the Security Market Line translates that beta into a required return — the rate investors must expect to voluntarily hold the security in a well-diversified portfolio.
