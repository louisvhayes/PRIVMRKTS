01

What Private Equity Is Broadly Doing

At the highest level, private equity firms allocate capital to private businesses or private assets with the goal of increasing value over time and then realising that value through an exit or repayment event. But the industry is not one single model. Different firms focus on different stages of a company’s life, take different levels of control, and earn returns through very different mechanisms.

Some firms back founders when the company is still young. Others invest once the business is already proven and needs capital to scale. Traditional buyout firms acquire control of mature companies and work on operations, governance, acquisitions, and capital structure. Distressed firms step in when businesses are under pressure. Infrastructure firms own essential long-duration assets. Private credit firms lend rather than buy.

The easiest way to understand private equity is to see it as a set of specialised ownership and financing models that sit across the full lifecycle of a business.

The Main Types of Private Equity Firms

Venture Capital
VC firms invest in early-stage companies with high upside but high risk. They usually take minority stakes, back management teams before profitability, and try to help founders build products, hire talent, and raise future rounds.
Growth Equity
Growth investors back businesses that already have a working model and meaningful revenue. They often remain minority investors and provide capital for expansion, product development, acquisitions, and international growth.
Buyout Firms
Buyout funds acquire controlling stakes in established businesses, often with leverage. They are the classic private equity model: active board involvement, operational improvement, and eventual exit.
Distressed / Special Situations
These investors focus on troubled companies, stressed capital structures, or complicated situations where valuation dislocation creates opportunity. They often rely on restructuring expertise.
Infrastructure
Infrastructure funds invest in long-term assets such as energy, transport, telecom towers, and data infrastructure. Their appeal is usually steady, visible cash flow rather than rapid resale.
Private Credit
Private credit managers lend directly to companies. Their returns come mainly from interest income and structuring, not from equity appreciation, making them economically distinct from buyout funds.
03

What These Firms Actually Do Day-to-Day

Although every firm raises capital and looks for attractive investments, the work differs a lot by strategy. Venture firms spend a great deal of time sourcing founders, evaluating markets early, and helping startups with hiring, partnerships, and future fundraising. Their value-add is often network, judgment, and access.

Growth equity firms tend to work on scaling. They help businesses professionalise the go-to-market function, expand internationally, add product capability, or make selective acquisitions, usually without taking full control away from founders.

Buyout firms are usually the most hands-on in strategy, governance, resourcing, and capital structure. They take board seats, monitor performance closely, support management hiring, fund bolt-on M&A, and prepare companies for exit.

Typical buyout toolkit
  • Set the strategic plan and investment thesis
  • Improve governance through board control or strong reserved matters
  • Recruit CEOs, CFOs, or specialist operating talent
  • Use follow-on capital for acquisitions and expansion
  • Refinance debt or optimise the capital structure
  • Prepare the company for sale, IPO, or recapitalisation
Distressed investors
Distressed and special situations firms spend more time on downside analysis, debt documents, creditor negotiations, restructuring, and scenario planning. Their edge often comes from being able to navigate complexity that others avoid.
Private credit managers
Private credit firms focus on underwriting cash flows, downside protection, loan covenants, collateral, and repayment capacity. They may still influence the business, but their primary economic return is contractual rather than ownership-driven.

A single company may interact with multiple parts of this ecosystem over time. A startup may first be backed by venture capital, later raise growth capital, and eventually be sold to a buyout fund once it is large and cash-generative. In difficult periods, the same company might refinance through private credit or become a target for special situations capital.

The biggest practical distinction is how each firm creates value. Venture relies on backing exceptional future winners. Growth equity relies on scaling already-validated businesses. Buyouts combine control, governance, leverage, and execution. Distressed strategies rely on buying complexity cheaply and fixing it. Infrastructure relies on stable long-duration economics. Private credit relies on underwriting repayment and downside protection.

So while these are all often grouped under the umbrella of private equity or private markets, they are not interchangeable. They use different tools, hire different kinds of investors, and think about risk in very different ways.

06

The Core Takeaway

Bottom line
Private equity is best understood not as a single strategy but as a family of specialised investing models. Each type of firm plays a different role in financing, owning, scaling, stabilising, or lending to businesses — and the differences between them come down to stage, control, risk, and how value is actually created.
