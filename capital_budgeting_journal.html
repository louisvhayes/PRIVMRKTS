Forecasting Incremental Earnings

Capital budgeting is the process a firm uses to evaluate long-term investments and decide which ones to accept. The starting point is always the same: how will this project change the firm's earnings? We focus exclusively on incremental effects — the difference between the firm's financial position with the project versus without it. This seemingly simple principle has several important and sometimes counterintuitive implications.

Capital budget & capital budgeting
  • Capital budget — the list of projects and investments a company plans to undertake in the coming period; the output of the capital budgeting process
  • Capital budgeting — the systematic process of analysing alternative investments, estimating their cash flows, and deciding which ones to accept or reject
  • Incremental earnings — the change in the firm's earnings caused specifically by the investment decision; not total earnings, only the delta
  • Both revenue-affecting and cost-affecting projects fall under capital budgeting — the framework is identical
Pro-forma statements
  • Pro-forma financials — projected financial statements prepared using specific assumptions about the future; the primary tool for evaluating project economics
  • Used externally (investment proposals to investors/lenders) and internally (management decision-making)
  • May not comply with GAAP — they highlight economically relevant items, not accounting conventions
  • The key question pro-formas answer: what do revenues, costs, and profits look like if our assumptions are correct?
What incremental means in practice
  • Only include costs and revenues that change because of the project
  • Exclude sunk costs — money already spent regardless of the decision (e.g., feasibility study fees)
  • Include opportunity costs — the value of resources used that could have generated income elsewhere
  • Include project externalities — cannibalization of existing products, synergies with other divisions
  • Exclude financing costs — analyse the project on its own merits; financing is a separate decision

Key concept — unlevered net income
In capital budgeting, interest expense is excluded. The rationale: the project should be evaluated on its own operating merits, not on how it will be financed. Financing is a separate decision. This gives us unlevered net income — what the project earns as if it had no debt:
Unlevered NI = EBIT × (1 − τ_c)
= (Revenue − Costs − Depreciation) × (1 − 0.40)
EBIT is "earnings before interest and taxes." By taxing at the firm rate and excluding interest, we get the after-tax operating profit the project generates for all capital providers, independent of capital structure. This is the input to the FCF calculation.
Depreciation — non-cash but tax-deductible
Straight-line depreciation: $7.5m ÷ 5 years = $1.5m/year for years 1–5. Depreciation reduces taxable income (EBIT) even though no cash leaves the firm each year. Cash leaves once at year 0 (CapEx). The subsequent annual depreciation charges are accounting allocations, not cash outflows — but they generate a real tax saving of τ × D = 40% × $1.5m = $600k/year.
Year 5 still has depreciation ($1.5m) even after HomeNet revenues stop — creating a tax credit of $600k

Indirect Effects: Opportunity Costs & Cannibalization

1. Opportunity cost — lab space
The HomeNet lab occupies warehouse space Cisco could rent out for $200k/year. Even though no cash changes hands internally, this forgone rent is a real cost of using the space for HomeNet.
After-tax impact = $200k × (1 − 40%) = $120k/year reduction
The opportunity cost is treated as an additional SG&A expense increasing from $2.8m to $3.0m
The after-tax rate is used because the foregone rent would have been taxable income — so the real cost is net of tax. This is a critical principle: always evaluate opportunity costs on an after-tax basis to ensure consistency with the project's own after-tax earnings.
2. Cannibalization — lost router sales
25% of HomeNet buyers would have bought Cisco's existing $100 wireless router. This reduces incremental revenue:
Lost sales−$2.5m/yr
Saved COGS (at $60/unit)+$1.5m/yr
Net gross profit reduction−$1.0m/yr
Cannibalization is a classic project externality. It must be included because it is an incremental effect of the HomeNet decision — without HomeNet, those router sales continue. Including it reduces revenue to $23.5m and COGS to $9.5m.

Determining Free Cash Flow & NPV

Earnings are not cash flow. This distinction is critical. The incremental earnings forecast tells us how profit changes, but it is free cash flow (FCF) — not profit — that we discount to find NPV. The difference between earnings and FCF comes from three adjustments: adding back non-cash depreciation, subtracting capital expenditures, and subtracting increases in net working capital.

Free Cash Flow Formula
FCF = EBIT(1−τ) + Depreciation − CapEx − ΔNWC
EBIT(1−τ) = Unlevered Net Income (after-tax operating profit)
+ Depreciation: add back non-cash charge to convert earnings → cash
− CapEx: cash spent on physical assets (not in earnings)
− ΔNWC: cash tied up in working capital (not in earnings)
Equivalent direct form
FCF = (Revenue − Costs)(1−τ) − CapEx − ΔNWC + τ·Depreciation
The term τ·Depreciation is the depreciation tax shield
= 40% × $1,500 = $600k/yr for HomeNet
Depreciation expenses reduce taxes → real cash saving each year
Three adjustments: earnings → FCF
  • + Depreciation: depreciation reduced earnings but no cash left the firm in that year (cash left at CapEx in year 0). Adding it back converts earnings to cash basis.
  • − CapEx: cash spent buying equipment (year 0: $7.5m). Not in earnings because it is capitalised and depreciated over time. But the cash goes out immediately.
  • − ΔNWC: when the project starts, Cisco must fund receivables before collecting cash from customers. This working capital requirement is a cash use not reflected in earnings.
Net Working Capital — HomeNet
Receivables = 15% of sales = 15% × $23,500 = $3,525k
Payables = 15% of COGS = 15% × $9,500 = $1,425k
NWC = Receivables − Payables = $3,525 − $1,425 = $2,100k
HomeNet needs $2,100k of NWC from year 1. This is a cash outflow of $2,100k at year 1 (ΔNWC = +$2,100k). At year 5, when the project ends, receivables and payables unwind — NWC returns to zero, releasing $2,100k of cash (ΔNWC = −$2,100k at year 5).
NWC ties up $2.1m in years 1–4; recovered in full at year 5 when project winds down
Why CapEx ≠ Depreciation
  • CapEx ($7.5m) is a cash outflow at year 0 — money leaves the firm immediately to buy the equipment
  • Depreciation ($1.5m/yr) is a non-cash accounting charge spread over 5 years — it allocates the original cost over the asset's life but involves no additional cash payment
  • The FCF formula corrects for this: subtract CapEx (actual cash) and add back depreciation (non-cash reduction in earnings)
  • The net effect over 5 years: CapEx of $7.5m minus total depreciation added back of $7.5m = zero. Fully consistent.

Net Present Value & Investment Rules

With the FCF schedule in hand, we can now evaluate whether the project creates value. The Net Present Value (NPV) is the sum of all future free cash flows discounted to today at the project's cost of capital (WACC). It represents the incremental value the project creates for shareholders — the exact cash equivalent of undertaking the project.

Net Present Value
NPV = Σ FCF_t / (1+r)ᵗ for t=0 to n
r = WACC (weighted average cost of capital) — the required return for all investors
Discounts each year's FCF back to today using the project's blended cost of capital

The WACC is the rate that a company pays on average to all its security holders (debt and equity) to finance its assets. It blends the after-tax cost of debt and the cost of equity, weighted by their proportions in the capital structure. Using WACC as the discount rate correctly values the project independently of how it is financed.

HomeNet NPV at WACC = 12%
Year 0: −16,500 × 1.000(16,500)
Year 1: 5,100 × 0.8934,554
Year 2: 7,200 × 0.7975,740
Year 3: 7,200 × 0.7125,125
Year 4: 7,200 × 0.6364,576
Year 5: 2,700 × 0.5671,532
NPV+$5,027k
Taking HomeNet is equivalent to Cisco having an extra $5.027m in the bank today. PV of inflows = $21.527m vs upfront cost of $16.5m.
The NPV Investment Rule
  • Single project: Accept if NPV > 0 (better than doing nothing); reject if NPV < 0
  • Mutually exclusive projects: Take the project with the highest NPV — this is equivalent to receiving that NPV in cash today
  • The NPV rule is the gold standard — it always gives the correct answer when applied correctly
  • Alternative rules (IRR, payback) sometimes agree with NPV but can conflict — when they conflict, always follow NPV
  • NPV directly measures value creation in today's pounds/dollars — it has clear economic meaning
NPV = value created by project = PV(inflows) − PV(outflows) = $21.527m − $16.5m = $5.027m ✓
Terminal / continuation value
For projects with an indefinite life (e.g., business expansions), we cannot forecast FCF to infinity. Instead, we forecast over a finite horizon and add a terminal value at the end — an estimate of all remaining FCFs, typically using the Gordon Model:
TV_N = FCF_{N+1} / (WACC − g)
Then discount: PV(TV) = TV_N / (1+WACC)ᴺ
The terminal value often represents 60–80% of a project's total NPV in practice, making the assumed long-run growth rate g a critically sensitive input.
HomeNet NPV profile — NPV as a function of discount rate

The Internal Rate of Return

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the discount rate that sets a project's NPV exactly to zero. It is the project's own "break-even" cost of capital — the rate at which the present value of inflows exactly equals the present value of outflows. The IRR rule says: accept if IRR > cost of capital; reject if IRR < cost of capital.

IRR Definition
0 = Σ FCF_t / (1+IRR)ᵗ
No closed-form solution — must be found by iteration (trial-and-error) or numerical methods (Excel's IRR/RATE function)
For HomeNet: IRR = 24.1% (well above WACC of 12%)
IRR rule — HomeNet
IRR24.1%
WACC12.0%
IRR − WACC (spread)12.1% — accept
Since IRR (24.1%) > WACC (12%), accept the project. This is consistent with NPV > 0 — both rules agree here because all negative cash flows precede positive ones (conventional cash flow pattern).
When IRR conflicts with NPV — three failure modes
  • Delayed investments: if the initial cash flow is positive (e.g., selling a resource now), the NPV rule flips: accept when IRR < WACC. The IRR rule gives the wrong answer if applied naively.
  • Multiple IRRs: if cash flows change sign more than once (e.g., positive, then negative, then positive), there can be multiple IRRs or no real IRR at all. The NPV profile may cross the x-axis multiple times.
  • Mutually exclusive projects: the project with the higher IRR may not have the higher NPV if projects differ in scale, timing, or risk. Always compare NPVs — never choose between mutually exclusive projects on IRR alone.
Rule: when IRR and NPV conflict → always follow NPV
IRR failure mode — mutually exclusive projects with different scale
Project A (large, lower IRR) vs Project B (small, higher IRR)
■ Project A: higher NPV at low discount rates despite lower IRR  |  ■ Project B: higher IRR but lower NPV at typical discount rates  |  At WACC=10%, choose A (higher NPV) — not B (higher IRR)

Payback Rule & Profitability Index

Payback Rule
Profitability Index

The payback rule says: accept a project if its undiscounted cash flows recover the initial investment within a specified time threshold. It is widely used in practice due to its simplicity and intuitive appeal — how quickly do we get our money back?

Payback Period
Time until cumulative undiscounted FCF ≥ initial outlay
Three critical pitfalls
  • Ignores time value of money — treats $1 in year 1 the same as $1 in year 3; no discounting
  • Ignores post-payback cash flows — a project that pays back in 2 years but generates nothing thereafter beats one that pays back in 3 years but generates massive cash flows in years 4–20
  • Ad hoc threshold — the choice of "2 years" or "3 years" is arbitrary and not grounded in theory
Despite these flaws, many firms use payback as a quick screen — not a primary decision rule
HomeNet — payback period (cumulative undiscounted FCF)
Initial investment = $16,500k
HomeNet payback ≈ 3.0 years — cumulative FCF crosses $16.5m between year 2 and year 3. The dashed line marks the initial outlay.

When a firm faces a binding resource constraint (e.g., limited engineers, factory capacity, or capital), it cannot undertake all positive-NPV projects. The goal becomes choosing the combination of projects that maximises total NPV within the constraint. The Profitability Index (PI) ranks projects by NPV created per unit of scarce resource consumed.

Profitability Index
PI = NPV / Resource Consumed
= "Value created per pound/unit of scarce resource"
Sort projects by PI descending; accept from top until resource exhausted

PI limitations

  • PI fails with multiple resource constraints (e.g., both engineering headcount AND capital are limited) — requires linear programming
  • PI is only valid when the resource constraint is truly binding — if additional resources can be acquired, standard NPV ranking applies

Equivalent Annual Cost — Unequal Lives

The NPV rule requires modification when comparing mutually exclusive projects with different lifespans. A simple NPV comparison is misleading because a shorter-lived project will need to be replaced sooner — its lower NPV cost must be weighed against how long it lasts. The Equivalent Annual Cost (EAC) converts each project's total cost into a uniform annual figure, enabling fair comparison.

Equivalent Annual Cost
EAC = NPV / A_r^n
A_r^n = annuity factor = [1 − (1+r)^(−n)] / r
EAC converts the NPV of a cost stream into a constant annual cost
Pick the machine with the lower (less negative) EAC — cheapest per year
Machine comparison — which to choose?
Machine A upfront cost$4,000
Machine A annual operating cost$100/yr
Machine A lifespan10 years
Machine B upfront cost$1,000
Machine B annual operating cost$500/yr
Machine B lifespan5 years
Discount rate10%
Step-by-step EAC calculation
Machine A — 10-year life
NPV_A = −4,000 − 100 × A(10%,10) = −4,614.5
A(10%,10) = [1−1.10⁻¹⁰]/0.10 = 6.1446
EAC_A = −4,614.5 / 6.1446 = −$751/yr
Machine B — 5-year life
NPV_B = −1,000 − 500 × A(10%,5) = −2,895.4
A(10%,5) = [1−1.10⁻⁵]/0.10 = 3.7908
EAC_B = −2,895.4 / 3.7908 = −$763.8/yr
Decision: Choose Machine A (EAC = −$751/yr vs −$763.8/yr). Machine A costs $12.80 less per year over its life. The naive NPV comparison suggested B ($2,895) but this ignored that A lasts twice as long.

Break-Even & Sensitivity Analysis

The most difficult part of capital budgeting is estimating cash flows and the cost of capital. These estimates are inherently uncertain. Three tools help assess the importance of that uncertainty: break-even analysis identifies the threshold values below/above which the project fails; sensitivity analysis shows how NPV changes when one assumption varies; and scenario analysis changes multiple assumptions simultaneously.

Break-Even Analysis
Sensitivity Analysis

Break-even analysis finds the value of each input at which the project's NPV is exactly zero — the minimum performance required to justify the investment. If actual performance falls below break-even, the project destroys value. These thresholds give managers a clear picture of how much room for error they have.

The NPV line is linear in units sold. Every additional thousand units sold adds approximately $X to NPV. The break-even is where this line crosses zero.

Sensitivity analysis varies one input at a time while holding all others at their baseline values. It shows which assumptions drive the NPV most strongly — revealing where management attention and due diligence should be focused. Parameters with steep NPV-sensitivity slopes are the "key value drivers."

Marketing cost sensitivity example
Baseline SG&A: $3m/yr. Pessimistic: $4m/yr. How does NPV change?
ΔNI = −$1m × (1−40%) = −$0.6m/yr (after-tax)
ΔNPV = PV of −$0.6m for 4 years at 12% = −$1.82m
New NPV = $5.027m − $1.82m = $3.2m
The logic: a $1m increase in costs → EBIT falls $1m → tax falls $0.4m → FCF falls $0.6m after tax → PV of $0.6m/yr for 4 yrs at 12% = $1.82m reduction in NPV.
What sensitivity analysis reveals
  • Steep slope → small changes in that input cause large NPV swings → this is a key risk driver requiring careful estimation and monitoring
  • Flat slope → NPV is relatively insensitive to that input → errors here don't matter much
  • Guides where to invest in better information (market research, engineering studies, etc.)
  • Also guides project management focus — steep-slope parameters are the ones to monitor most closely once the project is underway

Scenario Analysis

Sensitivity analysis varies one parameter at a time. But in reality, multiple parameters often move together. A recession simultaneously reduces units sold, forces price cuts, and may increase costs — these effects are correlated. Scenario analysis captures this by defining complete alternative scenarios (e.g., optimistic, base case, pessimistic) where multiple parameters change simultaneously.

The scenario analysis for HomeNet examines three alternative pricing strategies, each with a different combination of price and expected volume. Because price and volume move together (lower price → more units, higher price → fewer units), this is precisely the kind of multi-parameter change that scenario analysis is designed to handle.

All formulas — exam reference
  • Unlevered NI = EBIT × (1−τ) — after-tax operating profit excluding financing costs
  • FCF = Unlevered NI + D − CapEx − ΔNWC — cash available to all investors each year
  • FCF = (Revenue−Costs)(1−τ) − CapEx − ΔNWC + τD — direct form; τD is the depreciation tax shield
  • NWC = Current Assets − Current Liabilities — cash tied up in operations; ΔNWC is the year-on-year change
  • NPV = Σ FCF_t/(1+r)ᵗ — sum of discounted FCFs; r = WACC
  • NPV rule: Accept if NPV > 0 · Highest NPV if mutually exclusive
  • IRR: solve 0 = Σ FCF_t/(1+IRR)ᵗ — accept if IRR > WACC; fails for multiple sign changes & mutually exclusive projects
  • PI = NPV / Resource consumed — rank by PI when facing resource constraint; accept from top until resource exhausted
  • EAC = NPV / A_r^n — where A_r^n = [1−(1+r)^(−n)]/r; choose lowest EAC for cost-minimisation decisions
  • TV = FCF_{N+1}/(WACC−g) — terminal value at date N for perpetually growing cash flows
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • Never include sunk costs — money already spent (feasibility studies, past R&D) is irrelevant to the investment decision; only future incremental costs matter
  • Always include opportunity costs — if a resource is being diverted from another use, its forgone value is a real cost even if no cash changes hands
  • Earnings ≠ FCF — always add back depreciation, subtract CapEx and ΔNWC; failing to do so can make a value-destroying project look profitable
  • Higher IRR ≠ better project — when choosing between mutually exclusive projects, always compare NPV; the higher-IRR project can have lower NPV if scales or timings differ
  • NWC investment is recovered — the ΔNWC cash outflow at project start is fully reversed at project end; forgetting the recovery understates NPV
  • Depreciation is real in terms of tax — while non-cash, depreciation creates a real cash saving via the tax shield (τ×D per year)
  • Payback ignores TVM — a project paying back in 2 years with $0 thereafter can lose to a 4-year payback project with huge late cash flows when NPV is computed properly
  • EAC assumes identical replacement — the EAC comparison is only valid if machines can be replaced by identical machines at end of life; if technology changes, more careful analysis is needed
STEP 1 — Build the incremental earnings forecast (pro-forma)
Revenues − COGS − SG&A − R&D − Depreciation = EBIT → Unlevered NI
Start with only incremental effects. Exclude sunk costs. Include opportunity costs and cannibalization. Exclude interest expense. Tax at the corporate rate. Depreciation reduces tax even though it is non-cash. A tax credit (negative tax) arises whenever EBIT is negative — valid if the firm has other taxable income to offset.
STEP 2 — Convert earnings to free cash flow
FCF = Unlevered NI + D − CapEx − ΔNWC
Three adjustments: (1) add back depreciation (non-cash); (2) subtract CapEx (cash not in earnings); (3) subtract increases in NWC (cash tied up in receivables etc.). The NWC investment is recovered at project end — don't forget to include the reversal.
STEP 3 — Compute NPV at the WACC
NPV = Σ FCF_t/(1+WACC)ᵗ — the gold standard
Discount each year's FCF at the WACC. Sum. A positive NPV means the project generates more value than it costs. NPV directly measures value created in today's dollars. For long-lived projects, add a terminal value at the forecast horizon using the Gordon Model.
STEP 4 — Cross-check with IRR; apply PI/EAC where needed
IRR, PI, EAC — useful supplements, not replacements for NPV
IRR is a useful break-even check — accept if IRR > WACC for conventional cash flows. But when projects are mutually exclusive or have unusual cash flow patterns, always rely on NPV. Use PI only under genuine resource constraints. Use EAC only when comparing mutually exclusive alternatives with different useful lives.
STEP 5 — Analyse uncertainty with break-even, sensitivity, and scenario analysis
Understand the range of outcomes, not just the base case
Break-even tells you how much the key inputs can deteriorate before the project fails. Sensitivity analysis tells you which inputs matter most for NPV. Scenario analysis tells you what happens when multiple inputs move together. These tools don't replace the base-case NPV — they tell you how much to trust it.
One-line synthesis — Capital Budgeting
Capital budgeting reduces to a single question: do the incremental free cash flows this project generates, discounted at the firm's cost of capital, exceed the investment required? When they do, NPV is positive and the project creates value. Every technique — FCF construction, NPV, IRR, EAC, break-even, sensitivity, and scenario analysis — is simply a different way of answering that same question with more or less precision, under more or less uncertainty.
