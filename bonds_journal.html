What is a Bond?

A bond is a legally binding agreement between a borrower (the issuer) and a lender (the bondholder) that can be traded as a security on financial markets. Unlike a bank loan, which is a private bilateral contract, a bond is a standardised instrument: once issued, it can be bought and sold freely, meaning the identity of the lender can change throughout the bond's life. The issuer's obligation — to pay coupons and repay the face value at maturity — remains fixed regardless of who holds the bond.

Corporate bonds — key features
  • Issued by companies to raise debt capital without diluting equity ownership
  • Carry credit risk — the risk the issuer defaults on coupon or principal payments
  • Credit spread = corporate yield − equivalent government bond yield; reflects default risk

Why bonds are tradeable — key properties
  • Standardised terms allow anonymous trading on secondary markets
  • Price changes daily with interest rates — giving bondholders capital gain/loss risk in addition to income
  • Bond investors can exit before maturity by selling to another investor at market price
  • Liquidity varies dramatically: Treasuries trade constantly; small corporate bonds may trade rarely

How to Value a Bond

The value of any financial security is the present value of its expected future cash flows, discounted at the rate the market requires for that level of risk. For a bond, the future cash flows are perfectly known in advance (for fixed-rate bonds): a stream of coupon payments plus the face value at maturity. The only unknown is the discount rate — the yield to maturity (y) — which the market sets based on current interest rates, credit risk, and bond characteristics.

General Bond Pricing Equation
Bond Price = Σ [C / (1+y)ᵗ] + F / (1+y)ᵀ
C = periodic coupon payment · y = yield to maturity · F = face value · T = periods to maturity
The first term is the PV of the annuity of coupons; the second is the PV of the lump-sum face value
Simplified using the Annuity Formula
Bond Price = C × [1 − (1+y)^(−T)] / y + F / (1+y)ᵀ
The first term collapses the coupon stream into the annuity PV formula · Second term discounts the face value
This is the most operationally useful form of the bond pricing equation

The two components of a bond's value are conceptually distinct:

Component 1 — PV of coupon stream (annuity)
The coupons form a fixed annuity — equal payments C at dates 1, 2, …, T. The PV of this annuity falls as yield rises (because the same cash flows are discounted more heavily). A bondholder who values income receives this component throughout the bond's life.
PV(coupons) = C × [1−(1+y)^(−T)] / y
Component 2 — PV of face value (terminal value)
The face value F is a single lump sum received at maturity T. Its present value shrinks dramatically as T increases or y rises. A 30-year ZCB at 6% discounts £100 to just £17.41 — illustrating how time and rates erode terminal value. This component is the bondholder's "return of principal."
PV(face value) = F / (1+y)ᵀ
Interest rates and bond prices move in opposite directions. When yields rise, you discount future cash flows more heavily, so the present value — and therefore the market price — of every bond falls. This is the most important single relationship in fixed income.

Premium, Par & Discount: The Three States of a Bond

Premium Bond — coupon rate > yield
The bond pays coupons that are more generous than what the market currently requires. To compensate, investors bid up the price above par — they pay extra upfront for the right to receive those above-market coupons. Over time, the premium amortises to par as maturity approaches.
Example: 6.375% coupon, 5% yield → Price = £110.62 (above £100 par)
Par Bond — coupon rate = yield
When the coupon rate exactly equals the market yield, the bond is priced at exactly par (face value). This is the equilibrium state: the bondholder receives a market-rate return entirely through coupons, with no capital gain or loss at maturity. Newly issued bonds are typically priced at or near par.
Example: 6.375% coupon, 6.375% yield → Price = £100.00 exactly
Discount Bond — coupon rate < yield
The coupons are below market rate. Investors are only willing to buy at below par so that the combination of below-market coupons and capital appreciation (from price to par at maturity) delivers the required yield. The discount compensates for inadequate income.
Example: 6.375% coupon, 9% yield → Price = £83.15 (below £100 par)
Core intuition — premium vs discount
The yield y is the rate that makes "what you pay today" exactly equal to the discounted value of "what you receive over the bond's life." If the coupon is high relative to the market, you must pay more today. If the coupon is low, you must pay less. The price adjusts until the bond's total return — coupons plus capital gain/loss — equals the market yield.
Price–Yield relationship — 6.375% coupon, 10-year bond, F=£100
Dashed lines mark par (y=6.375%, P=£100)
As yield rises from 1% → 15%, price falls from ~£147 to ~£57. The curve is convex — price falls faster at low yields than at high yields.

Types of Bonds

Coupon Bonds
Zero-Coupon Bonds
Consols (Perpetuities)

A coupon bond makes periodic interest payments — the coupons — throughout its life, plus repays the face value at maturity. It is the most common type of bond in global debt markets. Because the cash flows consist of a fixed annuity (the coupons) plus a terminal lump sum (the face value), the bond pricing formula is a direct application of TVM.

Coupon payments are typically made annually in Europe and semi-annually in the United States. When coupons are semi-annual, the stated annual coupon rate is divided by two, the YTM per period is halved, and the number of periods is doubled. This affects the effective annual yield calculation.

Coupon Bond Price — Annual Coupons
P = C × [1 − (1+y)^(−T)] / y + F / (1+y)ᵀ
C = F × coupon rate · T = years to maturity · y = annual YTM
Coupon Bond Price — Semi-Annual Coupons
P = (C/2) × [1−(1+y/2)^(−2T)] / (y/2) + F/(1+y/2)^(2T)
Divide coupon and yield by 2 · Double the number of periods
Coupon bond example — premium case
Bond: 6.375% coupon · F=100 · 10 years · annual · Required yield: 5%
P = 6.375/0.05 × [1−(1.05)^(−10)] + 100/(1.05)^10
= 6.375 × 7.722 + 100 × 0.6139 = 49.24 + 61.39 = £110.62
Coupon rate (6.375%) > yield (5%), so the bond trades at a premium of £10.62 above par. Investors pay extra today because they receive above-market coupons for 10 years.
Coupon bond example — discount case
Same bond but required yield is now 9%:
P = 6.375/0.09 × [1−(1.09)^(−10)] + 100/(1.09)^10
= 6.375 × 6.418 + 100 × 0.4224 = 40.91 + 42.24 = £83.15
Coupon rate (6.375%) < yield (9%), so the bond trades at a discount of £16.85 below par. The capital gain from £83.15 to £100 at maturity compensates for the below-market coupons.

A pure discount bond (or zero-coupon bond, ZCB) pays no periodic interest whatsoever. The entire return comes from the difference between the purchase price (always below par) and the face value received at maturity. The investor is lending money today and receiving a single payment at the end — a pure TVM problem.

Because there is only one cash flow, the ZCB's YTM is exactly equal to the spot rate for that maturity. This is a critically important property: zero-coupon bonds are the purest measurement tools for the term structure of interest rates. Every point on the spot rate curve is conceptually equivalent to the yield on a ZCB of that maturity.

Zero-Coupon Bond Price
P = F / (1 + y)ᵀ
F = face value · y = YTM = spot rate for maturity T · T = years to maturity
The bond must always sell below par — it can never sell at or above par because the only return is price appreciation
Zero-coupon bond — 30-year example

The investor pays £17.41 today and receives £100 in 30 years — a total return of 475% in nominal terms, but only 6% per year once time value is accounted for. The deep discount reflects the full weight of 30 years of compounding at 6%.
Treasury Bills are classic short-term ZCBs — sold at discount, mature at face value

A consol (or perpetual bond) is a bond with no maturity date. It pays a fixed coupon C every period forever — making it a classic example of a perpetuity. The pricing formula is the same as the perpetuity formula from TVM: the present value of a constant infinite stream of cash flows.

The UK government historically issued consols — perpetual gilts that paid a fixed coupon indefinitely with no redemption date. The last UK consols were finally redeemed in 2015, more than 300 years after some of the original issuances. Some were originally issued to consolidate debt from the South Sea Bubble of 1720.

Consol (Perpetual Bond) Price
P = C / y
C = fixed coupon payment per period · y = yield / discount rate
Note: same as the perpetuity PV formula — a consol IS a perpetuity

The consol formula reveals a stark sensitivity to the discount rate. A consol paying £5/year at a 5% yield is worth £100. If yields rise to 10%, it is worth only £50 — a 50% price loss from a doubling of rates. This extreme interest-rate sensitivity is why long-duration bonds (and consols in particular) are highly volatile instruments.

Consol example & interest rate sensitivity
A UK consol pays £5 per year forever. What is its price at different yields?
y = 2%P = 5/0.02 = £250
y = 5%P = 5/0.05 = £100
y = 10%P = 5/0.10 = £50
y = 20%P = 5/0.20 = £25
A yield doubling from 5% to 10% cuts the price in half. This inverse relationship is most extreme for consols because they have the longest possible duration — all future cash flows stretch infinitely into the future, maximising interest-rate sensitivity.
Duration of a consol = (1+y)/y. At 5% yield, duration ≈ 21 years
Why consols illustrate duration risk
  • Every cash flow is far in the future — all are heavily discounted, making price very sensitive to rate changes
  • No principal repayment to "anchor" value at a near-term maturity date
  • Illustrates why pension funds and insurers matching ultra-long liabilities need very long-duration assets

Yield to Maturity

The yield to maturity (YTM) is the single discount rate that makes the present value of all a bond's future cash flows exactly equal to its current market price. It is the internal rate of return (IRR) of the bond investment — the return an investor earns if they buy the bond today, hold it to maturity, and reinvest all coupons at the same rate. YTM is the most widely quoted measure of a bond's return.

YTM — Implicit Definition
P = C × [1−(1+y)^(−T)] / y + F/(1+y)ᵀ
Solve this equation for y given market price P, coupon C, face value F, maturity T
⚠ No closed-form solution in general — must use trial-and-error, interpolation, or Excel's RATE/YIELD function
How to solve for YTM — trial and error
  • Start with an educated guess: if P < F, then YTM > coupon rate (discount bond)
  • If P > F, then YTM < coupon rate (premium bond). Use this as your first bracket
  • Plug your guess into the pricing formula. If computed price > market price, guess a higher yield
  • If computed price < market price, guess a lower yield. Iterate until they converge
  • In Excel: use =YIELD() or =IRR() on the bond's cash flow stream
YTM with annual coupons — worked example
Bond: 10% annual coupon · 15 years · F=100 · Current price = 92.809
Step 1: Since P (92.809) < F (100), the bond is at a discount → YTM > 10% coupon rate.
92.809 = 10 × [1−(1+y)^(−15)] / y + 100/(1+y)^15
Solving by iteration: y = 11%
Verification: At y=11%: PV(coupons) = 10×7.191 = 71.91; PV(face) = 100/(1.11)¹⁵ = 20.90. Total = 92.81 ✓
YTM with semi-annual coupons — worked example
Bond: 10% annual coupon (semi-annual) · 20 years · F=100 · Price = 119.793
Since P (119.793) > F (100) → premium → YTM < 10% coupon rate. Semi-annual coupon = 5; periods = 40.
119.793 = 5 × [1−(1+y)^(−40)] / y + 100/(1+y)^40
Solving: y (per period) = 4% → Annual EAR = (1.04)² − 1 = 8.16%
The semi-annual yield of 4% converts to an effective annual yield of 8.16% — which is below the 10% coupon rate, consistent with a premium bond. Always convert semi-annual yields to EAR for fair comparison.

The Term Structure of Interest Rates

The term structure of interest rates (also called the yield curve) describes how bond yields change with maturity. Up until now, we have assumed a flat yield curve — the same discount rate for all maturities. In reality, yields vary substantially across maturities, and understanding the yield curve is one of the most important topics in fixed income and macroeconomics.

The yield curve is typically upward-sloping (normal): longer-term bonds yield more than shorter-term bonds. But the curve can also be flat, inverted (short yields above long yields — typically associated with recession expectations), or humped. Each shape carries different information about market expectations and risk premia.

What the yield curve's slope tells us
  • Normal (upward-sloping) — investors expect rates to rise, or demand extra compensation for holding long-term bonds. Most common shape in expansions.
  • Inverted (downward-sloping) — markets expect future short rates to fall, typically because a recession and central bank cuts are anticipated. Historically one of the best recession predictors.
  • Flat — transition state; market uncertain about rate direction. Often seen before curve inversions.
  • Humped — medium-term rates peak above both short and long rates. Seen in transitional monetary policy environments.
Term structure lessons from 2006–2008 (US)
  • 2006: Nearly flat/slightly inverted curve — Fed funds rate high (tight policy), markets beginning to price in future cuts as housing market stress emerged
  • 2007: Curve began to steepen — Fed started cutting short rates as credit crisis emerged; long rates initially less reactive
  • 2008: Aggressive steepening — Fed cut rates toward zero (ZLB); long rates fell less because investors revised long-run inflation and real rate expectations less violently than short-run path
  • Short maturities are dominated by central bank policy; long maturities by long-run inflation and real rate expectations

Spot Rates — Building Blocks of the Yield Curve

A spot rate r(T) is the interest rate today for a risk-free cash flow paid once at time T. Spot rates are the fundamental building blocks of bond pricing. Because a zero-coupon bond involves exactly one cash flow at maturity T, its YTM is precisely the spot rate for that maturity. Spot rates are therefore sometimes called "zero rates."

Spot Rate from Zero-Coupon Bond
P = F / (1 + r(T))ᵀ → r(T) = (F/P)^(1/T) − 1
The YTM of a ZCB is exactly the spot rate for maturity T
Example: £95 ZCB, F=£100, T=5 → r(5) = (100/95)^(1/5) − 1 = 1.031%
Spot rates vs YTM for coupon bonds
A coupon bond pays cash flows at many different dates — each needs its own spot rate for proper discounting. The single YTM quoted for a coupon bond is an average of the spot rates across its payment dates, weighted by the size of each cash flow. The YTM is a summary statistic, not a fundamental rate.
YTM = weighted average of spot rates r(1), r(2), …, r(T)
Coupon bond priced using spot rates — example
Bond: 4.5% annual coupon · F=100 · 3-year maturity
Spot rates: r(1)=3%, r(2)=4%, r(3)=5%
P = 4.5/1.03 + 4.5/1.04² + (4.5+100)/1.05³
= 4.369 + 4.160 + 90.271 = £98.80
Each cash flow is discounted at its own maturity's spot rate. The t=1 coupon uses r(1)=3%, t=2 uses r(2)=4%, and t=3 coupon plus face value uses r(3)=5%. This is the correct pricing method — using a single YTM would only be an approximation.
YTM of this bond ≈ 4.9% — not equal to any of the three spot rates; it is their weighted average

Forward Rates & No-Arbitrage

A forward rate is an interest rate agreed upon today for a loan that begins at a future date. Forward rates are not directly observed — they are implied by the spot curve through a no-arbitrage argument. The key insight is that two investment strategies leading to the same outcome at the same future date must yield the same return. If they did not, a riskless arbitrage profit would be available.

No-Arbitrage Condition — One-Year Forward Rate (T to T+1)
(1 + r_{T+1})^{T+1} = (1 + r_T)^T × (1 + f_{T,T+1})
Strategy A: invest for T+1 years at the (T+1)-year spot rate r(T+1)
Strategy B: invest for T years at r(T), then roll over for 1 year at forward rate f
Both must give the same terminal wealth — otherwise arbitrage exists
Solving for the Forward Rate
f_{T,T+1} = (1 + r_{T+1})^{T+1} / (1 + r_T)^T − 1
The forward rate is fully determined by the two spot rates r(T) and r(T+1)
General Forward Rate (T to T+N)
f_{T,T+N} = [(1+r_{T+N})^{T+N} / (1+r_T)^T]^{1/N} − 1
N-year forward rate starting at time T, implied by spot rates r(T) and r(T+N)
Forward rate intuition — the no-arbitrage logic
Suppose you want to invest for 2 years. You have two equivalent strategies:
  • Strategy A: Lock in the 2-year spot rate r(2) for the full 2 years today
  • Strategy B: Invest at the 1-year spot rate r(1) today, then at year 1 reinvest at whatever 1-year rate prevails
The forward rate f(1,2) is the rate in Strategy B's second year that makes both strategies give identical terminal wealth. If Strategy B's second-year rate were any different from f(1,2), you could earn a risk-free profit by simultaneously entering both strategies in opposing directions.
Forward rate = the implied future 1-year rate locked in by today's spot curve
Forward rate calculation — spot rates: r(1)=3%, r(2)=4%, r(3)=5%
No-arbitrage implied rates
Forward rate from Year 1 → Year 2
(1.04)² = (1.03) × (1 + f_{1,2})
f_{1,2} = 1.04²/1.03 − 1 = 1.0816/1.03 − 1 ≈ 5.01%
The market today implies that a 1-year loan starting in year 1 will cost approximately 5.01% — higher than today's 1-year rate of 3%, consistent with an upward-sloping spot curve.
Forward rate from Year 2 → Year 3
(1.05)³ = (1.04)² × (1 + f_{2,3})
f_{2,3} = 1.05³/1.04² − 1 = 1.1576/1.0816 − 1 ≈ 7.03%
The market implies a 1-year rate of 7.03% starting in year 2. The forward rates (5.01%, 7.03%) are higher than the spot rates (3%, 4%, 5%), which is always the case when the spot curve slopes upward.
Chaining: (1+r3)³ = (1+r1)(1+f12)(1+f23) = 1.03 × 1.0501 × 1.0703 = 1.1576 ✓
Spot rates vs implied forward rates — r(1)=3%, r(2)=4%, r(3)=5%
Forward rates always exceed spot rates on an upward-sloping curve
■ Spot rates: 3%, 4%, 5%  |  ■ Forward rates: 3%, 5.01%, 7.03% — the forward curve lies above the spot curve when spot is upward-sloping
Spot rates vs forward rates — the key relationship
  • Spot rate r(T) — the return from today to maturity T; a "starting now" rate
  • Forward rate f(T, T+1) — the implied 1-year rate starting at time T; a "future" rate
  • When the spot curve is upward-sloping, forward rates lie above spot rates (you must be compensated more in the future)
  • When the spot curve is inverted, forward rates lie below spot rates (markets expect rates to fall)
  • Forward rates are NOT forecasts — they include term premia in addition to rate expectations
Future spot rates — uncertainty & forward rates
  • Future spot rates are unknown today — they depend on future monetary policy, inflation, and macro outcomes
  • Under certainty, forward rates = future spot rates (no arbitrage + no risk premium)
  • In reality, forward rates = expected future spot rates + term premium
  • The term premium compensates investors for the risk of holding longer-dated bonds — even if they expect rates to stay flat
  • This is why forward rates overstate future short rates on average — the premium biases them upward

Yield Curve Theories

Three competing theories attempt to explain why the yield curve has the shape it does at any given time. Each theory has different implications for what long-term yields tell us about future short rates, and each has empirical support in different environments. Understanding all three is essential for interpreting yield curve signals in practice.

Expectations Theory
Liquidity Premium Theory
Market Segmentation

The Expectations Theory is the purest and most elegant explanation of the yield curve. It states that long-term yields are simply the geometric average of expected future short-term rates — with no additional risk premium for holding longer-term bonds. The yield curve's shape is entirely determined by the market's expectations for future monetary policy.

Expectations Theory — Approximate Relationship
y(T) ≈ (1/T) × Σ E[r_{t+k}] for k=0 to T−1
y(T) = T-year spot yield · E[r_{t+k}] = expected one-year rate k years from now
Long yield = simple average of expected future one-year rates
Implications of the Expectations Theory
  • Upward-sloping curve → markets expect future short rates to rise
  • Downward-sloping (inverted) curve → markets expect future short rates to fall — often signalling recession
  • Flat curve → markets expect short rates to remain roughly unchanged
  • Forward rates = unbiased forecasts of future spot rates (no term premium)
  • Investors are indifferent between long and short bonds of equal duration — perfect substitutes
Strengths & weaknesses
  • Strength: Elegant, simple, and explains why inverted curves predict recessions (rate cut expectations)
  • Strength: Consistent with the no-arbitrage forward rate formula
  • Weakness: Empirically, forward rates consistently overpredict future short rates — suggesting term premia exist
  • Weakness: Cannot explain why the yield curve is usually upward-sloping even when short rates are expected to remain flat
  • Weakness: Assumes investors are perfectly indifferent to maturity — unrealistic for pension funds and insurers
Numerical example
If the 1-year rate is 3% today and the market expects the 1-year rate in year 2 to be 5%:
y(2) ≈ (3% + 5%) / 2 = 4%
The 2-year spot rate should equal the average of expected 1-year rates
An upward-sloping 2-year spot rate of 4% (above the current 1-year rate of 3%) implies the market expects year-2 short rates to be about 5%.

The Liquidity Premium Theory (also called the Term Premium Theory) extends expectations theory by adding a risk premium for holding longer-term bonds. Long-term bonds expose investors to more interest-rate risk — if rates rise unexpectedly, their price falls more than short-term bonds. Rational, risk-averse investors demand extra compensation for this risk: the term premium (LP_T).

Liquidity Premium Theory
y(T) ≈ (1/T) × Σ E[r_{t+k}] + LP_T
LP_T > 0 and LP_{T+1} > LP_T (term premium is positive and increasing with maturity)
Long yields = expected short rates + compensation for holding long-dated bonds
Key implication — the most practically important theory
Because LP_T > 0, the yield curve tends to be upward-sloping even when expected short rates are flat. This explains the empirical regularity that the yield curve is upward-sloping most of the time — not because rates are always expected to rise, but because long bonds always command a risk premium.
Expected rates flat → curveUpward-sloping (LP effect)
Expected rates falling → curveFlat or slightly inverted
Steeply inverted curve → impliesSharp expected rate declines
Downward-sloping curve under LP theory — numerical example
Suppose: y(1)=4%, y(10)=3%, LP(10)=1%
3% ≈ (1/10) × Σ E[r_{t+k}] + 1%
→ Average expected short rate ≈ 2% over 10 years
Despite an inverted curve (10yr at 3% vs 1yr at 4%), the term premium of 1% means the market is actually pricing in an even sharper fall in short rates than the raw curve suggests. Expected future short rates of ~2% are well below today's 1-year rate of 4%. The inversion must overcome the LP to appear at all.
Inverted curve under LP theory → very strong expectation of rate cuts
Why long bonds have more interest-rate risk
  • A 1bp rise in yield reduces a 30-year bond's price ~15× more than a 1-year bond's price
  • Duration (interest-rate sensitivity) increases with maturity — longer bonds are riskier to hold
  • Investors are compensated for this risk via the term premium embedded in long yields

The Market Segmentation Theory takes a fundamentally different approach. Rather than assuming investors freely arbitrage across maturities, it argues that different investors have strong preferences for specific maturity segments — and these maturity-specific supply and demand forces, not rate expectations alone, determine yields in each segment.

The weaker version of this theory — Preferred Habitat Theory — acknowledges that investors have maturity preferences but can be induced to move to other segments if offered a sufficiently large premium. This is the most realistic and nuanced view: investors have habitats but are not completely captive.

Natural maturity "habitats" — who operates where
  • Short end (0–2 years) — money market funds, corporate treasurers, banks' liquidity portfolios; strong demand keeps short yields contained
  • Medium term (2–10 years) — most active segment; insurance companies, mutual funds, hedge funds; benchmark Treasuries/gilts in this range
  • Long end (10–30+ years) — pension funds and life insurers matching long-dated liabilities; strong structural demand for 20–30yr bonds keeps long yields lower than expectations theory would predict
  • Ultra-long (30–50 years) — specialist buyers only; UK DMO issues 50yr gilts; limited demand from anyone except liability-driven investors
Implications for the yield curve shape
  • The yield curve reflects not just rate expectations but also structural supply and demand at each maturity
  • Government debt issuance decisions (e.g., issuing more short-term bills vs long bonds) can directly shift the yield curve even without changing rate expectations
  • QE (Quantitative Easing) is the most powerful real-world example: by purchasing long-duration bonds, central banks reduce the supply available to private investors, pushing long yields down irrespective of rate expectations
  • The UK gilt market is strongly influenced by pension fund demand at the long end — regulatory LDI (liability-driven investment) mandates create large, inelastic demand for 30–50yr gilts
BoE Gilt Curve — spot vs forward (hump-shaped)
The BoE's estimated gilt curve shows a hump-shaped forward curve: forward rates rise from near-zero at short maturities, peak around 10–15 years, then decline. This pattern reflects:
  • Rising expected rates + term premium at medium maturities
  • Very strong pension fund demand at ultra-long maturities suppressing those yields and implied forward rates
  • Possibly negative term premia at the very long end as captive buyers accept lower compensation

Question — downward-sloping curve under LP theory
When the yield curve is downward-sloping, what can we say about future spot rates under the Liquidity Premium Theory?
Setup: Suppose y(1) = 4% and y(10) = 3% — the yield curve is inverted (short yields above long yields). Under LP theory, assume the 10-year term premium LP(10) = 1%.
Solution — step by step
STEP 1: Write the LP theory equation for the 10-year yield
y(10) ≈ (1/10) × Σ E[r_{t+k}] + LP(10)
STEP 2: Substitute known values
3% ≈ (1/10) × Σ E[r_{t+k}] + 1%
→ Average expected short rate ≈ 2%
STEP 3: Interpret
The market is pricing an average future short rate of ~2% — well below the current 1-year rate of 4%. Despite the positive term premium of 1%, long yields are still below short yields, which means expected future short rates must have fallen sharply to pull the 10-year yield down to 3%. Under LP theory, an inverted curve is an even stronger signal of expected rate cuts than it appears under expectations theory alone.
Key lesson — Exercise 1
Under liquidity premium theory, any observed long yield already contains a positive term premium. An inverted curve means long yields are low despite that upward premium — implying that expected future short rates must be falling significantly. The more realistic the term premium assumption, the more dramatically the market must be pricing expected rate cuts when the curve inverts. This is why an inverted curve under LP theory is an even stronger recession signal than under the pure expectations view.
Question — interpreting the BoE gilt curve
The Bank of England estimates a gilt spot (zero) curve and an instantaneous forward curve from market prices of UK government bonds. What can we read from these?
Gilt: A UK government bond (HM Treasury / Debt Management Office) that pays coupons and repays face value at maturity. Gilt market prices allow the BoE to infer a risk-free term structure.
What each curve shows
  • Spot curve r(T) (blue) — the yield on a zero-coupon gilt maturing at T. Rises from near 0% at short maturities to ~2.1% around 20–25 years, then gently declines to ~1.8% by 40 years. Upward-sloping in the medium term; slightly humped and declining at the very long end.
  • Instantaneous forward curve f(T) (orange) — the marginal rate implied for a borrowing/lending over an infinitesimally short period starting at horizon T. Peaks around 10–15 years before declining.
Interpretation — what the shape tells us
  • The hump-shaped forward curve peaking around 10–15 years suggests market-implied future short rates/term premia are highest in the medium term — not monotonically increasing with maturity
  • Declining forward rates beyond ~15 years (eventually falling below the spot curve) indicate lower implied marginal rates far in the future — consistent with lower long-run inflation/real rate expectations, or strong pension fund demand suppressing ultra-long yields
  • The forward curve lying above the spot curve at all points up to ~25 years confirms the upward-sloping yield curve: forward rates must exceed spot rates when the spot curve slopes up (a mathematical necessity)
  • The convergence of spot and forward curves at the very long end reflects strong structural demand from liability-driven investors (pension funds, insurers) compressing the term premium at ultra-long maturities
Preferred habitat theory best explains the BoE gilt curve — captive pension demand suppresses 30–50yr yields
Key lesson — Exercise 2
Real-world yield curves do not conform perfectly to any single theory. The BoE gilt curve shows the imprint of all three theories simultaneously: medium-term rates reflect rate expectations plus a term premium (LP theory), while ultra-long rates are depressed by structural pension fund demand for liability-matching assets (preferred habitat). Reading a yield curve requires holding all three theories in mind at once and judging which force is dominant at each maturity segment.

All bond formulas — exam reference
  • Coupon bond price = C×[1−(1+y)^(−T)]/y + F/(1+y)ᵀ — annuity of coupons + PV of face value
  • Zero-coupon bond price = F/(1+y)ᵀ — single cash flow; YTM equals spot rate
  • Consol price = C/y — fixed coupon forever; same as perpetuity PV formula
  • Coupon rate > y → Premium; Coupon rate = y → Par; Coupon rate < y → Discount
  • Spot rate r(T) = (F/P)^(1/T) − 1 — implied from ZCB price; the fundamental term structure measure
  • Coupon bond value = Σ C_t/(1+r_t)ᵗ + F/(1+r_T)ᵀ — discount each cash flow at its own spot rate
  • f_{T,T+1} = (1+r_{T+1})^{T+1}/(1+r_T)^T − 1 — one-year forward rate implied by spot curve
  • f_{T,T+N} = [(1+r_{T+N})^{T+N}/(1+r_T)^T]^(1/N) − 1 — N-year forward rate from T to T+N
  • EAR (semi-annual) = (1+y/2)² − 1 — convert semi-annual YTM to effective annual rate
  • LP theory: y(T) ≈ avg[E(short rates)] + LP_T — long yields = rate expectations + term premium
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • YTM ≠ spot rate for coupon bonds — only for zero-coupon bonds. YTM is a weighted average of the spot rates across payment dates
  • Forward rates ≠ future spot rates — forward rates include term premia; they systematically overpredict actual future rates on average
  • Semi-annual coupons: divide coupon AND rate by 2, double periods — both adjustments required; easy to apply one and forget the other
  • An inverted curve doesn't necessarily mean rates will fall exactly as implied — under LP theory, the implied expected rate fall is even sharper than the raw curve suggests
  • Higher yield ≠ better investment — a high yield often signals higher credit risk or distress; compare apples to apples (same credit quality)
  • Premium bonds do NOT outperform discount bonds — given the same YTM, all bonds with the same maturity and credit quality deliver identical total returns
  • A falling bond price doesn't mean a loss if you hold to maturity — the YTM return is locked in at purchase; price moves only matter if you sell before maturity
  • Coupon rate and yield are different things — coupon rate is fixed at issuance; yield moves with market prices every day
STEP 1 — Master the bond anatomy and pricing equation
Bond = annuity of coupons + PV of face value
Every bond pricing problem reduces to this. The annuity formula handles the coupons; the single discount factor handles the face value. Know how to apply both for annual and semi-annual payments. Always check: is the computed price above, below, or equal to par? It should match the coupon-rate-vs-yield comparison.
STEP 2 — Understand the price-yield inverse relationship deeply
Yields up → prices down. Always. No exceptions.
When market yields rise, all future cash flows are discounted more heavily, reducing present values. A bond paying fixed cash flows becomes less valuable when alternatives offer higher yields. This relationship is the foundation of all bond risk management. Duration measures the magnitude of this sensitivity.
STEP 3 — Distinguish spot rates, YTM, and forward rates
Three different interest rates — three different questions
Spot rate r(T): the pure market rate for a single cash flow at time T — derived from ZCBs. YTM: a single blended rate summarising a coupon bond's total return — a weighted average of spot rates. Forward rate f(T,T+N): the rate implied for a future lending period — derived from two spot rates via no-arbitrage. Never confuse the three.
STEP 4 — Apply the three yield curve theories to interpret the curve
No single theory is always right — use all three
In practice, the yield curve reflects rate expectations (Expectations Theory), risk premia (LP Theory), and structural supply/demand (Market Segmentation) simultaneously. An inverted curve is most powerful as a recession signal, but the strength of that signal depends on how large the term premium is. The BoE gilt curve shows all three forces operating at different maturities.
One-line synthesis — Bonds
A bond is a promise of future cash flows, and its price is simply the present value of those promises — discounted at whatever rate the market demands for that level of risk and maturity. Every bond concept flows from this: premium vs discount pricing, yield to maturity, spot rates, forward rates, and even the competing theories of the yield curve are all different ways of asking the same question — how does the market today value cash flows that arrive at different points in the future?
