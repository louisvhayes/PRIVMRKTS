The Four Deal Structures — Explained

Private equity firms use different legal and financial structures depending on who is selling, who is buying, and what the goal is. Understanding these structures is essential to making sense of Chiltern's portfolio. Here are the four Chiltern uses most frequently — explained from first principles.

Structure 01
Management Buy-Out (MBO)
An MBO is when the existing management team of a company buys the business from its current owner — typically using a combination of their own money and capital provided by a private equity firm. The managers become the new shareholders, and the PE firm (like Chiltern) provides the majority of the equity capital in exchange for an ownership stake.

Why does this happen? Often a founder wants to retire, a corporate wants to sell a division, or shareholders want to exit. The managers — who know the business best — believe they can run it better with PE backing and want the financial upside of ownership rather than just a salary.

Chiltern's role: Chiltern provides the £10–40m equity cheque that makes the management team's dream of ownership possible. In return, Chiltern owns a majority or significant minority stake and earns its return when the business is sold at a higher valuation in the future.
How it works — step by step
  1. Management team identifies they want to buy the business from the current owner
  2. They approach PE firms (like Chiltern) to provide the capital to fund the purchase
  3. Chiltern conducts due diligence and agrees terms: price, equity split, board seats
  4. A new holding company is created; Chiltern and management become shareholders
  5. Holding company acquires the trading business, often using a mix of equity + debt
  6. Management runs the business; Chiltern adds strategic value and M&A support
  7. After 3–7 years, business is sold at a higher price — both parties share the upside
Chiltern example → Train'd Up (2025)
Chiltern backed CEO Alan McGhee and the management team in a £TBC MBO of Train'd Up Railway Resourcing — the UK's largest rail apprenticeship provider — from its original founders David Gillespie and Paul McGlynn. ThinCats provided debt finance.
Structure 02
Buy-and-Build
A buy-and-build (also called a platform-plus-bolt-on or roll-up strategy) starts with a platform acquisition — a core company in a fragmented market — and then systematically acquires smaller competitors (bolt-ons) to consolidate them under one roof. The goal is to create a market leader significantly larger and more valuable than the sum of its parts.

Why does it work? Scale creates value in multiple ways: larger businesses trade at higher valuation multiples; combined businesses have more pricing power; operational efficiencies emerge from shared back-office and management; and market leadership positions attract higher-quality clients and employees.

Chiltern's role: Chiltern provides the initial platform acquisition capital, then funds each bolt-on acquisition — either from retained portfolio company cash flow or follow-on equity. Chiltern's director Alistair Gray leads M&A origination across the portfolio specifically for this purpose.
How it works — step by step
  1. Identify a fragmented market where no single player dominates (e.g. automotive repair)
  2. Acquire a "platform" — a strong, well-managed business with growth potential
  3. Work with management to identify smaller competitors or adjacent businesses to acquire
  4. Fund bolt-on acquisitions one by one — integrating each into the platform
  5. As size grows, negotiate better supplier terms, attract larger clients, share overheads
  6. Valuation multiple "re-rates" upward as the business becomes a market leader
  7. Exit to a larger PE fund or strategic buyer who pays a premium for the scale achieved
Chiltern example → Steer Automotive (2021–2024)
Chiltern backed Steer from a top-20 regional repairer (2021) through 19 acquisitions to become the UK's market leader with 100+ repair centres and 2,300 employees. Exited to Oakley Capital (2024) — a textbook buy-and-build success. Sunday Times top-100 fastest-growing businesses.
Structure 03
Carve-Out
A carve-out occurs when a large corporate or group sells off a subsidiary, division or business unit that is no longer central to its core strategy. The corporate "carves out" the division and sells it — often to a PE firm who backs the incumbent management team to run it independently.

Why do corporates carve-out? Strategy focus (they want to concentrate on their main business), capital recycling (the proceeds fund their core business), or conglomerate discount reduction (investors value focused businesses more highly than diversified ones).

Why it's attractive for PE: Carve-outs often represent hidden value. The division may have been starved of investment or attention within the larger corporate. Under new independent ownership, with management free to run the business for themselves (not a large corporate HQ), performance often improves quickly. The management team typically reinvests equity — creating strong alignment.
How it works — step by step
  1. A large corporate decides a division is non-core and initiates a sale process
  2. PE firm (Chiltern) and the incumbent management team partner to bid for the division
  3. Legal separation is complex — IT systems, contracts, HR, accounting must all be untangled
  4. A Transitional Services Agreement (TSA) keeps the corporate supporting the business during separation
  5. Management team receives equity stake — often the first time they have owned the business
  6. PE firm and management work to build the business as a standalone, independent entity
  7. Over 3–7 years, grow the business and exit at a profit
Chiltern example → Strata Products (2024)
Chiltern carved Strata Products out of FTSE-listed Berry Global Inc (NYSE: BERY), backing CEO Jay Ilsen and the management team. Won Insider Media Deal of the Month. Strata is now an independent UK plastic products manufacturer with new product development mandate.
Structure 04
Minority Growth Capital
A minority growth capital investment is when a PE firm invests in a company without taking a controlling stake — buying less than 50% of the shares. The existing owner (often a founder) retains majority control and day-to-day decision-making. The PE firm provides capital and support without directing the business.

Why do founders choose this? They want capital to accelerate growth (fund an acquisition, hire a sales team, expand internationally) but do not want to sell control of their business. A minority investor is a partner, not a buyer.

Why Chiltern offers this: Most PE firms require majority control. Chiltern's flexibility to take minority positions is a genuine differentiator — it opens up a wider market of founders who want capital but not the loss of control that a full buyout requires. Chiltern's returns come from holding an equity stake that grows in value as the business succeeds, then selling that stake when the founder eventually exits.
How it works — step by step
  1. Founder or management team wants to grow but doesn't need (or want) to sell the business
  2. Chiltern invests £10–40m for a minority equity stake (typically 20–49%)
  3. Founder remains majority shareholder and day-to-day decision-maker
  4. Chiltern takes a board seat and provides strategic input, M&A support, and network access
  5. Capital is deployed to fund growth: acquisitions, headcount, technology, international expansion
  6. Both parties benefit as the business grows and the equity value increases
  7. Exit when the founder is ready — sale to PE, trade buyer, or IPO — Chiltern exits its stake
Chiltern example → Burgess Farms (2025)
Chiltern invested in Burgess Farms (October 2025) as a growth capital investment, backing the existing team to accelerate expansion of the UK agriculture and distribution business. Chiltern's most recent investment at the time of publication.
Chiltern's structural flexibility
"We are flexible partners and will consider a range of investment situations including owners that want to sell their business in full or just a minority shareholding; management teams who wish to buy their business through an MBO; management teams who want to raise capital to accelerate growth, either organically, through a roll-out or via acquisition; and entrepreneurs that want to de-risk and crystallise value but still have the ambition to achieve more." — Chiltern Capital, LinkedIn
