EQT’s sell-down in Galderma shows how private equity exits often continue long after the IPO itself. The deal was not a fresh capital raise for the company. What happened

EQT and other major shareholders — including ADIA and Auba Investment — sold 34 million existing shares in Galderma, representing around 14.3% of the company. The transaction raised roughly CHF 4.89bn and was executed as an overnight placement to institutional investors.

The crucial detail is that this was a secondary sale. Galderma itself did not issue new shares and did not receive the proceeds. Instead, the money went to the selling shareholders, who were reducing their holdings after the company’s strong share price performance since listing in March 2024.

The Galderma trade is a reminder that an IPO is often not the end of a private equity exit — it is the beginning of a staged sell-down process.

What is a secondary block trade?

A secondary block trade is a large sale of already-existing shares by current shareholders in a listed company. Because the shares already exist, the transaction is called secondary. Because the amount sold is large and placed at once, it is called a block trade.

This differs from a primary share sale, where the company issues new shares and receives fresh capital. In Galderma’s case, the company itself did not raise cash. EQT and the other shareholders monetised part of their position.

These transactions are usually executed via an accelerated bookbuild: investment banks market a large block of shares to institutional investors over a very short period, often overnight, and place the stock at a modest discount to the last traded price.

That is why the Galderma deal can be both a public markets transaction and a private equity exit event at the same time.
Secondary

Existing shareholders sell their own shares. The company does not receive the proceeds.

Block trade

A large amount of stock is sold in one transaction rather than dripped into the market over time.

Accelerated bookbuild

Banks gather orders quickly — often overnight — to place the shares with institutional investors.

Why Galderma has been such a successful PE-backed IPO

Galderma has become one of Europe’s most successful recent private equity-backed IPOs because the company sits at the intersection of several attractive themes: high-growth healthcare exposure, strong brands, recurring product demand, and an aesthetic medicine market that still offers long runway for international expansion.

Industry tailwinds

Aesthetic medicine and dermatology have benefited from rising consumer demand, ageing populations, and continued interest in non-surgical treatments.

Product portfolio

Galderma owns high-quality brands and treatment franchises that give investors confidence in durability, pricing power, and recurring revenue.

Execution

By the time it listed, the business had a growth profile and strategic positioning that public investors were willing to pay for.

Private equity value creation also matters here. EQT did not simply take the company public and hope for a rerating. The investment thesis depended on positioning Galderma as a stronger global dermatology and aesthetics platform — one that public market investors would view as both a growth company and a defensible healthcare name.

Why this matters for private equity

Galderma is a good example of how private equity exits increasingly blend public and private markets. The “exit” is not always one event. It can be a sequence: operational improvement under private ownership, an IPO to open liquidity and establish a market valuation, and then one or more secondary block trades to monetise the remaining stake.

That is important because it shows how sponsors can maximise value over time rather than selling everything at once. When the listed company performs well, public markets effectively become an additional exit channel that can be tapped repeatedly.

Stage 1

Private equity ownership improves the business and prepares it for public scrutiny.

Stage 2

The IPO creates liquidity, visibility, and a reference market valuation.

Stage 3

Secondary sell-downs allow investors to monetise at scale while preserving orderly market trading.
