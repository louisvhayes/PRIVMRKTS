The Six Converging Forces

No single cause explains the explosion of continuation funds. Instead, six structural forces collided simultaneously — each one individually might have produced a modest uptick; together, they generated a self-reinforcing cycle that transformed continuation vehicles from an emergency tool into the dominant PE exit mechanism of the 2020s.

Longer Value Arcs

The most visible trigger for the continuation fund boom has been the near-total seizure of traditional PE exit channels. IPO markets — the historic prestige exit for PE sponsors — have remained deeply depressed since their 2021 peak. Volatile public markets, rising interest rates that compress growth-stock valuations, and regulatory complexity have all contributed to a profound reluctance among sponsors to list portfolio companies at prices they consider inadequate.

Simultaneously, strategic M&A — the other primary route — hit a decade low in 2023 and has only partially recovered. Antitrust scrutiny, financing costs, and a wide bid-ask gap between seller expectations (still anchored to ZIRP-era multiples) and buyer willingness have paralysed deal activity. The result: GPs holding high-quality assets at fund maturity with no viable exit at an acceptable price.

Continuation funds allow managers to retain high-conviction assets and provide investors with liquidity options in an environment where the backdrop of low financing costs that encouraged record transaction volumes has evaporated.

Why GPs won't sell at any price
  • Assets acquired at 11–13x EBITDA during ZIRP era — forced sale now would crystalise a loss relative to GP's own valuation marks
  • Selling at distressed prices locks in a poor IRR that damages GP's fundraising track record for successor funds
  • Portfolio company fundamentals often still strong — GP conviction that more value remains to be captured
  • LP pressure to maximise DPI, but not at any price — LPs also don't want write-downs on their books

Private equity fund lifecycles are fixed — typically 10 years, with possible extensions. The deal-making frenzy of 2014–2018 produced an enormous cohort of funds that are now approaching or past their natural wind-down dates. More than 50% of PE funds globally are now 6+ years old, and Pitchbook estimates that 1,607 funds are scheduled to dissolve in 2025 or 2026 alone.

This creates a structural imperative: either sell assets, or find a vehicle to hold them. In a closed exit environment, continuation funds are the only credible mechanism that allows GPs to avoid forced sales. The fund maturity wall is not a temporary phenomenon — it reflects the accumulated legacy of years of above-average fundraising that must eventually unwind.

The average buyout holding period reached 6.7 years in 2024, up from a two-decade average of 5.7 years — meaning the typical PE-backed company now outlives the original expectation for its fund by more than a year. This single statistic encapsulates the structural mismatch between fund design and modern business reality.

Why businesses outlive their funds
  • Buy-and-build strategies require multiple sequential acquisitions — often taking 6–8 years to fully assemble the platform
  • Digital transformation and operational improvement programmes now embedded in value creation — these take time
  • Companies staying private longer overall — average time-to-IPO for VC-backed companies doubled in 10 years
  • GPs underestimated time needed at entry; exit conditions at fund maturity often worse than expected

LP distributions from private equity fell to just 11% of NAV in 2024 — the lowest level in more than a decade, according to Bain & Company. Pension funds, endowments, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds that committed capital to PE in 2014–2019 are sitting on large, unrealised positions with no distributions arriving to fund their own obligations.

This has created an acute "DPI drought" (Distributed to Paid-In capital). Institutions that depend on PE distributions to fund pension payments or re-deploy into new vintages are experiencing real cash flow strain. Continuation funds address this directly by offering existing LPs a clean binary choice: take liquidity now at current NAV, or roll over for further upside — without being forced to sell on the secondary market at a discount.

The mantra "DPI is the new IRR" became a genuine industry catchphrase in 2023–2024, reflecting the shift in LP priorities from theoretical return multiples towards actual cash-in-hand. CVs became the primary mechanism through which GPs could satisfy this demand without compromising on valuation.

One of the most underappreciated drivers of continuation fund growth is the powerful economic incentive for GPs themselves. When a GP structures a continuation fund, they are not acting purely on behalf of LPs — they are also engineering a material benefit to their own firm.

The economics reset is threefold. First, management fees are re-established on the continuation vehicle's committed capital, extending the income stream for the GP firm. Second, carried interest is reset — a new carry clock starts, meaning the GP earns a fresh 20% of future appreciation from the entry price in the new fund, even on value they themselves created in the legacy fund. Third, GPs frequently increase their own equity co-investment in the assets at the valuation set for the transaction — at the same price new institutional buyers are entering.

This creates an inherent conflict of interest: the GP is simultaneously advising the legacy fund (the seller) and managing the continuation fund (the buyer). Governance safeguards like LPAC approval and fairness opinions exist to mitigate this — but the financial incentive to structure a continuation vehicle is always present, regardless of whether it is in LPs' interests.

GP economic benefits from a CV
  • Management fee reset — fees re-established on new capital base, often 1.5–2% p.a. on a multi-hundred-million fund — multi-year income stream
  • Carry reset — 20% of all future appreciation from CV entry price accrues to GP, even on value already created
  • GP co-invest — opportunity to increase personal stake in best assets at same price as institutional buyers
  • Track record management — avoids selling at depressed prices that would impair IRR and damage fundraising story for next fund
  • Fundraising signal — CVs signal conviction in the asset and the GP's own thesis, aiding the next flagship fund raise
Conflict of interest: the fundamental tension
The GP acts as fiduciary for both the legacy fund (seller) and the continuation vehicle (buyer) simultaneously. Every CV decision — the transfer price, which assets to include, new fund terms — involves a party the GP controls on both sides. This is structurally unresolvable; governance can only mitigate it.

Continuation funds cannot exist without buyers — and until recently, the secondary market for GP-led deals was too thin, too fragmented, and too sceptical to support the volume now being transacted. The dramatic maturation of the dedicated secondary fund universe has been the essential enabling infrastructure for the CV boom.

Total secondary market volume reached $240 billion in 2025 — up 48% year-on-year and more than triple the 2019 level. Dedicated secondary fund managers raised $97.5 billion in the trailing 12 months to Q3 2024 alone — a 31% year-on-year increase. Available dry powder earmarked specifically for secondaries stood at $302 billion by mid-2025. This capital needs to be deployed, and GP-led continuation vehicles — with their visible assets, knowable GPs, and structured processes — represent an ideal deployment channel.

The buyer universe has also diversified. Historically dominated by a handful of specialist firms (Ardian, HarbourVest, Lexington, Coller), the space now includes dedicated GP-led desks at major banks, insurance company allocators, sovereign wealth funds, and increasingly, retail and HNW investor vehicles — broadening the capital pool further.

Beyond the market dislocations, there is a deeper structural reason continuation funds have proliferated: the businesses PE backs have simply outgrown the original fund model. The traditional 10-year closed-end fund structure was designed in the 1980s for an era of simpler, primarily financial engineering — buy, lever, cut costs, sell in five years.

Modern private equity value creation looks fundamentally different. Buy-and-build platform strategies require years of sequential acquisitions to assemble and integrate. Digital transformation, internationalisation, and ESG repositioning programmes have multi-year horizons. Companies that are genuinely performing often hit inflection points — a product launch, a market expansion, an M&A opportunity — at exactly the point their fund would naturally need to exit.

This is the legitimate case for continuation funds: not emergency liquidity management, but rational extension of a value creation thesis that requires more time than the original fund lifecycle allows. The best GPs argue persuasively that forcing a sale at year 10 simply because a fund document says so is contrary to LP interests when the asset is at an inflection point.

Businesses that benefit most from CVs
  • Buy-and-build platforms — 6–8 years needed to assemble, integrate, and realise full synergies from a fragmented sector roll-up
  • Inflection-point assets — company about to launch a new product line, enter a new geography, or complete a transformative acquisition
  • Infrastructure assets — index-linked revenues, 20–30 year concessions; 10-year fund life is structurally too short
  • Late-stage VC / growth equity — strong fundamentals but IPO market closed; VC funds need extended runway for companies near but not yet at listing readiness
  • Regulated industries — financial services, healthcare, utilities — where transformation timelines are inherently longer due to regulatory complexity

The Macro Context: How Rates Created the Crisis

The continuation fund boom cannot be understood without appreciating the most aggressive monetary tightening cycle in four decades. From near-zero rates in early 2022, the Federal Reserve raised rates to 5.5% by mid-2023 — a 525 basis point increase in 18 months. The ECB moved from negative rates to 4.5%; the Bank of England from 0.1% to 5.25%.

This shock hit private equity from multiple directions simultaneously. Leveraged buyout economics broke down: debt that cost 4% in 2021 cost 9–10% by 2023, collapsing the financial engineering that drove buyout returns. Equity valuations compressed: the public market re-rating of growth assets (particularly SaaS, which fell from 20x+ to sub-5x revenue) meant portfolio company marks fell sharply. And M&A dried up: the wide bid-ask gap between sellers (anchored to ZIRP valuations) and buyers (repricing in a new rate environment) paralysed strategic deal-making.

The result was a vast accumulation of PE-held assets that could not be sold at prices GPs were willing to accept. Continuation funds filled the vacuum — allowing GPs to provide LP liquidity at current marks, without crystallising the valuation reset that an open-market sale would have forced.

Structural Shifts Enabling the Boom

Beyond the immediate macro triggers, three long-run structural evolutions in private markets have made continuation funds permanently viable at scale — irrespective of the interest rate or exit environment.

1. The reputational pivot
Until ~2015, GP-led restructurings carried a stigma of distress — "zombie funds" used to hide problem assets. The shift to structuring CVs around high-conviction "trophy assets" fundamentally changed perception. Today, a continuation fund signals GP conviction, not weakness. This reputational rehabilitation was essential to attracting top-tier institutional buyers.
2024: 90% of CV volume involves performing / high-conviction assets
2. Asset class expansion
CVs began in buyout PE but have spread rapidly to infrastructure (84% of LP survey respondents saw more infra CVs in 2025), venture capital (Lightspeed, Insight Partners, RockPort 2024), and private credit (managers extending duration on performing portfolios). This diversification dramatically widens the addressable market.
Infrastructure CV LPs seeing increase: 84% · Venture CVs: emerging rapidly
3. Retail / evergreen capital
The emergence of semi-liquid, evergreen, and interval fund structures targeting retail and HNW investors has created a vast new pool of potential secondary buyers. These vehicles need to deploy continuously and cannot wait years for primary fund cycles — GP-led CVs with known assets and shorter J-curves are an ideal fit. Secondary market "overheating" in LP-led deals has partly been attributed to this influx.

Regulatory & Governance Landscape

The regulatory backdrop for continuation funds has shifted materially since 2022 — significantly reducing mandatory protections for LPs at precisely the moment the market is scaling fastest. Understanding this evolution is essential context for any LP or GP operating in the space.

2022
Background
SEC proposes Private Fund Advisers Rule
The SEC proposed sweeping new rules requiring registered investment advisers to obtain fairness opinions or independent valuations for continuation fund transactions, and to provide full disclosure to all LPs before completing a GP-led secondary. The intent was to formalise protections against the inherent conflict of interest in CV transactions.
2023
Rule Finalised
SEC finalises rule — fairness opinions mandated
The final rule required advisers conducting GP-led secondaries to obtain an independent fairness opinion on the transaction price and to distribute a written summary of material business relationships. Industry pushed back hard, arguing the requirements were overly prescriptive and increased transaction costs without commensurate LP benefit.
June 2024
Major Change
5th Circuit Court of Appeals vacates key provisions
In a landmark ruling, the US Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals vacated the SEC's Private Fund Advisers Rule in its entirety, holding the SEC had exceeded its statutory authority. This removed the mandatory fairness opinion and disclosure requirements for GP-led secondaries — just as the market was hitting new volume records. The ruling dramatically increased GP flexibility but materially reduced mandatory LP protections.
2024–2025
Industry Response
ILPA guidance & voluntary best practices emerge
The Institutional Limited Partners Association (ILPA) published updated guidance on GP-led secondary transactions, recommending LPs require fairness opinions, competitive processes, and enhanced disclosure on a contractual rather than regulatory basis. Leading GPs continued obtaining fairness opinions voluntarily, recognising they are essential to deal credibility even without regulatory compulsion.
2025–2026
Ongoing
Regulatory uncertainty remains — LP scrutiny intensifying
Despite the rule vacatur, LP scrutiny of CV terms has intensified rather than relaxed. Sophisticated LPs increasingly negotiate enhanced governance rights — LPAC independence requirements, no-fault removal rights, mandatory competitive processes — as contractual conditions of rolling over into continuation vehicles. The market is self-regulating in the absence of formal rules.
Key governance risks post-vacatur
  • No mandatory fairness opinion requirement — GP can set transfer price with only voluntary disclosure
  • Information asymmetry between GP (full asset knowledge) and selling LPs (reliant on GP-provided data)
  • Small LPs lack resources to mount opposition to transactions backed by large LP majorities
  • LPAC "capture" risk — committee composition may favour GP-friendly incumbents
  • GPeconomic reset may not be disclosed with full clarity to rolling LPs
Best-practice governance safeguards
  • Third-party independent fairness opinion — voluntary but strongly recommended even without mandate
  • Competitive auction process to validate transfer price via market discovery, not GP-set NAV alone
  • LPAC with genuinely independent members not dominated by GP-friendly large LPs
  • No-fault removal rights for GPs in the continuation vehicle — stronger than legacy fund terms
  • Full disclosure of GP economics reset (fees, carry, co-invest) in writing to all LPs pre-close

Structural tailwinds — why CV growth is durable
  • $3.6tn exit overhang ensures a persistent pipeline of CV candidates for years to come — the backlog is not clearing quickly
  • Infrastructure, private credit, and VC adoption are widening the addressable market well beyond traditional buyout PE
  • Growing retail / HNW capital pools dedicated to secondaries expand the buyer universe dramatically
  • Rate cutting cycle improves LBO financing conditions but exit market normalisation will take years
  • LP sophistication increasing — institutional LPs building dedicated GP-led evaluation teams, reducing friction
  • 2021–2025 vintage CV track records beginning to emerge — if returns are strong, further legitimisation follows
Structural risks — what could slow the market
  • Market saturation risk: too many mediocre assets labelled "trophy" assets — LP fatigue with overpriced CVs will emerge if returns disappoint
  • Regulatory re-intervention: Congress or a future SEC administration could reimpose mandatory disclosure rules more stringently than the vacated 2022 rule
  • Normalising exit markets: a genuine IPO and M&A recovery would reduce the supply of forced CV candidates, tightening deal flow for secondary buyers
  • LP power shift: LPs are becoming more sophisticated and less willing to accept GP-set terms — increasing negotiation friction and deal timelines
  • Performance track record risk: the 2021–2025 CV cohort has yet to fully realise — poor exits would damage the asset class' reputation materially
Editorial view — Private Markets Intelligence
Continuation funds have passed the all-weather test. They grew through the ZIRP boom, accelerated through the rate-shock bust, and are maintaining momentum through the easing recovery. The structural forces that created them — a maturing PE industry, longer value creation arcs, a deeper secondary market, and LP demand for liquidity — are not temporary. The question is no longer whether continuation funds belong in private equity. It is whether the governance frameworks being constructed — voluntarily, contractually, in the absence of regulatory compulsion — can keep pace with the speed and scale of a market now approaching $120 billion annually.
