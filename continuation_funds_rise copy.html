LONGER VALUE ARCS

The most visible trigger for the continuation fund boom has been the near-total seizure of traditional PE exit channels. IPO markets — the historic prestige exit for PE sponsors — have remained deeply depressed since their 2021 peak. Volatile public markets, rising interest rates that compress growth-stock valuations, and regulatory complexity have all contributed to a profound reluctance among sponsors to list portfolio companies at prices they consider inadequate.

Simultaneously, strategic M&A — the other primary route — hit a decade low in 2023 and has only partially recovered. Antitrust scrutiny, financing costs, and a wide bid-ask gap between seller expectations (still anchored to ZIRP-era multiples) and buyer willingness have paralysed deal activity. The result: GPs holding high-quality assets at fund maturity with no viable exit at an acceptable price.

Continuation funds allow managers to retain high-conviction assets and provide investors with liquidity options in an environment where the backdrop of low financing costs that encouraged record transaction volumes has evaporated.

03

The Macro Context: How Rates Created the Crisis

The continuation fund boom cannot be understood without appreciating the most aggressive monetary tightening cycle in four decades. From near-zero rates in early 2022, the Federal Reserve raised rates to 5.5% by mid-2023 — a 525 basis point increase in 18 months. The ECB moved from negative rates to 4.5%; the Bank of England from 0.1% to 5.25%.

This shock hit private equity from multiple directions simultaneously. Leveraged buyout economics broke down: debt that cost 4% in 2021 cost 9–10% by 2023, collapsing the financial engineering that drove buyout returns. Equity valuations compressed: the public market re-rating of growth assets (particularly SaaS, which fell from 20x+ to sub-5x revenue) meant portfolio company marks fell sharply. And M&A dried up: the wide bid-ask gap between sellers (anchored to ZIRP valuations) and buyers (repricing in a new rate environment) paralysed strategic deal-making.

The result was a vast accumulation of PE-held assets that could not be sold at prices GPs were willing to accept. Continuation funds filled the vacuum — allowing GPs to provide LP liquidity at current marks, without crystallising the valuation reset that an open-market sale would have forced.

04

Structural Shifts Enabling the Boom

Beyond the immediate macro triggers, three long-run structural evolutions in private markets have made continuation funds permanently viable at scale — irrespective of the interest rate or exit environment.

1. THE REPUTATIONAL PIVOT

Until ~2015, GP-led restructurings carried a stigma of distress — "zombie funds" used to hide problem assets. The shift to structuring CVs around high-conviction "trophy assets" fundamentally changed perception. Today, a continuation fund signals GP conviction, not weakness. This reputational rehabilitation was essential to attracting top-tier institutional buyers.

2024: 90% of CV volume involves performing / high-conviction assets

2. ASSET CLASS EXPANSION

CVs began in buyout PE but have spread rapidly to infrastructure (84% of LP survey respondents saw more infra CVs in 2025), venture capital (Lightspeed, Insight Partners, RockPort 2024), and private credit (managers extending duration on performing portfolios). This diversification dramatically widens the addressable market.

Infrastructure CV LPs seeing increase: 84% · Venture CVs: emerging rapidly

3. RETAIL / EVERGREEN CAPITAL

The emergence of semi-liquid, evergreen, and interval fund structures targeting retail and HNW investors has created a vast new pool of potential secondary buyers. These vehicles need to deploy continuously and cannot wait years for primary fund cycles — GP-led CVs with known assets and shorter J-curves are an ideal fit. Secondary market "overheating" in LP-led deals has partly been attributed to this influx.

05

Regulatory & Governance Landscape

The regulatory backdrop for continuation funds has shifted materially since 2022 — significantly reducing mandatory protections for LPs at precisely the moment the market is scaling fastest. Understanding this evolution is essential context for any LP or GP operating in the space.

2022

BACKGROUND

SEC proposes Private Fund Advisers Rule

The SEC proposed sweeping new rules requiring registered investment advisers to obtain fairness opinions or independent valuations for continuation fund transactions, and to provide full disclosure to all LPs before completing a GP-led secondary. The intent was to formalise protections against the inherent conflict of interest in CV transactions.

2023

RULE FINALISED

SEC finalises rule — fairness opinions mandated

The final rule required advisers conducting GP-led secondaries to obtain an independent fairness opinion on the transaction price and to distribute a written summary of material business relationships. Industry pushed back hard, arguing the requirements were overly prescriptive and increased transaction costs without commensurate LP benefit.

June 2024

MAJOR CHANGE

5th Circuit Court of Appeals vacates key provisions

In a landmark ruling, the US Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals vacated the SEC's Private Fund Advisers Rule in its entirety, holding the SEC had exceeded its statutory authority. This removed the mandatory fairness opinion and disclosure requirements for GP-led secondaries — just as the market was hitting new volume records. The ruling dramatically increased GP flexibility but materially reduced mandatory LP protections.

2024–2025

INDUSTRY RESPONSE

ILPA guidance & voluntary best practices emerge

The Institutional Limited Partners Association (ILPA) published updated guidance on GP-led secondary transactions, recommending LPs require fairness opinions, competitive processes, and enhanced disclosure on a contractual rather than regulatory basis. Leading GPs continued obtaining fairness opinions voluntarily, recognising they are essential to deal credibility even without regulatory compulsion.

2025–2026

ONGOING

Regulatory uncertainty remains — LP scrutiny intensifying

Despite the rule vacatur, LP scrutiny of CV terms has intensified rather than relaxed. Sophisticated LPs increasingly negotiate enhanced governance rights — LPAC independence requirements, no-fault removal rights, mandatory competitive processes — as contractual conditions of rolling over into continuation vehicles. The market is self-regulating in the absence of formal rules.

KEY GOVERNANCE RISKS POST-VACATUR

  • No mandatory fairness opinion requirement — GP can set transfer price with only voluntary disclosure Information asymmetry between GP (full asset knowledge) and selling LPs (reliant on GP-provided data) Small LPs lack resources to mount opposition to transactions backed by large LP majorities LPAC "capture" risk — committee composition may favour GP-friendly incumbents GPeconomic reset may not be disclosed with full clarity to rolling LPs

BEST-PRACTICE GOVERNANCE SAFEGUARDS

  • Third-party independent fairness opinion — voluntary but strongly recommended even without mandate Competitive auction process to validate transfer price via market discovery, not GP-set NAV alone LPAC with genuinely independent members not dominated by GP-friendly large LPs No-fault removal rights for GPs in the continuation vehicle — stronger than legacy fund terms Full disclosure of GP economics reset (fees, carry, co-invest) in writing to all LPs pre-close

STRUCTURAL TAILWINDS — WHY CV GROWTH IS DURABLE

  • $3.6tn exit overhang ensures a persistent pipeline of CV candidates for years to come — the backlog is not clearing quickly Infrastructure, private credit, and VC adoption are widening the addressable market well beyond traditional buyout PE Growing retail / HNW capital pools dedicated to secondaries expand the buyer universe dramatically Rate cutting cycle improves LBO financing conditions but exit market normalisation will take years LP sophistication increasing — institutional LPs building dedicated GP-led evaluation teams, reducing friction 2021–2025 vintage CV track records beginning to emerge — if returns are strong, further legitimisation follows

STRUCTURAL RISKS — WHAT COULD SLOW THE MARKET

  • Market saturation risk: too many mediocre assets labelled "trophy" assets — LP fatigue with overpriced CVs will emerge if returns disappoint Regulatory re-intervention: Congress or a future SEC administration could reimpose mandatory disclosure rules more stringently than the vacated 2022 rule Normalising exit markets: a genuine IPO and M&A recovery would reduce the supply of forced CV candidates, tightening deal flow for secondary buyers LP power shift: LPs are becoming more sophisticated and less willing to accept GP-set terms — increasing negotiation friction and deal timelines Performance track record risk: the 2021–2025 CV cohort has yet to fully realise — poor exits would damage the asset class' reputation materially

EDITORIAL VIEW — PRIVATE MARKETS INTELLIGENCE

Continuation funds have passed the all-weather test. They grew through the ZIRP boom, accelerated through the rate-shock bust, and are maintaining momentum through the easing recovery. The structural forces that created them — a maturing PE industry, longer value creation arcs, a deeper secondary market, and LP demand for liquidity — are not temporary. The question is no longer whether continuation funds belong in private equity. It is whether the governance frameworks being constructed — voluntarily, contractually, in the absence of regulatory compulsion — can keep pace with the speed and scale of a market now approaching $120 billion annually.
