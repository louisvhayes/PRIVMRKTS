The Four Core Decisions of Corporate Finance

Corporate finance is not a single question — it is four interlocking questions that together define how a firm creates, sustains, and distributes value. Every financial decision a manager makes flows from one of these four pillars. Together they describe the entire lifecycle of capital inside a firm, from acquisition to deployment to return.

The investment decision asks: what long-term investments in fixed assets should the firm undertake to maximise value? This is the most fundamental question a firm faces. It concerns the left side of the balance sheet — specifically, which assets to acquire and hold. The nature of the business typically dictates the types and proportions of assets needed, but the firm must rigorously evaluate each investment opportunity.

The formal process for making these decisions is called capital budgeting — the planning, evaluation, and control of a firm's long-term investments. It involves identifying investment opportunities, estimating future cash flows, assessing risks, and applying decision-making techniques to determine whether an investment adds value.

Capital budgeting is about deciding which long-term investments are worth the money and managing them over their life to ensure they continue to create value for the firm.
— Corporate Finance Foundations

Capital budgeting decisions are uniquely consequential because they involve large sums of capital, they are often irreversible once made, and they have a lasting impact on long-term profitability. A wrong investment decision cannot easily be undone, which is why rigorous evaluation techniques are indispensable.

What capital budgeting involves
  • Identify opportunities — new projects, expansions, replacements, acquisitions, new markets, or technology upgrades
  • Estimate cash flows — project future revenues, costs, and net cash flows for the life of each investment
  • Assess risk — sensitivity analysis, scenario analysis, and discount rate adjustments to account for uncertainty
  • Apply decision criteria — Net Present Value (NPV), Internal Rate of Return (IRR), Payback Period
  • Accept or reject — invest only in projects that generate positive NPV or exceed the hurdle rate
  • Monitor expenditures — post-investment review to ensure the project delivers the expected return

The financing decision asks: how should the firm's long-term investments be funded? This is the right side of the balance sheet — the mix of debt and equity used to finance the firm's assets. The answer determines the firm's capital structure: the proportions of funding from current liabilities, long-term debt, and equity.

Think of the firm as a pie. The total size of the pie is the value of the firm. The financing decision is not about making the pie bigger — that is the investment decision. It is about how to slice the pie between debt holders and equity holders. The critical insight from modern corporate finance theory is that the optimal capital structure minimises the firm's overall cost of capital while managing financial risk.

The key tension is between debt financing (cheaper due to interest tax shields, but creates fixed obligations and financial risk) and equity financing (more flexible, no repayment pressure, but dilutes ownership and is typically more expensive than debt). The firm must balance these trade-offs carefully in light of its cash flow stability, asset type, and growth prospects.

Debt financing — characteristics
  • Loan or debt security — firm promises to repay principal plus contractual interest
  • Interest is tax-deductible — creates a valuable interest tax shield
  • Debt holders are senior — paid before equity holders in liquidation
  • Fixed obligations increase financial risk and probability of distress
  • Does not dilute existing shareholders' ownership stakes
Equity financing — characteristics
  • Firm sells shares and gives up a fraction of ownership to investors
  • Shareholders receive residual cash flows — only paid after all obligations are met
  • No fixed repayment — equity is permanent capital with no maturity date
  • More expensive than debt — shareholders bear more risk and demand a higher return
  • Dilutes ownership and may dilute earnings per share (EPS)

The management of cash flows decision focuses on the upper portion of the balance sheet — specifically, how the firm manages the gap between cash inflows and cash outflows in its day-to-day operations. There is almost always a timing mismatch between when cash comes in (customers paying) and when cash must go out (paying suppliers, wages, rent, utilities).

The formal measure of this challenge is Net Working Capital (NWC), defined as current assets minus current liabilities. If NWC is positive, the firm has a buffer of liquid assets over near-term obligations. If NWC is zero, the firm is exactly balanced. If NWC is negative, the firm is relying on future cash inflows to cover current obligations — a sign of inefficient cash management or structural fragility.

Financial managers must actively plan, monitor, and control these short-term flows to ensure the firm can always meet its obligations. Failure to manage working capital is a common cause of corporate failure even in otherwise profitable businesses.

Net Working Capital — what it tells you
Formula
NWC = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
NWC = 0Fine, but improve
NWC > 0Healthy buffer
NWC < 0Inefficient / risky
Sources of cash timing mismatch
  • Customers pay on credit terms (30–90 days) while suppliers must be paid sooner
  • Inventory sits unsold — tying up cash that could be deployed elsewhere
  • Seasonal revenue spikes create temporary gaps that must be bridged with working capital facilities
  • Rapid growth requires more working capital even as profitability improves

The payout decision asks: how should the firm return cash to investors? This concerns the lower right side of the balance sheet — specifically the equity claims. The firm's payout policy determines what fraction of earnings is retained within the business for future investment versus distributed to shareholders as a reward for their capital.

There are two main payout mechanisms. Cash dividends are direct periodic payments to shareholders, signalling financial stability and rewarding income-seeking investors. Share repurchases (buybacks) are when the firm uses cash to buy back its own shares from the open market, reducing shares outstanding, increasing earnings per share, and returning cash in a more tax-efficient way in most jurisdictions (capital gains tax versus income tax on dividends).

Repurchased shares are typically cancelled or held as treasury stock. Buybacks also signal management's confidence that the stock is undervalued. The payout decision is ultimately a balance between rewarding investors now versus retaining capital to fund future growth.

Factors shaping the payout decision
  • Cash availability — the firm must have sufficient liquidity before committing to payouts
  • Future investment opportunities — high-growth firms retain more; mature firms pay more
  • Shareholder expectations — income investors expect consistent dividends; growth investors prefer buybacks
  • Tax implications — buybacks are often more tax-efficient than dividends for investors
  • Earnings stability — firms with volatile earnings are cautious about committing to regular dividends
  • Debt obligations — paying bondholders faster (deleveraging) may take priority over equity payouts
Share buyback — how it works
The firm uses cash to buy shares from the open market or directly from shareholders → shares outstanding fall → earnings per share (EPS) rises → remaining shareholders own a larger fraction of the firm → cash returned in a tax-efficient manner → repurchased shares cancelled or held as treasury stock.
Signal effect: buybacks communicate management's belief the stock is undervalued

The Balance Sheet & Accounting Equation

The balance sheet is the foundational document of financial analysis. It shows the financial position of a business at a single point in time — a snapshot of what the firm owns, what it owes, and what belongs to shareholders. Every corporate finance concept — from capital structure to working capital to leverage — is ultimately rooted in the balance sheet.

The Fundamental Accounting Equation
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
This equation always balances because every asset is financed either by borrowing (liabilities) or by owners' funds (equity). What the company owns = what it owes + what belongs to owners.
Assets — ordered by liquidity (most → least)
CURRENT ASSETS (convert to cash within 1 year)
  • Cash & equivalents — already cash; the most liquid asset of all
  • Accounts receivable — money owed by customers for credit sales; converts soon
  • Inventories — raw materials, work-in-progress, finished goods; must be sold first
  • Other current assets — prepaid expenses, short-term deposits
LONG-TERM ASSETS (used >1 year)
  • Land — not depreciated because it does not wear out or become obsolete
  • Buildings & equipment — depreciated over their useful life (systematic cost allocation)
  • Net PPE — gross property, plant & equipment minus accumulated depreciation
  • Goodwill & intangibles — brand value, patents, trademarks, acquisition goodwill
Liabilities — ordered by maturity (earliest → latest)
CURRENT LIABILITIES (due within 1 year)
  • Accounts payable — money owed to suppliers for goods/services purchased on credit
  • Notes payable / short-term debt — loans or borrowings due within one year
  • Current maturities of LT debt — the portion of long-term loans due this year
  • Other current liabilities — accrued expenses, taxes payable
LONG-TERM LIABILITIES (due after 1 year)
  • Long-term debt — loans and bonds with repayment periods beyond one year
  • Capital lease obligations — long-term lease commitments treated as debt
  • Deferred taxes — taxes owed in future due to timing differences in accounting vs tax rules
Shareholders' equity
The residual claim on the firm after all liabilities are satisfied. Includes share capital (what shareholders paid in) and retained earnings (cumulative profits not distributed as dividends). Equity grows when the firm earns net income and shrinks when it incurs losses or pays dividends.
Ending Equity = Beginning Equity + Net Income − Dividends
Liquidity ordering of assets tells you how quickly the firm can generate cash. Maturity ordering of liabilities tells you when the firm needs cash. Together, these orderings reveal the firm's liquidity risk at a glance.
— Balance Sheet Fundamentals

How Firms Create Value: The Cash Flow Cycle

Value is not created by holding assets — it is created by generating more cash flow than was invested. The cash flows paid to bondholders and shareholders must exceed the cash flows they originally put into the firm. This is the basic test of value creation in corporate finance.

1. Investors provide capital
Bondholders lend money (buying bonds or providing loans). Shareholders invest in exchange for ownership stakes. This capital flows into the firm via financial markets — the primary market for new issuances.
2. Firm invests in assets
Capital is deployed into current assets (cash, inventory, receivables) and fixed assets (machinery, buildings, technology). These assets are used to produce goods and services and generate operating cash flows.
3. Assets generate cash flows
Operating activities generate cash. This is split three ways: taxes go to government, some cash is paid out to investors (dividends, interest, principal), and some is retained and reinvested to reduce future financing needs.
Core principle — value creation
Firms raise funds from financial markets, invest in assets to generate operating cash flows, pay taxes and return cash to investors, and reinvest retained earnings. Value is created when the cash flows returned to investors exceed the cash flows originally provided by them — when the firm earns a return above its cost of capital.

The Income Statement

While the balance sheet is a snapshot of a firm's position at a single point in time, the income statement is a flow statement — it records all revenues earned and all expenses incurred during a period, and establishes the net income or net loss for that period. Think of the balance sheet as two photographs (opening and closing) and the income statement as the film that shows everything that happened between them.

The income statement is the flow of cash between two balance sheets. The balance sheet alone cannot tell you how the firm's cash was generated — only the income statement can explain that journey from revenue to profit.
— Corporate Finance Foundations

The income statement cascades downward through several layers of profit, each one stripping away another category of cost. This waterfall structure is deliberate — it separates operating performance from financing decisions from tax obligations, allowing analysts to evaluate each layer independently.

The key insight is that EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) belongs entirely to capital providers (both debt and equity), while the net income that ultimately reaches the bottom of the statement belongs solely to equity holders. This is why EBIT-based ratios allow fair comparison across firms with different capital structures — they measure operating performance before any financing decisions affect the result.

The profit waterfall — five layers
① RevenuePrice × Quantity sold
② Gross ProfitRevenue − COGS
③ EBIT (Operating Income)Gross Profit − OpEx
④ EBT (Earnings Before Tax)EBIT ± Non-op items
⑤ Net IncomeEBT − Tax
Key line items explained
  • Revenue / Net Sales — total sales minus any returns, discounts, and allowances. The top line.
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) — direct costs of producing goods or services: raw materials, direct labour, manufacturing overhead
  • Gross Profit — what is left after paying to produce; measures core production profitability
  • SG&A — Selling, General & Administrative expenses: salaries, rent, marketing, management costs — the overhead of running the business
  • D&A — Depreciation and Amortisation: non-cash allocation of asset costs over time (see EBITDA note)
  • EBIT — operating profit; belongs to all capital providers; the purest measure of operating performance
  • Interest expense — cost of debt financing; below EBIT because it is a financing decision, not an operating one
  • Net Income — the bottom line; belongs entirely to equity holders; flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet

Financial Markets: Structure & Composition

Firms need cash to invest in new projects. Financial markets are the mechanism through which that cash is raised. Understanding the structure of financial markets — primary vs secondary, money vs capital, debt vs equity — is essential to understanding how the financing decision operates in practice.

Money markets vs capital markets
Money marketsShort-term debt <1yr
Trading venuePrimarily OTC
ExamplesLIBOR, EURIBOR
Capital marketsLong-term debt >1yr + equity
Trading venueExchanges (NYSE, LSE)
ExamplesBonds, equities
Primary markets vs secondary markets
Primary markets: The firm issues securities (stocks or bonds) for the first time. This is where the firm actually raises new capital. Mechanisms include IPOs (Initial Public Offerings), SEOs (Seasoned Equity Offerings), and private placement. The firm receives the proceeds.
Secondary markets: Buying and selling of previously issued securities between investors. The firm receives no cash from secondary market transactions — only investors trade with each other. Key functions: price discovery and liquidity provision. Examples: NYSE, Euronext, LSE.
Secondary market liquidity makes primary market issuance possible — investors need to know they can exit
Equity issuance routes
  • Private negotiation — sold to a small number of institutional investors directly (private placement)
  • IPO — Initial Public Offering; first listing on a public exchange, raising capital from the general public
  • SEO — Seasoned Equity Offering; further shares issued by a company already listed
  • Equity gives up ownership but creates no fixed repayment obligation
Debt issuance routes
  • Bank loans (private credit) — bilateral or syndicated loan agreements; more common in continental Europe and for small firms
  • Bonds (public debt) — debt securities traded in financial markets; accessible at scale for large firms
  • Debt is cheaper than equity but creates fixed obligations that can lead to distress
  • Publicly issued bonds are tradeable in secondary markets, providing liquidity for bondholders
Banking system & other markets
  • Bank lending — firms can bypass capital markets entirely and borrow from banks; standard route for SMEs
  • Interbank market — where banks lend to each other (LIBOR, EURIBOR benchmark these rates)
  • Foreign exchange market — spot FX transactions for international trade and cross-border investment
  • Bank financing is more flexible but typically more expensive than public debt at scale

Financial Ratio Analysis

Financial ratios are the primary toolkit for evaluating a firm's performance. They transform raw accounting numbers into standardised metrics that can be compared over time (against the firm's own history) or across companies (relative to industry peers). Seven categories of ratios cover the full spectrum of corporate performance — from profitability to solvency to market valuation.

Profitability ratios measure how efficiently a firm converts revenue into profit at different stages of the income statement. They require both the balance sheet (for equity) and the income statement — which records the flow of revenues and expenses between balance sheet dates, establishing net income for the period.

Gross Margin
Gross Profit / Sales
= (Sales − Cost of Sales) / Sales
Measures the firm's ability to sell a product for more than it costs to produce. A high gross margin means the core business model is inherently profitable before any operating expenses are deducted. It reflects pricing power and production efficiency.
Operating Margin
Operating Income / Sales
= (Gross Profit − Operating Expenses) / Sales
Reveals how much a company earns before interest and taxes from each dollar of sales. This is the profit from actual operations — after all operating costs (salaries, rent, D&A) but before financing costs. Compares across firms with different capital structures.
Net Profit Margin
Net Income / Sales
Shows the fraction of each dollar of revenue available to equity holders after the firm has paid all expenses, interest, and taxes. The bottom-line profitability measure. Low margins don't necessarily indicate poor performance — high-volume, low-margin businesses (e.g. supermarkets) can still be highly profitable in absolute terms.
Income statement — simplified structure & link to equity
  • Operating Revenue = Price × Quantity sold
  • Operating Costs = Cost × Quantity + Depreciation
  • Operating Income (EBIT) = Op Revenue − Op Costs
  • Net Income = EBIT + Financial Revenue − Financial Expenses − Tax
  • Net income increases retained earnings → increases book equity
  • Net loss reduces retained earnings → reduces book equity
  • Dividends paid reduce retained earnings directly
  • Key link: Income Statement → Retained Earnings → Balance Sheet Equity

Liquidity ratios assess the firm's ability to meet its short-term obligations using available assets. Creditors use these ratios to evaluate whether the firm has sufficient working capital to avoid a cash shortfall. Each ratio is progressively more stringent — the cash ratio is the most conservative test.

Current Ratio
Current Assets / Current Liabilities
The broadest liquidity measure. Includes all current assets, including inventory. A ratio above 1.0 means the firm has more short-term assets than short-term obligations. Benchmark: typically 1.5–2.0x is considered healthy, though this varies significantly by industry.
Example: 761 / 486 = 1.63×
Quick Ratio (Acid Test)
(Cash + ST Investments + AR) / CL
More stringent than the current ratio because inventory is excluded. Inventory must first be sold (and the sale collected) before it becomes cash — making it less immediately liquid. The quick ratio asks: can we pay our obligations without relying on selling inventory? A conservative worst-case test.
Example: (761 − 269) / 486 = 1.01×
Cash Ratio
Cash / Current Liabilities
The most stringent liquidity test. Uses only actual cash (and cash equivalents) — the most liquid asset. Asks: if every short-term obligation came due today, could we pay immediately with cash on hand? Very low ratios are common and not necessarily alarming — most firms are not expected to hold enough cash to repay all current liabilities at once.
Example: 140 / 486 = 0.29×
Why is inventory excluded from the quick ratio? Because inventory must first be sold, the sale may be on credit, and collection may take weeks — making it unreliable in a short-term cash crunch. The quick ratio gives a more conservative, realistic picture of immediate liquidity.
— Liquidity Ratio Foundations

Working capital ratios measure how efficiently the firm manages the cycle of cash → inventory → sales → receivables → cash. Together, these ratios form the Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) — the number of days the firm's cash is tied up in operations before being recovered as revenue.

Accounts Receivable Days (DSO)
AR Days = AR / (Sales / 365)
Measures the average number of days it takes to collect cash from customers after a credit sale. Lower is better — it means faster collection and improved cash flow. High AR days may indicate weak credit control or customers in financial difficulty.
Example: AR = 294, Sales = 2,262 → AR Days = 294 / (2,262/365) ≈ 47 days. Customers take 47 days on average to pay.
Inventory Days (Days Inventory Held)
Inventory Days = Inventory / (COGS / 365)
Measures how many days inventory sits on the shelf before being sold. Lower is better — faster turnover means less capital tied up in stock and lower obsolescence risk. Cost of Sales is used (not revenue) because inventory is recorded at cost.
Example: Inventory = 269, COGS = 1,655 → Inventory Days = 269 / (1,655/365) ≈ 59 days.
Accounts Payable Days
AP Days = AP / (COGS / 365)
Measures the average number of days the firm takes to pay its suppliers. Higher payable days improve short-term cash flow (the firm is essentially borrowing from suppliers). But excessively high payable days can damage supplier relationships and result in less favourable purchase terms.
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)
CCC = AR Days + Inv Days − AP Days
The CCC measures the number of days cash is tied up in the operating cycle. A lower CCC is better — it means the firm converts its operations into cash more quickly. Negative CCC (common in supermarkets and software) means the firm collects from customers before it has to pay suppliers — a powerful cash flow advantage.
Turnover ratios = 365 / Days ratio. Higher turnover = shorter days = more efficient

Interest coverage ratios measure the firm's ability to service its debt obligations from operating earnings. Lenders use these ratios to assess whether a borrower is earning enough to comfortably cover its interest payments. They are also called solvency ratios because they test the firm's financial sustainability under its current debt load.

EBIT-based Interest Coverage
Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest Expense
How many times over the firm can cover interest from operating profit
EBITDA-based Interest Coverage
Interest Coverage = EBITDA / Interest Expense
Adds back non-cash D&A — a better proxy for cash available to service debt
EBITDA is a proxy for operating cash flow, not actual cash flow. It is commonly used in credit analysis because it removes non-cash depreciation and amortisation — costs that reduce accounting profit but do not reduce the firm's ability to pay interest in cash.

EBIT vs EBITDA — what's the difference?
  • EBIT = Operating profit after D&A → reflects the full economic cost of using assets
  • EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation + Amortisation → removes non-cash charges to approximate cash generation
  • D&A is a non-cash expense — cash left the firm when the asset was bought, not when depreciation is recorded each year
  • EBITDA ≠ cash flow — it ignores capex, working capital changes, and tax payments
Why depreciation & amortisation exist
  • Matching principle — spread asset cost over the years the asset helps generate revenue
  • True cost of operations — machines wear out; this cost must be reflected in profit
  • Tax shield — D&A reduces taxable income, creating real cash savings
  • Depreciation applies to tangible assets; amortisation to intangible assets (patents, software)

Leverage ratios measure the extent to which a firm relies on debt as a source of financing. More leverage amplifies both returns to equity holders (in good times) and losses (in bad times). High leverage increases financial risk — the probability that the firm cannot service its debt obligations, potentially leading to financial distress or bankruptcy.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio
Total Debt / Total Equity
The most commonly cited leverage ratio. Example: (486 + 588) / 805 = 1.33 = 133%
Debt-to-Capital Ratio
Total Debt / (Total Equity + Total Debt)
What fraction of the firm's total capital is debt-funded?
Net Debt
Total Debt − Excess Cash & ST Investments
More relevant than gross debt — a firm with more cash than debt has no effective leverage
Debt-to-Enterprise Value
Net Debt / (Market Value of Equity + Net Debt)
Uses market values, not book values — more forward-looking
Example — leverage ratios from sample firm

Total equity (book)£805m

Equity multiplier
Equity Multiplier = Total Assets / Book Equity
= 1,879 / 805 ≈ 2.33. Equivalent to 1 + Debt/Equity = 1 + 1.33
Captures the amplification of accounting returns that results from leverage. A multiplier of 2.33 means the firm holds £2.33 of assets per £1 of equity — the remainder is funded by debt. This magnifies ROE relative to ROA.
Enterprise Value (EV)
EV = Market Value of Equity + Net Debt. It represents the total value of the company defined in terms of how it is financed. EV is capital-structure-neutral — useful for comparing firms with different debt levels. It is the value an acquirer would need to pay to own the whole business (assuming the acquirer also inherits the debt).

Valuation ratios compare a firm's market value to its accounting metrics to assess whether it is over- or under-valued relative to peers. Unlike other ratios that use book values, valuation ratios incorporate the market's forward-looking expectations about future profitability and growth.

Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
P/E = Market Cap / Net Income = Share Price / EPS
How many dollars investors pay per dollar of current earnings

A high P/E ratio typically reflects high expected growth (investors paying a premium for future earnings) or low perceived risk. A low P/E may indicate undervaluation, slow expected growth, or high risk. Because P/E uses net income — which is affected by the firm's debt structure — it has limited usefulness comparing firms with very different leverage levels.

EV-based multiples (capital-structure-neutral)
EV / Revenue   ·   EV / EBIT   ·   EV / EBITDA
EV multiples are preferred for comparing firms across different capital structures
P/E ratio — key properties
  • Highest P/E ratios in high-growth industries (technology, biotech) — market pays for future earnings
  • Lower P/E for riskier firms — investors demand more current earnings per dollar paid
  • P/E is not meaningful if earnings are negative — use EV/Revenue instead
  • Sensitive to capital structure — two identical businesses with different leverage will show different P/E ratios
Why EV multiples are preferred
  • EV includes debt — reflects the full cost of ownership, not just equity value
  • EBITDA and EBIT are pre-interest — not affected by capital structure choices
  • Allows apples-to-apples comparison across firms with different debt levels
  • EV/Revenue used when EBIT is negative — common for early-stage firms

Operating return ratios evaluate how efficiently the firm generates profit from the capital invested in it. They answer the question: for every pound of capital put into this business, how many pounds of profit does it generate? The three key ratios — ROE, ROA, and ROIC — each have different strengths and limitations.

Return on Equity (ROE)
ROE = Net Income / Book Value of Equity
Example: 86 / 805 ≈ 10.7%
Measures the return earned on shareholders' past investments. A high ROE may indicate the firm finds very profitable opportunities — but it can also be inflated by high leverage (the equity multiplier effect). ROE is highly sensitive to capital structure.
Return on Assets (ROA)
ROA = (Net Income + Interest) / Book Assets
Example: 86 / 1,879 ≈ 4.6%
Less sensitive to leverage than ROE because interest expense is added back to numerator (reflecting returns to all capital providers) and total assets fund both debt and equity. However, ROA is sensitive to working capital — an equal increase in receivables and payables raises total assets without changing earnings, mechanically lowering ROA.
Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)
ROIC = EBIT×(1−t) / (Equity + Net Debt)
The most analytically rigorous return measure. Uses after-tax operating profit (NOPAT) relative to the capital actually invested in the business (equity plus net debt, excluding excess cash). ROIC is the most useful measure for assessing genuine business performance because it eliminates distortions from both leverage and working capital fluctuations.
Example: 219 / (1,118 + 761 − 486) ≈ 15.7%

The DuPont Identity — Decomposing ROE

The DuPont Identity is one of the most powerful analytical tools in corporate finance. It decomposes Return on Equity (ROE) into its three fundamental drivers, revealing exactly where a firm's equity returns come from — and where they can be improved. Rather than treating ROE as a single number, DuPont forces you to understand the operating mechanics behind it.

The DuPont Identity
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
= (Net Income / Sales) × (Sales / Total Assets) × (Total Assets / Book Equity)
Component 1 — Net Profit Margin
Net Income / Sales
Measures overall profitability — how much of each pound of revenue is kept as profit after all expenses and taxes. A higher margin indicates the firm is more efficient at converting sales into bottom-line profit, whether through pricing power, cost discipline, or both.
Example: 86 / 2,262 ≈ 3.8%
Component 2 — Asset Turnover
Sales / Total Assets
Measures asset efficiency — how much revenue the firm generates for every pound of assets deployed. A higher turnover indicates assets are being used productively. Asset-heavy industries (utilities, manufacturing) typically have low turnover; asset-light industries (consulting, software) have high turnover.
Example: 2,262 / 1,879 ≈ 1.2×
Component 3 — Equity Multiplier
Total Assets / Book Equity
Measures financial leverage — how many pounds of assets the firm holds per pound of equity. The greater the reliance on debt financing, the higher this multiplier will be. Leverage amplifies both positive and negative outcomes for equity holders. It is equivalent to (1 + Debt/Equity).
Example: 1,879 / 805 ≈ 2.33 = 1 + 1.33

Combining the three components from our example firm:

DuPont Calculation — Example Firm
ROE = 3.8% × 1.2 × 2.33 ≈ 10.7%
Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier

This breakdown is far more informative than simply reporting ROE = 10.7%. It tells us that the firm's equity returns are moderate in isolation (3.8% margin), the assets are generating reasonable revenue (1.2× turnover), and leverage is amplifying those returns by a factor of 2.33. If ROE needed to improve, management now knows exactly which lever to pull.

How to improve ROE via DuPont
  • Improve margin — raise prices, cut costs, optimise product mix, improve operating efficiency
  • Improve asset turnover — generate more revenue per pound of assets; reduce idle assets; improve inventory and receivables management
  • Increase leverage — use more debt financing; but this increases financial risk — there are limits
Two firms with same ROE may have very different risk/quality profiles — DuPont reveals why

Expanding DuPont: Linking to Market Value

The DuPont system can be extended further to incorporate market valuation — connecting accounting performance (ROE) to the market's forward-looking assessment (P/E ratio) to explain a firm's market-to-book ratio. This full expansion reveals the three drivers of shareholder wealth: operating performance, financial leverage, and market expectations of growth.

From ROE to market-to-book
Market-to-book = Market Value of Equity / Book Value of Equity. For most successful firms, this exceeds 1.0 because the market value of assets in productive use exceeds their historical accounting cost. The gap is driven by intangible assets, goodwill, growth options, and market confidence in management.
High market-to-book = market believes significant value exists beyond the balance sheet
Market Value Added (MVA)
MVA = Market Value of Equity − Capital Contributed by Shareholders. It represents the total value a firm has created above and beyond the capital shareholders invested. Positive MVA indicates the firm has created value; negative MVA means it has destroyed capital. MVA is the most comprehensive external measure of whether management is succeeding.
MVA > 0 = value created · MVA < 0 = value destroyed
Book value vs market value
  • Book value = accounting value; based on historical costs; backward-looking
  • Market value = what someone is willing to pay; forward-looking; incorporates expectations
  • Gap arises from intangibles, growth options, and assets not marked-to-market
  • Use market values for investment and financing decisions; book values for financial analysis and reporting

Key formulas — exam reference
  • Assets = Liabilities + Equity — fundamental accounting equation; always holds
  • NWC = Current Assets − Current Liabilities — measures short-term liquidity buffer
  • Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Sales — core production profitability
  • EBITDA = EBIT + D&A — operating cash-flow proxy (not actual cash flow)
  • Interest Coverage = EBIT / Interest — benchmark >5× for high-quality borrowers
  • ROE = Margin × Turnover × Equity Multiplier — the DuPont decomposition
  • ROIC = EBIT(1−t) / (Equity + Net Debt) — best measure of business performance
  • EV = Market Cap + Net Debt — total firm value regardless of capital structure
  • P/E = Market Cap / Net Income — market's multiple on current earnings
  • CCC = AR Days + Inv Days − AP Days — days cash is tied up in the operating cycle
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • Depreciation is NOT a cash outflow — cash leaves when the asset is purchased, not year-by-year as depreciation is recorded
  • EBITDA ≠ cash flow — it ignores capex, working capital changes, and actual tax payments; never confuse the two
  • High ROE ≠ good performance — a highly leveraged firm will have a mechanically high ROE; use ROIC to strip out this distortion
  • Dividends are NOT an expense — they reduce retained earnings directly, not via the income statement
  • Negative NWC ≠ always bad — supermarkets and some tech firms run negative NWC by design (collect before paying suppliers)
  • Market value ≠ book value — book value is historical cost accounting; market value reflects forward-looking investor expectations
  • P/E is useless for negative earnings firms — use EV/Revenue or EV/EBITDA instead
  • High inventory days are not always bad — context matters; compare to industry benchmarks, not absolute levels
STEP 1
Understand the balance sheet — the foundation of everything
Assets = Liabilities + Equity always. Assets ordered by liquidity; liabilities by maturity. Current vs long-term distinction is critical for working capital and solvency analysis. Every corporate finance decision maps to one side of the balance sheet.
STEP 2
Map the four decisions to the balance sheet
Investment decision = left side (assets). Financing decision = right side (debt/equity). Working capital management = top portion of both sides. Payout decision = lower right (equity). Every management decision has a balance sheet home.
STEP 3
Calculate and interpret ratios from the income statement and balance sheet
Seven ratio categories cover the full performance spectrum. Always compare ratios to industry peers and historical trends — absolute values are rarely informative in isolation. Understand why each ratio is constructed the way it is before memorising the formula.
STEP 4
Apply the DuPont identity to decompose ROE
ROE = Margin × Turnover × Equity Multiplier. A firm with high ROE driven by a high equity multiplier (leverage) has a very different risk profile from one with high ROE driven by superior margins. DuPont separates operating performance from financial engineering.
STEP 5
Connect book value to market value and the firm's ultimate objective
Market value captures what investors believe the firm will generate in the future. Book value captures historical cost. The gap between them — market value added (MVA) — is the truest measure of whether management is creating or destroying value. The firm's ultimate objective is to maximise this gap.
One-line synthesis — Corporate Finance Foundations
Corporate finance reduces to a single idea: use capital efficiently to generate cash flows that exceed what investors put in. Every ratio, formula, and framework — from the balance sheet equation to the DuPont identity — is ultimately a tool for measuring whether the firm is succeeding at that objective, and for identifying precisely where it can do better.
