What Is a Leveraged Buyout?

A leveraged buyout (LBO) is the acquisition of a company using a significant amount of borrowed money — typically 50–70% of the purchase price — alongside a smaller equity contribution from the private equity sponsor. The acquired company's own assets and future cash flows serve as collateral for the debt. The result: the PE firm controls a large asset while committing relatively little of its own capital, dramatically amplifying potential returns on equity.

The core insight is simple but powerful: if the business generates more in operating returns than it costs to service the debt, the equity holder captures the entire surplus. Leverage is the financial equivalent of buying a house with a mortgage — you put in 20%, borrow 80%, and if the asset appreciates, your equity return is multiplied. The PE firm's job is to buy the right house, improve it, and sell it at a higher price before the mortgage becomes a burden.

Private equity firms don't just buy companies — they engineer returns. The LBO is the instrument that allows a disciplined operator to transform a good business into an exceptional investment by combining financial leverage with genuine operational improvement.
Private Markets Intelligence — LBO Mechanics
Why debt amplifies equity returns
  • Consider buying a £100m business: all-equity at 2× exit = £100m profit (100% return). With 60% debt (£60m): equity in = £40m, exit equity = £140m → 250% return on equity at the same 2× asset return
  • Debt is cheaper than equity (interest is tax-deductible; lenders accept lower returns for seniority in the capital structure)
  • By substituting cheap debt for expensive equity, the sponsor reduces the equity required and boosts equity IRR — as long as the business earns more than the interest rate on debt
  • The interest tax shield adds further value: interest payments reduce taxable income, so the government effectively subsidises part of the cost of debt
Rule: leverage amplifies both gains AND losses — works brilliantly for stable cash flows, catastrophically for volatile ones
The LBO transaction in four steps
  • 1. Identify target: PE firm sources a company meeting LBO criteria (stable cash flows, strong market position, identifiable operational improvements)
  • 2. Structure financing: Arrange a debt package (senior secured, TLB, mezzanine) alongside the equity cheque. Debt is sized based on target leverage ratios and the company's EBITDA
  • 3. Acquire & operate: Complete the acquisition, implement the 100-day plan and strategic value creation agenda over the hold period. Cash flows service debt, reducing leverage over time
  • 4. Exit: Sell the company (IPO, trade sale, secondary buyout) — ideally at a higher multiple than entry — and return capital to LPs
Typical hold: 3–7 years. Target: 2.0–3.5× MOIC, 20–30% IRR
Brief history — from raiders to institutions
  • 1980s: KKR's $31.1bn buyout of RJR Nabisco (1988) defined the decade — junk bonds financed aggressive takeovers, often hostile. Michael Milken and Drexel Burnham pioneered high-yield debt markets that made large LBOs possible
  • 1990s–2000s: Industry matures. Focus shifts from pure financial engineering to operational improvement. Mega-buyouts of the 2005–07 vintage (Hilton Hotels, Harrah's, TXU) reached $40bn+
  • Post-GFC: Tighter credit markets reduced leverage multiples. Operational value creation, add-on acquisitions, and buy-and-build strategies became essential — not optional. ESG enters underwriting conversations
  • Today: $1.1T+ in annual PE deal volume. Technology, healthcare, and software LBOs dominate. Continuation funds and NAV lending reshape the exit landscape

The LBO Capital Structure

The capital structure of an LBO is a layered stack of financing, each layer carrying a different risk profile, seniority in repayment, and cost of capital. Think of it as a waterfall: in a liquidation, senior lenders get paid first, mezzanine second, and equity holders receive whatever remains. This seniority hierarchy is what allows senior lenders to accept lower interest rates and equity holders to demand much higher returns for bearing first-loss risk.

ILLUSTRATIVE LBO CAPITAL STRUCTURE — £500M DEAL
Senior Secured (TLA / TLB)£200m · 40% · SOFR+350–450bps
Second Lien / Senior Notes£75m · 15% · 7–9% fixed
High-Yield Bonds / Mezzanine£50m · 10% · 10–13% PIK/cash
Vendor / Rollover Equity£25m · 5%
Sponsor Equity£150m · 30% · target 20–30% IRR
Total: £500m · Debt/EBITDA = ~5.5× (assuming £50m EBITDA) · Entry multiple = 10× EV/EBITDA
Senior secured debt — the backbone
  • Term Loan A (TLA): bank-held, amortises (repaid in instalments) over 5–7 years. Lower cost, quarterly cash interest
  • Term Loan B (TLB): sold to institutional investors (CLOs, hedge funds). Minimal amortisation (1%/yr bullet), 7-year maturity. Most LBOs use TLB as primary vehicle — flexibility is key
  • Revolving Credit Facility (RCF): undrawn backstop for working capital needs; not drawn at close but available. Commitment fee on the undrawn portion
  • Secured against company assets: property, receivables, IP. Carries financial covenants (leverage ratio, interest coverage)
Senior secured: cheapest debt, first claim on assets. Typical cost: SOFR/SONIA + 350–500bps
Mezzanine & high-yield — the subordinated layer
  • High-Yield Bonds: publicly traded, fixed-rate. Longer maturities (8–10yrs). No amortisation. Covenants are "incurrence-based" (looser than bank debt). Callable after 4–5 years
  • Mezzanine: privately placed, subordinated. May include PIK (Payment-in-Kind) interest — rolled up rather than paid in cash, preserving FCF for senior debt service
  • Second Lien: similar economics to mezzanine but secured on the same collateral as senior debt — with a second-priority claim
  • These layers exist because they allow PE sponsors to increase total leverage beyond what senior lenders will provide alone, stretching the total debt quantum and reducing the equity cheque
Leverage ratios — how debt is sized
All debt is sized as a multiple of the company's EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation & Amortisation). EBITDA is the proxy for operating cash generation before financing and accounting effects.
Senior leverage (Senior Debt / EBITDA)3.0× – 4.5×
Total leverage (Total Debt / EBITDA)4.0× – 7.0×
Interest coverage (EBITDA / Interest)≥ 2.0×
Debt-to-equity ratio60:40 typical
Leverage multiples expand in benign credit conditions (low rates, tight spreads) and compress in stressed markets. The 2021–22 vintage saw 7–8× leverage; post-2023 tightening brought multiples back to 4–5.5×.
Debt covenants — the guardrails
  • Financial covenants (maintenance): tested quarterly. Company must stay within agreed leverage and coverage ratios at all times (e.g., Debt/EBITDA < 6×, Interest cover > 2×). Breach triggers renegotiation or default. Bank loans are covenant-heavy; TLBs often "cov-lite"
  • Incurrence covenants: only tested when the company takes a specific action (additional debt, restricted payments, asset sales). Less restrictive — used in HY bonds and cov-lite TLBs
  • Restricted payment baskets: limit how much cash can flow up to the sponsor (dividends, management fees) versus staying in the business to service debt
  • EBITDA add-backs: banks and borrowers negotiate what counts as EBITDA — synergies, one-offs, run-rate cost savings. A contentious battleground in deal negotiations

Building the LBO Model

The LBO model is the analytical heart of every private equity deal. It is a financial model that answers one question: given this entry price, this capital structure, and these operating assumptions, what returns will the equity investor earn at exit? It works backwards from a target IRR to determine whether a given price is supportable, or forwards from a given price to determine what IRR the investment will generate.

The model begins with the Sources & Uses — a simple table accounting for every pound in and out at deal close. Sources = where the money comes from (debt tranches + sponsor equity). Uses = where it goes (equity purchase price, existing debt refinanced, transaction fees).

Entry Enterprise Value
EV = Entry Multiple × LTM EBITDA
LTM = Last Twelve Months. Entry multiple reflects the negotiated price vs comparable transactions and trading multiples. A 10× EV/EBITDA entry on a £50m EBITDA business = £500m EV.
Equity Purchase Price
Equity Value = EV − Net Debt
Net Debt = gross debt minus cash on the balance sheet. The equity cheque buys the equity value of the business.

At exit, the PE firm sells the business (or IPOs it) and the proceeds flow: first to debt repayment, then to equity holders. The exit equity value is simply the exit enterprise value minus remaining net debt.

Exit Enterprise Value
Exit EV = Exit Multiple × Exit Year EBITDA
Exit multiple may be higher, lower, or the same as entry. "Multiple expansion" (exit > entry) is one of the three value creation levers — but models are typically built assuming flat multiples to be conservative.
Returns Calculation
Equity Proceeds = Exit EV − Net Debt at Exit
MOIC = Equity Proceeds / Equity Invested
IRR: solve 0 = −Equity_0 + Equity_Exit/(1+IRR)⁵
MOIC = Money-on-Invested-Capital. IRR accounts for the time value of money — the same MOIC achieved in 3 years vs 7 years generates a very different IRR.

MOIC vs IRR — why both matter
  • MOIC tells you how many times you multiplied your money — but ignores time. 3× in 3 years is better than 3× in 10 years
  • IRR accounts for time — the annualised return. Preferred by LPs and used for fund benchmarking. Sensitive to hold period: the same exit value generates a much higher IRR if achieved in year 3 vs year 6
  • Best used together: a 2.5× / 25% IRR deal is better than 3.0× / 18% IRR for most institutions given capital deployment cycles

The Three Levers of Value Creation

Every pound of equity value created in an LBO comes from exactly one of three sources — and the best PE firms use all three simultaneously. Understanding which lever contributes most to a given deal's returns reveals a lot about the quality of the investment thesis and the skill of the sponsor.

Value creation bridge — decomposing equity returns (illustrative £m)
Entry equity £150m → Exit equity £656m · 3.7× MOIC
Entry equity Debt paydown EBITDA growth Multiple expansion Exit equity
Lever 1 — EBITDA growth
Growing EBITDA directly increases enterprise value at exit (since EV = Multiple × EBITDA). This is the most sustainable and valued source of return — it reflects genuine business improvement, not financial engineering.
  • Revenue growth: organic expansion, pricing power, new geographies, new products. Often the hardest to execute but creates durable competitive moats
  • Margin improvement: procurement savings, operational efficiencies, headcount rationalisation, overhead reduction, insourcing. PE firms bring operating partners, specialist consultants, and cross-portfolio benchmarking
  • Buy-and-build: using the platform company as a base to acquire smaller competitors ("add-ons"), achieving scale, eliminating duplication, and buying at lower multiples than the exit valuation. A 5× add-on absorbed into a 10× platform creates instant value
  • EBITDA add-backs: restructuring one-off costs before the exit P&L to present the cleanest possible "run-rate" EBITDA to buyers
Historically ~35–50% of LBO equity returns globally from EBITDA growth
Lever 2 — debt paydown
As the company generates FCF and services debt, the outstanding principal falls. At exit, lower debt = higher equity value from the same EV. This is the "mechanical" component of LBO returns — it occurs automatically if the business hits its FCF projections.
  • Cash sweep: excess FCF above mandatory amortisation is automatically swept to voluntary debt repayment. The healthier the business, the faster debt falls
  • Working capital optimisation: releasing cash trapped in receivables and inventory (faster collection, extended payables) accelerates deleveraging. PE firms often conduct intensive NWC reviews in the first 100 days
  • Asset disposals: selling non-core assets or real estate (sale-and-leaseback) generates one-off cash that reduces debt ahead of schedule
  • Dividend recaps: once leverage has fallen sufficiently, the company takes on new debt specifically to return capital to the sponsor mid-hold — monetising part of the gain before exit. Common in robust credit markets
Pure financial engineering contribution: historically ~25–35% of LBO returns
Lever 3 — multiple expansion
If the company is sold at a higher EV/EBITDA multiple than it was bought, the equity holder benefits from this re-rating independently of any operational improvement. Multiple expansion is the least reliable lever — it depends on market conditions, buyer appetite, and timing — but historically it has contributed meaningfully to PE returns in bull markets.
  • Why multiples expand: improved quality of earnings, reduced risk profile, enlarged scale attracting a wider buyer universe, sector tailwinds, or simply a better credit/M&A market at exit vs entry
  • Software premium: PE firms have aggressively pivoted portfolios toward SaaS and tech-enabled businesses — recurring revenues command 15–25× EBITDA vs 8–12× for industrial businesses
  • The risk: PE firms that rely on multiple expansion are making an implicit market bet. When markets contract (2022–23), expected multiple expansion becomes multiple compression, destroying projected returns. Good sponsors build cases that work at flat or compressed multiples
  • Arbitrage: buy-and-build explicitly exploits multiple arbitrage — small add-ons bought at 4–6× are valued at the platform multiple of 10–12× at exit
Highly cycle-dependent: drove 30–40% of returns in 2010–21 bull run; negligible/negative post-2022

Exit Routes & Return Dynamics

Exit is where PE returns are crystallised. The choice of exit route — and the timing — significantly affects realised returns. In theory, exit simply involves selling the equity to a new buyer at an agreed enterprise value. In practice, it involves years of preparation, positioning the business for the specific buyer universe, and navigating credit markets and valuation cycles.

Exit route 1 — trade sale / strategic sale
  • Selling to a corporate acquirer (competitor, adjacent business, conglomerate seeking diversification). Typically achieves the highest valuation because strategic buyers can justify paying for synergies that a financial buyer cannot
  • Synergy value: the acquirer expects cost savings or revenue uplift post-acquisition, so pays a "control premium" above standalone value. A trade buyer might pay 12–14× for a business worth 10× to a financial buyer
  • Downside: management team may not have the same autonomy post-close; cultural integration risk; buyers conduct extensive due diligence; negotiations can be protracted
  • Common in mid-market PE where businesses achieve scale sufficient to interest large strategics but remain sub-IPO size
~40–45% of PE exits · typically highest realised multiples
Exit route 2 — secondary buyout (SBO)
  • Selling to another PE firm. Has become the most common exit route by volume in recent years as PE dry powder grew
  • Works best when the incoming sponsor has a differentiated value creation plan (e.g., buy-and-build opportunity the existing sponsor was too small to pursue, or geographic expansion requiring different relationships)
  • Sceptics argue SBOs are financial recycling without genuine value creation — each successive sponsor must find growth to justify the increasing leverage load
  • Tertiary (third) and quaternary buyouts exist. Businesses like Watchfinder have been through multiple PE owners successively
~45–50% of exits by count · valuation limited to financial buyer logic (no synergies)
Exit route 3 — IPO
  • Taking the business public via listing on a stock exchange. Historically the most prestigious exit and often associated with the largest deals
  • PE firm typically doesn't exit fully at IPO — retains a stake and sells down over 12–24 months subject to lock-up periods. Returns are therefore not fully crystallised at listing
  • Requires scale (typically £200m+ EV), a compelling growth narrative, robust governance and reporting infrastructure, and receptive equity capital markets
  • IPO markets are volatile — window-dependent. The 2022–23 ECM drought forced many PE-backed IPOs to be delayed or shelved. When markets are open, IPO can achieve the highest theoretical valuation (no control discount)
  • Post-IPO lock-up periods mean IRR calculation becomes complex — partial exits over time
~10–15% of exits · window-dependent · enables gradual monetisation
Exit route 4 — continuation funds & NAV loans
  • Continuation fund: the GP transfers the best portfolio companies into a new vehicle ("continuation fund"), giving existing LPs the option to cash out while new investors buy in. Allows the sponsor to hold assets longer without forcing a sub-optimal exit — the fastest-growing part of the secondaries market
  • NAV-based lending: borrowing against the net asset value of the whole portfolio to return capital to LPs or fund new investments, without selling individual assets. Creates liquidity without realising gains (and triggering tax events)
  • Both structures have drawn regulatory scrutiny as conflicts of interest (sponsor valuing assets they retain) are inherent

What Makes an Ideal LBO Target?

Not every business can support an LBO. The debt-heavy capital structure demands specific financial and operational characteristics. PE firms have refined their screening criteria over decades — learning painfully which businesses can carry leverage and which collapse under it. Understanding these criteria is the fastest way to think like a PE investor.

Financial characteristics — non-negotiable
  • Strong, predictable FCF: the business must generate consistent cash flow to service debt. Cyclical or lumpy revenue models make lenders nervous. Subscription businesses, long-term contracts, and recurring revenues are the gold standard
  • High EBITDA margins: a 25%+ EBITDA margin provides a buffer between operating profits and interest obligations. Low-margin businesses (retail, distribution) require very low leverage to be viable LBOs
  • Low CapEx requirements: a business that requires heavy reinvestment to maintain its competitive position generates less FCF available for debt service. Asset-light business models (software, business services) are structurally better LBO candidates than capital-intensive ones (manufacturing, infrastructure)
  • Moderate existing leverage: a clean, unleveraged (or lightly leveraged) balance sheet allows the acquirer to add debt without immediately breaching covenants. Companies with complex existing debt structures are harder and more expensive to refinance
Operational characteristics — value creation enablers
  • Strong market position: pricing power and defensible margins require market leadership or a genuine niche. A #3 player in a fragmented commodity market cannot pass cost inflation to customers; a #1 niche player often can
  • Identifiable operational improvements: PE doesn't want a business running at peak efficiency — it wants one with clear, underexploited levers (bloated corporate overhead, unprofessional procurement, sub-optimal pricing, fragmented back-office). The opportunity for improvement is the investment thesis
  • Non-cyclical or counter-cyclical demand: debt is a fixed obligation; revenues shouldn't be. Healthcare, education, B2B software, and business process outsourcing show low revenue volatility relative to GDP
  • Fragmented market (buy-and-build): if the sector is populated by dozens of small owner-managed businesses, a PE firm can use a platform acquisition to consolidate, achieving scale and multiple arbitrage simultaneously
  • Management team or succession opportunity: either a strong existing management team that will continue under PE ownership, or a leadership gap that PE can fill with an operating partner or hired CEO — removing a key-person risk that suppresses valuation

Sectors typically suited to LBOs
  • Software & SaaS: high margins (60%+), recurring ARR, low churn, asset-light. The darling of 2015–22 PE. High entry multiples (15–25×) demand strong growth; rate sensitivity compressed multiples post-2022
  • Business services & outsourcing: sticky contracts, high switching costs, fragmented (buy-and-build), moderate margins. Classic PE territory (Capita, Serco in early years; now more specialist BPO)
  • Healthcare services: non-discretionary demand, government-backed revenue in many cases, fragmented physician groups / dental chains / care homes ripe for consolidation
  • Consumer & retail (select): only where brand equity is strong, margins are high, and the model is not exposed to physical retail capex. Premium consumer goods, branded food & beverage
  • Industrials (niche): highly specialised components or services with dominant market share, long-term customer relationships, and high switching costs. Not commodity manufacturing
Sectors that make poor LBO candidates
  • Capital-intensive industrials: steel, mining, chemicals, aerospace manufacturing. Heavy CapEx requirements absorb FCF needed for debt service. High fixed costs create leverage to downturns — operational leverage compounds financial leverage catastrophically
  • Cyclical businesses: commodity trading, housebuilding, automotive. Revenue volatility means EBITDA can halve in a downturn while debt is fixed. The 2008 LBO failures were concentrated here
  • Regulated utilities: stable cash flows but regulated returns cap EBITDA growth; covenants and regulatory change create compliance risk; capital structure constrained by regulatory capital requirements
  • Financial services (generally): leverage ratios and capital requirements complicate the traditional LBO structure. Banks and insurers cannot simply have their balance sheets laden with additional acquisition debt
  • Early-stage / loss-making businesses: no EBITDA to size debt against; no FCF to service it. These are venture / growth equity situations — not LBOs

Landmark LBOs — Learning from Real Deals

The best way to internalise LBO mechanics is through real deals — understanding what the sponsor's thesis was, how they structured the financing, and whether the returns lived up to expectations. The full spectrum from legendary successes to catastrophic failures illustrates exactly where LBO analysis succeeds and fails in practice.

KKR — RJR Nabisco (1988) · $31.1bn · The deal that defined an era
The Barbarians at the Gate
KKR's hostile takeover of RJR Nabisco remains the most famous LBO in history. Financed with $29bn of junk bonds and bank debt against $2bn of equity, it exemplified the excesses of 1980s financial engineering. The thesis: strip out the tobacco division's cash flows to service debt while restructuring the food business. In practice, the debt load was so punishing that KKR earned only a modest return — a cautionary tale that leverage alone is not a strategy. The deal introduced "LBO" to the public lexicon and triggered congressional scrutiny of junk-bond financing.
Blackstone — Hilton Hotels (2007) · $26bn · The greatest LBO return ever?
Thesis: operational transformation of an underperforming iconic brand
Blackstone acquired Hilton for $26bn at the peak of the 2007 credit bubble — $20.5bn of debt against $5.5bn equity. Timing looked disastrous: the GFC hit immediately, hospitality revenues collapsed, and Hilton's debt-to-EBITDA spiked above 10×. But Blackstone did not panic. They worked constructively with lenders, installed a new CEO (Chris Nassetta), transformed the franchising model, and launched the Homewood Suites and Hampton Inn growth strategy. When Hilton IPO'd in 2013, Blackstone had turned $5.5bn equity into $14bn — a 2.6× MOIC and estimated 25% IRR. The lesson: operational transformation can overcome terrible timing if you have the staying power and management talent.
Apollo — ADT / Protection One (2010s) · Buy-and-build in security services
Thesis: fragmented market consolidation + recurring revenue
Apollo's security services consolidation is a textbook example of buy-and-build LBO execution. The home security monitoring business generates highly predictable recurring monthly revenue (RMR) — the perfect LBO cash flow profile. Apollo acquired multiple platforms, drove procurement savings, standardised operations, and cross-sold services, creating scale that justified premium exit multiples. The recurring revenue model meant EBITDA was visible and bankable even at high leverage multiples. This deal type — fragmented B2C/B2B service with contractual revenue — has been replicated across dental chains, care homes, and specialist outsourcing.
KKR + TPG — TXU Energy (2007) · $45bn · The largest LBO ever; the largest PE bankruptcy
The cautionary tale of regulatory and commodity risk
KKR and TPG acquired TXU (a Texas utility) for $45bn — $37bn in debt — betting that natural gas prices would stay high, keeping TXU's coal-fired generation economics advantaged. The thesis collapsed spectacularly: US shale gas revolution crashed natural gas prices, destroying TXU's (renamed Energy Future Holdings) competitive economics. The debt was never supportable and EFH filed for Chapter 11 in 2014 — the largest PE-backed bankruptcy in history, with KKR writing off its equity in full. The deal illustrates that LBO models are only as good as their macroeconomic assumptions — commodity price sensitivity is a fundamental risk that leverage cannot hedge.
Vista Equity — software consolidation (2010s–present) · Repeatable excellence
Building the playbook for enterprise software LBOs
Vista Equity Partners built a systematic approach to mid-market enterprise software LBOs: acquire founder-built or corporate-orphaned software businesses with sticky B2B customer bases, apply the "Vista Consulting Group" playbook (standardised operational improvements, cross-selling, pricing optimisation), grow organically and via tuck-in acquisitions, then exit at a significant multiple expansion. By applying the same playbook repeatedly across 80+ companies, Vista created a machine — demonstrating that operational repeatability is as valuable as financial creativity in modern PE. Returns consistently in the top quartile.

  • IRR: 0 = −E₀ + E_exit/(1+IRR)ᴺ — annualised return (accounts for time)
  • Leverage = Total Debt / EBITDA — key sizing ratio for lenders
  • Interest Cover = EBITDA / Cash Interest — debt service capacity (must be ≥2×)
  • FCF = EBITDA − Cash Interest − Cash Taxes − CapEx − ΔNWC — cash available for debt repayment
  • Tax Shield = τ × Interest Expense — annual tax saving from interest deductibility
Common LBO analysis mistakes & interview traps
  • Confusing MOIC and IRR: a 3× MOIC in year 2 vs year 7 are completely different IRRs. Always state both, and understand the hold period assumption
  • Forgetting the debt paydown contribution: EBITDA growth alone doesn't explain returns — deleveraging from 6.5× to 1.9× adds enormous equity value at the same exit multiple
  • Relying on multiple expansion: a robust LBO thesis must work at the same or lower exit multiple. Interviewers will always ask "what if markets compress?"
  • Ignoring interest coverage: it's not enough that leverage ratios look acceptable — the company must generate sufficient EBITDA to cover interest cash payments at all times during the hold
  • EBITDA ≠ FCF: a 25% EBITDA margin business with heavy CapEx and working capital growth can generate minimal FCF — leaving little for debt service. Always bridge from EBITDA to free cash flow
  • Assuming the exit is guaranteed: LBO models assume a clean exit at year 5. In practice, exits can be delayed, valuations can disappoint, and buyers can be scarce. The model is a base case, not a certainty
  • Management incentive alignment: PE firms almost always reserve a management equity pool (typically 10–15% of equity). Forgetting this dilutes sponsor returns — include it in your model
One-line synthesis — the LBO
A leveraged buyout is not financial wizardry — it is a disciplined framework for buying a good business cheaply enough, improving it operationally, and selling it expensively enough, with debt amplifying equity returns throughout. The art lies in choosing businesses with cash flows robust enough to carry the leverage, identifying the specific operational improvements that justify control, and timing the exit to realise the full value of what has been built. When all three levers align — growing EBITDA, deleveraging balance sheet, and expanding exit multiple — the LBO generates the extraordinary equity returns that have attracted capital to private markets for four decades.
