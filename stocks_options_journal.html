What is a Stock? Ownership, Rights & Valuation Logic

A stock (or equity) is a security representing fractional ownership of a company. When a firm issues shares to raise capital, each share confers a proportional claim on the firm's assets and earnings. Unlike bonds — which promise contractually fixed cash flows — stocks offer residual claims: shareholders receive whatever is left after all other claimants (employees, suppliers, creditors) have been paid. This makes equity riskier than debt, but also potentially more rewarding.

What you get as a shareholder
  • Dividends — share of profits distributed to shareholders; not contractually guaranteed (directors decide)
  • Capital gains/losses — profit or loss from changes in the share price between purchase and sale
  • Residual claim — paid after all creditors, suppliers, employees in liquidation; highest risk, highest potential return
  • Voting rights — vote on major decisions: electing board members, approving mergers, authorising new share issuances
The fundamental valuation principle
The value of any asset = PV of expected future cash flows. For a stock, those cash flows are dividends. But what about capital gains?
  • Dividends are the cash received directly each period
  • Capital gains occur because the next buyer is willing to pay more — but they pay more because they expect larger future dividends
  • Therefore: capital gains are simply the market re-pricing of future dividends — not an independent source of value
  • The stock's value today = PV of ALL future dividends, to infinity
P₀ = Σ Dₜ/(1+R)ᵗ for t=1 to ∞
Why the holding period doesn't matter
Whether you hold forever or sell at t=1, the price is the same:
P₀ = (D₁ + P₁)/(1+R)
P₁ itself = PV of all dividends from t=2 onward
Substituting P₁ back in gives the same infinite sum. The capital gain at t=1 (P₁ − P₀) is just the market updating its valuation of future dividends. The dividend discount model is therefore a complete valuation framework — not just a "long-hold" model.
Capital gains are not magic. They happen because the buyer at any future date is also discounting dividends — just from a later starting point. The entire return from owning a stock ultimately reduces to one thing: the present value of all dividends the firm will ever pay.

Case 1 — Zero Growth (Perpetuity)

The simplest stock valuation model assumes dividends remain constant forever: D₁ = D₂ = D₃ = … This is the same structure as a bond consol — a perpetuity. Firms in mature, stable industries with highly predictable earnings may approximate this behaviour (utility companies, for example).

Zero Growth Stock Price
P₀ = D₁ / R
D₁ = constant dividend per period (forever) · R = required return (discount rate)
Same as the perpetuity formula: PV = C/r from TVM

The formula reveals a critical insight: the stock price is entirely determined by the ratio of the dividend to the required return. A stock paying £5/year forever at a 10% required return is worth exactly £50. If required returns rise to 12%, the price falls to £41.67 — even though dividends haven't changed. The discount rate is the transmission mechanism between the macro environment and individual stock prices.

Zero growth sensitivity to required return
D₁ = £5, R = 5%P₀ = £100
D₁ = £5, R = 8%P₀ = £62.50
D₁ = £5, R = 10%P₀ = £50.00
D₁ = £5, R = 15%P₀ = £33.33
D₁ = £5, R = 20%P₀ = £25.00
Zero growth price vs required return (D₁ = £5)
P₀ = 5/R

Case 2 — Constant Growth (Gordon Growth Model)

The Gordon Growth Model (also called the Dividend Growth Model, DGM) is the workhorse of equity valuation. It extends the zero-growth perpetuity to allow dividends to grow at a constant rate g forever. This turns the infinite dividend stream into a growing perpetuity — the same structure encountered in TVM.

Gordon Growth Model — Stock Price
P₀ = D₁ / (R − g)
D₁ = next period's dividend · R = required return · g = constant dividend growth rate
⚠ Requires R > g — if g ≥ R, the denominator ≤ 0 and the formula breaks (price is infinite/undefined)
Rearranged: Required Return
R = D₁/P₀ + g
Required return = dividend yield + capital gains yield (= growth rate)
The total return on a stock has exactly two components

The rearrangement is powerful: it shows that the total return R on a stock equals the dividend yield (D₁/P₀) plus the capital gains rate (g). Under constant growth, dividends and prices both grow at rate g — so the capital gain each year is exactly g% of the current price. This gives a clean decomposition: income return + price appreciation = total return.

Gordon Model — Example 1 (Big D Inc.)
D₁£0.50
g (dividend growth)2% per year
R (required return)15%
P₀ = 0.50 / (0.15 − 0.02) = £3.846
Whether the investor holds forever or sells at t=1, the result is the same. Buyer at t=1 pays P₁ = D₂/(R−g) = 0.51/0.13 = £3.923. Then: P₀ = (D₁+P₁)/(1+R) = (0.50+3.923)/1.15 = £3.846 ✓
Capital gain: P₁−P₀ = £0.077 = 2% of £3.846 — exactly equal to g
Gordon Model — Example 2 (Solving for g)
Apple: D₁ = $0.82, R ∈ [8%, 10%]. What is the implied growth rate?
g = R − D₁/P₀ = R − dividend yield
If P₀ is Apple's market price, the dividend yield y_D = 0.82/P₀. Then g ∈ [0.08 − y_D, 0.10 − y_D]. If P₀ is high (low yield), most of the return comes from expected growth; if P₀ is low (high yield), growth expectations are modest. This is how analysts back out market-implied growth assumptions.
Higher R → higher implied g for any given price P₀
Gordon Model — stock price vs growth rate g (D₁=£1, R=10%)
P₀ = D₁/(R−g) · Price → ∞ as g → R
As g approaches R=10%, the denominator (R−g) shrinks toward zero and the stock price explodes toward infinity. This is why the constraint g < R is essential — no firm can grow faster than the economy forever.

Case 3 — Differential Growth (Two-Stage Model)

The differential growth model is the most realistic of the three cases. Most companies don't grow at a constant rate forever — they often have a high-growth phase early in their life (driven by new products, market expansion, competitive advantage) followed by a steady-state phase once they mature and competition intensifies. The two-stage model captures this by using growth rate g₁ for N years and g₂ thereafter.

Step 1: PV of dividends in years 1 to N (growing annuity)
PV₀(1→N) = D₁/(R−g₁) × [1−((1+g₁)/(1+R))ᴺ]
Step 2: Terminal value at time N (growing perpetuity)
Pₙ = D_{N+1} / (R − g₂)
D_{N+1} = D₁ × (1+g₁)^N × (1+g₂) — first dividend under new growth rate
Step 3: Discount terminal value to today
P₀ = PV₀(1→N) + Pₙ/(1+R)ᴺ
Add the PV of early dividends to the PV of all post-N dividends

The intuition for Step 2 is that at date N, the stock transitions to constant growth at g₂. The Gordon Model then applies — but gives a value at date N, not today. Step 3 discounts that terminal value back N years to get today's contribution. The two terms are additive — the stock's total value today is the sum of its near-term dividends plus the present value of the perpetuity that begins at the end of the high-growth phase.

Differential growth — worked example
D₁$2.00
g₁ (years 1–3)8% p.a.
g₂ (year 4 onward)4% p.a.
R (discount rate)12%
Step-by-step cash flows
D₁$2.000
D₂ = 2 × 1.08$2.160
D₃ = 2 × 1.08²$2.333
D₄ = D₃ × 1.04$2.426
P₃ = D₄/(R−g₂)$30.375
P₀ = 2/1.12 + 2.16/1.12² + (2.333+30.375)/1.12³
= 1.786 + 1.722 + 23.292 ≈ $26.80
Differential growth — dividend path & discounted cash flows
D₁=$2, g₁=8% for 3 yrs, g₂=4% perpetuity, R=12%
■ Dividend cash flow · ■ PV of that cash flow · The terminal value at t=3 ($30.375) dominates — it represents 87% of the total stock value of $26.80

Growth Parameters & the NPVGO Model

Two key parameters drive every stock valuation: the growth rate g and the discount rate R. Understanding where they come from — not just plugging them into formulas — is essential. The NPVGO model then provides a second, complementary way to decompose stock value that reveals whether growth is actually creating value.

Where g comes from — sustainable growth rate
g = Retention ratio × ROE
= (1 − Payout) × Net Income / Book Value of Equity
Retention ratio = 1 − Payout ratio = 1 − (DPS/EPS)
DPS = dividends per share · EPS = earnings per share

The logic is elegant: growth in dividends comes from growth in earnings, which comes from reinvesting retained earnings. Each period, the firm keeps (1−Payout) of its earnings and reinvests them at ROE. Those reinvested pounds earn ROE going forward — generating higher future earnings and therefore higher future dividends.

Where R comes from — return decomposition
R = D₁/P₀ + g
R = dividend yield + expected capital gains rate
Rearranging Gordon Model to solve for R rather than P₀
Intuition behind g = retention × ROE
  • Suppose EPS = £10. If payout = 40%, the firm pays £4 as dividends and retains £6
  • Those £6 are reinvested at ROE = 15%, generating £6 × 15% = £0.90 extra earnings next year
  • Growth in EPS = 0.90/10 = 9%. So g = (1−0.40) × 15% = 9% ✓
  • This formula only holds if ROE is stable and the firm is in steady state
  • Key takeaway: growth doesn't come from just reinvesting — it comes from reinvesting profitably

The NPVGO Model — Value = Cash Cow + Growth

The NPVGO (Net Present Value of Growth Opportunities) model offers a second, equivalent way to value a stock. Instead of discounting all future dividends, it splits the stock's value into two parts: what the firm is worth if it pays out everything and never grows ("cash cow"), plus the incremental value created by future reinvestment opportunities.

NPVGO Decomposition
P₀ = E₁/R + NPVGO
E₁/R = cash cow value (firm pays out all earnings as dividends, never reinvests)
NPVGO = PV of all future positive-NPV investment opportunities
The cash cow component: E₁/R
Suppose the firm never reinvests — it pays out 100% of earnings each year as dividends. Then D₁ = E₁ (dividends equal earnings), growth is zero, and the stock is a perpetuity worth E₁/R. This is the "base case" value if management never takes on any new projects. It is the value of the firm's existing assets in place.
Cash cow: zero retention, zero growth, P₀ = EPS₁/R
The NPVGO component
NPVGO captures the value created by retaining earnings and reinvesting in positive-NPV projects. Each £1 retained and reinvested creates NPV = −1 + ROE/R. If ROE > R, this is positive (value creating). NPVGO is the growing perpetuity of these per-share NPVs, starting at NPV₁ and growing at rate g.
NPVGO = NPV₁ / (R − g)
The critical condition: ROE vs R
  • ROE > R: each reinvested £1 earns more than the cost of capital → NPVGO > 0 → growth adds value → retain earnings
  • ROE = R: reinvestment earns exactly the cost of capital → NPVGO = 0 → growth is neutral → payout irrelevant
  • ROE < R: reinvestment destroys value → NPVGO < 0 → pay everything out → higher payout raises P₀
Growth only adds value when ROE > R — "profitable growth" is the key
NPVGO example — EPS₁=£5, payout=30%, ROE=20%, R=16%
DGM and NPVGO give identical P₀=£75
DGM Route
D₁ = EPS × payout£5 × 0.30 = £1.50
g = (1−0.30) × 20%14%
P₀ = 1.50/(0.16−0.14)£75.00 ✓
NPVGO Route
Cash cow: E₁/R = 5/0.16£31.25
Retained EPS₁ = 5×0.7£3.50
NPV₁ = −3.5 + 3.5×0.20/0.16£0.875
NPVGO = 0.875/(0.16−0.14)£43.75
P₀ = 31.25 + 43.75£75.00 ✓
NPVGO decomposition — stock price vs payout ratio (EPS=£5, ROE=20%, R=16%)
Shows how payout affects cash cow vs NPVGO when ROE > R
Cash cow value (E₁/R) NPVGO (growth value) Total P₀
When ROE > R: reducing payout destroys value (less growth opportunity captured). Cash cow stays fixed at E₁/R = £31.25. NPVGO rises as retention increases because each retained pound earns above the cost of capital.

The Price-Earnings Ratio

The Price-Earnings (P/E) ratio is the most widely cited valuation metric in equity markets. It measures how much investors are willing to pay per pound of current earnings. While simple to compute, it encodes expectations about growth, risk, and dividend policy — making it a rich source of information when interpreted correctly.

P/E Ratio Definition & Gordon Model Derivation
P/E = P₀/EPS₁ = Payout / (R − g)
From Gordon: P₀ = D₁/(R−g) = EPS₁ × Payout/(R−g)
→ P/E = Payout/(R−g)

This derivation reveals what drives the P/E ratio. It increases with payout ratio (investors pay more for a stock that returns more cash today) and increases with growth (higher g reduces the denominator, inflating price relative to earnings). It decreases with the discount rate R — riskier firms or higher interest rate environments attract lower P/E multiples.

P/E ratio drivers
Payout ratio ↑P/E ↑
Growth rate g ↑P/E ↑
Discount rate R ↑P/E ↓
Risk ↑ (higher R)P/E ↓
Tech stocks: high P/E because g is high. Utilities: low P/E because g is low. High-yield value stocks: higher payout but lower growth, moderate P/E.
P/E ratio vs growth rate (payout=50%, R=10%)
P/E = 0.50/(0.10−g)
P/E comparison pitfall — must compare like-for-like
  • A high P/E could mean high growth expectations — or simply that the stock is overvalued
  • A low P/E could mean cheap value — or structural decline in earnings
  • Only compare P/E across firms with similar: payout ratio, growth rate, and risk (discount rate)
  • Cross-industry P/E comparisons are almost always misleading — a 30× P/E for a tech firm and a 12× for a utility are both "fair" if growth and risk differ appropriately

Options — Calls, Puts & Payoffs

An option is a financial contract giving the holder the right but not the obligation to buy or sell an underlying asset at a pre-specified price. This asymmetry — you choose whether to exercise, but the seller must comply if you do — is the source of options' unique risk-return profile. Options are both powerful hedging instruments and flexible speculative tools.

Options as Insurance

A call option gives the holder the right to buy the underlying asset at the strike price X. You exercise a call only when it is profitable to do so — i.e., when the market price Sᴛ exceeds the strike X. If Sᴛ ≤ X, you simply let the option expire worthless; it is never rational to pay X for something trading below X in the open market.

Call Payoff at Expiration
Payoff_call = max(Sᴛ − X, 0)
Sᴛ > X (in the money): exercise — payoff = Sᴛ − X
Sᴛ = X (at the money): indifferent — payoff = 0
Sᴛ < X (out of the money): do not exercise — payoff = 0
Call — moneyness terminology
Sᴛ > XIn-the-money (ITM) — exercise
Sᴛ = XAt-the-money (ATM) — indifferent
Sᴛ < XOut-of-the-money (OTM) — expire
The buyer's loss is always capped at the premium paid upfront. The potential gain is unlimited — the higher Sᴛ rises, the more valuable the call.
Call option payoff & profit (X=50, premium=6)
Payoff = max(Sᴛ−X,0) · Profit = Payoff − 6
Payoff Profit Break-even: Sᴛ=56

A put option gives the holder the right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price X. You exercise a put only when Sᴛ < X — when you can sell at the contractually fixed price X, which is higher than the current market price. If Sᴛ ≥ X, you can sell for a higher price in the open market, so the put expires worthless.

Put Payoff at Expiration
Payoff_put = max(X − Sᴛ, 0)
Sᴛ < X (in the money): exercise — sell at X when market is Sᴛ, payoff = X − Sᴛ
Sᴛ ≥ X (at or out of the money): do not exercise — payoff = 0
Put — key properties
  • Put profits when the stock falls — it is a bearish instrument (opposite of a call)
  • Maximum payoff = X (if the stock goes to zero, you sell worthless shares at X)
  • Maximum loss for the buyer = premium paid (if stock stays above X, option expires worthless)
  • Break-even: Sᴛ = X − premium (stock must fall below this for the put to be profitable)
Put option payoff & profit (X=50, premium=4)
Payoff = max(X−Sᴛ,0) · Profit = Payoff − 4
Payoff Profit Break-even: Sᴛ=46

It is essential to distinguish between payoff and profit. Payoff is the value received at expiration from exercising. Profit is the true economic gain — payoff minus the premium paid upfront (including the time value of money on the premium).

Profit = Payoff − Premium
Profit_call = max(Sᴛ−X, 0) − C₀
Profit_put = max(X−Sᴛ, 0) − P₀
Break-even points
Call break-evenSᴛ = X + premium
Put break-evenSᴛ = X − premium
Max loss (buyer)= premium paid
Max gain (call buyer)Unlimited (Sᴛ unbounded)
Max gain (put buyer)X − premium (if Sᴛ=0)

Note the critical asymmetry: the option seller has the exact mirror-image profile. The seller collects the premium upfront but faces unlimited losses (for a naked call seller) or large losses (for a put seller). The premium is just transfer of risk — what the buyer gains in asymmetry, the seller accepts as the counterparty.

Call vs Put profit comparison (X=50, C₀=6, P₀=4)

Call profit Put profit

The most intuitive way to understand options is as insurance policies. Just as you pay a premium to insure your car against accidents, you pay an option premium to protect a financial position against adverse price moves. The option gives you asymmetric exposure: you participate in favourable outcomes but are protected from unfavourable ones.

Protective put — the classic hedging strategy
Strategy: Hold 1 share of stock + Buy 1 put (strike X)
If Sᴛ falls below XPut pays X−Sᴛ → total value = X
If Sᴛ rises above XPut worthless → total value = Sᴛ
The protective put creates a floor at X — the portfolio value can never fall below X at expiration, while all upside above X is retained. This is exactly how insurance works: you sacrifice the premium for downside protection while keeping the upside.
Floor = X. Cost of floor = put premium paid upfront
Protective put — value at expiration (S₀=50, X=50, put premium=4)
Stock alone vs Stock + Put
Stock alone (Sᴛ) Stock + Put (insured) Floor at X=50
Why options are valuable — the asymmetry principle
  • More volatility (σ↑) → higher chance of extreme moves in either direction → higher payoff potential → option value rises for both calls AND puts
  • More time (T↑) → more opportunity for large price moves → option value usually rises
  • An option is always worth at least its intrinsic value (payoff if exercised today) — time value is strictly non-negative

Put-Call Parity

Put-call parity is one of the most important no-arbitrage relationships in finance. It precisely links the prices of calls, puts, the underlying stock, and a risk-free bond. If the relationship is violated, a riskless arbitrage profit exists — two portfolios with identical payoffs in every future state have different prices today, allowing you to buy the cheap one and sell the expensive one for a guaranteed profit.

Put-Call Parity (European options, no dividends)
C₀ + X/(1+R)ᵀ = P₀ + S₀
C₀ = call price · P₀ = put price · S₀ = stock price today
X/(1+R)ᵀ = PV of strike price (risk-free bond paying X at T)
Same strike X, same expiration T, European style, no dividends

The parity arises because two portfolios produce identical payoffs in all states at expiration. Portfolio A (Call + Bond) and Portfolio B (Stock + Put) both deliver max(Sᴛ, X) at time T. Since they are always worth the same at expiration, they must cost the same today — otherwise a riskless profit exists by buying the cheaper portfolio and shorting the expensive one.

Proof: both portfolios pay max(Sᴛ, X)
ScenarioPortfolio A: Call+BondPortfolio B: Stock+Put
Sᴛ > X (call ITM)Exercise call → SᴛPut worthless → Sᴛ
Sᴛ ≤ X (put ITM)Call worthless → X (bond)Exercise put → X
Both portfolios deliver max(Sᴛ, X) in all states → same price today

Portfolio B is overpriced relative to A. Arbitrage: short stock + short put + buy call + buy bond = $0.381 riskless profit today.
Arbitrage trade — step by step
  • Sell (short) 1 share → receive +$50
  • Sell (write) 1 put → receive +$4
  • Buy 1 call → pay −$6
  • Buy bond paying $50 at T=1 → pay −$47.619
  • Net cash today: 50 + 4 − 6 − 47.619 = +$0.381 riskless profit
  • At expiry: all obligations cancel perfectly in every scenario
Put-call parity — both portfolios at expiration (X=50)
Portfolio A = Call+Bond · Portfolio B = Stock+Put · Both = max(Sᴛ, X)
Portfolio A: Call + Bond (Call value + X) Portfolio B: Stock + Put Strike X=50
Both portfolios trace the same kinked line: flat at X=50 when Sᴛ≤50, then rising 1:1 with Sᴛ when Sᴛ>50. Identical payoffs → identical prices by no-arbitrage.

Key lesson — Exercise 2
Put-call parity is a law, not a guideline.   • C₀ + X/(1+R)ᵀ = P₀ + S₀ — put-call parity (European, no dividends)
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • Gordon Model requires R > g strictly — if g ≥ R, price is infinite/undefined; no firm can grow faster than the economy forever
  • Growth only creates value when ROE > R — reinvesting when ROE < R destroys value; higher payout would be better
  • DGM and NPVGO must give the same answer — they are two ways of writing the same thing; if they differ, you have a calculation error
  • P/E comparison requires like-for-like firms — comparing P/E across industries with different growth and risk is meaningless
  • Option payoff ≠ profit — profit subtracts the premium; break-even for a call is X + premium, not X
  • Put-call parity applies to European options only (no early exercise) and assumes no dividends; both conditions must hold
  • Higher volatility raises BOTH call and put values — never say "volatility is good for calls but bad for puts"; both benefit from extreme outcomes
  • Capital gains are not independent of dividends — they are entirely driven by market re-pricing of future dividends; a stock with no dividends ever is worth zero
STEP 1 — Understand the dividend discount model as the master framework
P₀ = Σ Dₜ/(1+R)ᵗ — stocks are just dividend perpetuities
Every stock valuation model is a special case of the infinite sum. Zero growth, Gordon Model, and two-stage models are all assumptions about the pattern of future dividends — simplifications that make the infinite sum tractable. Never lose sight of the master formula.
STEP 2 — Apply the Gordon Model and understand its limits
P₀ = D₁/(R−g) — elegant but requires care
The Gordon Model is powerful for mature, stable firms. But R must exceed g, and g must be sustainable forever. For high-growth firms, use the two-stage model. Know how to rearrange: P₀ to find R; P₀ to find g. Both R = D₁/P₀ + g and g = R − D₁/P₀ are essential rearrangements.
STEP 3 — Decompose value using the NPVGO model
P₀ = E₁/R + NPVGO — cash cow + growth
This is the most analytically powerful framing. It forces you to ask: is this firm's growth actually creating value? If ROE > R, growth is valuable. If ROE = R, growth is irrelevant. If ROE < R, pay out more. The NPVGO model also links cleanly to the P/E ratio and explains why P/E varies across firms.
STEP 4 — Master option payoffs and the asymmetry principle
max(Sᴛ−X, 0) and max(X−Sᴛ, 0) — always draw the diagram
The kinked payoff diagram is the key to understanding options. Always draw it. Calls benefit from upside, puts from downside. The kink at X is the exercise decision boundary. Profit diagrams shift down by the premium — this determines break-even and maximum loss.
STEP 5 — Apply put-call parity and identify arbitrage
C + X/(1+R)ᵀ = P + S₀ — a law, not a guideline
Always verify parity by computing both sides. If they differ, identify which side is overpriced and construct the arbitrage: short the expensive portfolio, long the cheap one. The cash inflow today is the riskless profit. All future payoffs will cancel. Parity also lets you derive any one price from the other three — a useful shortcut.
One-line synthesis — Stocks & Options
Stocks are valued as the present value of all future dividends — whether through the dividend discount model, the Gordon formula, the two-stage model, or the NPVGO decomposition. Options are contracts that transfer the right (not obligation) to transact at a fixed price, and their value comes entirely from the asymmetry of that right. Put-call parity links both worlds through a single no-arbitrage equation, reminding us that every financial price is ultimately determined by the principle that two portfolios with identical future payoffs must cost the same today.
