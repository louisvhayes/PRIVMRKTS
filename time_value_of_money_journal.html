Foundations: Why Time Matters

The time value of money (TVM) is the single most important concept in finance. It underpins every investment decision, every valuation model, and every capital budgeting calculation. The core idea is deceptively simple: a pound today is worth more than a pound in the future. But understanding exactly why — and how to quantify that difference — is the foundation of all financial analysis.

Why a pound today is more valuable
  • Opportunity cost — money today can be invested immediately and begin earning returns
  • Inflation — purchasing power erodes over time; a pound buys less in the future
  • Risk — future payments are uncertain; a promise to pay is not the same as cash in hand
  • Preference — humans inherently prefer consumption now over consumption later
Two fundamental valuation questions
  • Future Value (FV) — if I invest £X today at rate r for T periods, how much will I have at the end?
  • Present Value (PV) — if I am promised £X at a future date, what is that promise worth in today's money?
  • All TVM problems are variations of these two questions — even the most complex annuity or perpetuity reduces to PV or FV
  • The discount rate r is the bridge between present and future — it encodes opportunity cost and risk
The two core preferences in finance
  • A pound today is preferred to a pound in the future — time preference
  • A certain pound is preferred to a risky one — risk preference
  • These two preferences drive the entire structure of interest rates and discount rates in finance
  • A higher discount rate reflects higher risk or a stronger preference for receiving cash sooner

The One-Period Case: FV and PV

The simplest TVM case involves a single cash flow today and a single cash flow in one period's time. This one-period building block is the foundation for everything that follows — multi-period compounding, annuities, and perpetuities are all extensions of this same core logic.

Future Value — One Period
FV = C₀ × (1 + r)
C₀ = cash flow today · r = interest rate per period
Invest C₀ today at rate r; receive FV next period
Present Value — One Period
PV = C₁ / (1 + r)
C₁ = cash flow at date 1 · r = interest rate per period
Discounting: finding the value today of a future cash flow
Discounting is simply the reverse of compounding. If you know the future value, divide by (1+r) to find today's value. If you know today's value, multiply by (1+r) to find the future value.
— Time Value of Money Foundations
One-period FV example
Invest £100 at 5% interest for one year:
FV = 100 × (1 + 0.05) = £105
  • £100 — principal repayment (100 × 1)
  • £5 — interest earned (100 × 0.05)
  • £105 — total future value at year-end
One-period PV example
You are promised £100 in one year. Interest rate is 5%. What is it worth today?
PV = 100 / 1.05 = £95.24
This is the amount you would need to deposit today at 5% to have exactly £100 in one year. The £95.24 and the future £100 are economically equivalent at a 5% discount rate.

Multi-Period Compounding & Discounting

When we extend the one-period case to multiple periods, a powerful phenomenon emerges: compounding. Each period, the investment earns interest not just on the original principal but also on the interest accumulated in all previous periods. This creates exponential — not linear — growth, and it is the engine behind long-term wealth creation.

Future Value — Multi-Period
FV = C₀ × (1 + r)ᵀ
C₀ = initial cash flow · r = rate per period · T = number of periods
(1+r)ᵀ is the future value factor (FVF)
Present Value — Multi-Period
PV = Cᴛ / (1 + r)ᵀ
Cᴛ = cash flow at time T · 1/(1+r)ᵀ is the discount factor (DF)
Higher r or T → smaller discount factor → lower PV
Compounding vs simple interest — why it matters
Deposit £100 at 10% p.a. for 5 years:
Simple interest100 + 5×(100×0.10) = £150
Compound interest100 × (1.10)⁵ = £161.05
Extra from compounding£11.05
The difference of £11.05 represents interest on interest — the core of compounding. Over longer periods and higher rates, this gap becomes enormous.

PV discounting example
How much to invest today to have £5,000 in 5 years at 10%?
PV = 5,000 / (1.10)⁵ = £3,104.61
Deposit £3,104.61 today at 10% per year. After 5 years it will have grown to exactly £5,000. The £1,895.39 difference is the interest accumulated via compounding.
Solving for the required rate — "What rate is enough?"
College will cost £50,000 in 12 years. You have £5,000 today. What return must you earn?
5,000 × (1 + r)¹² = 50,000
→ (1+r)¹² = 10    → 1+r = 10^(1/12)    → r ≈ 21.15% per year
You need to earn approximately 21.15% per year for 12 years to turn £5,000 into £50,000. This illustrates how TVM can be rearranged to solve for any unknown: FV, PV, r, or T.
Rearranging: r = (FV/PV)^(1/T) − 1 is the general formula for solving for the rate
The four TVM variables — any one can be the unknown
Solve for FVFV = PV × (1+r)ᵀ
Solve for PVPV = FV / (1+r)ᵀ
Solve for r(FV/PV)^(1/T) − 1
Solve for Tln(FV/PV) / ln(1+r)
Every TVM problem gives you three of these four variables and asks you to find the fourth. Mastering rearrangement is the key skill.

Compounding Frequency & the Effective Annual Rate

In practice, interest is rarely compounded just once per year. Mortgages compound monthly, bonds typically semi-annually, and savings accounts may compound daily. The quoted annual rate (also called the nominal rate or APR) tells you the rate before accounting for intra-year compounding. The Effective Annual Rate (EAR) tells you the true annual cost or return after accounting for how often compounding actually occurs.

Future Value — m Compounding Periods per Year
FV = C₀ × (1 + r/m)^(m×T)
r = quoted annual rate · m = compounding frequency per year
T = years · r/m = rate per compounding period
Effective Annual Rate (EAR)
EAR = (1 + r/m)ᵐ − 1
The annual rate that produces the same end-of-year result as the given compounding frequency. Always use EAR when comparing across products with different compounding frequencies.

The key insight is that more frequent compounding produces a higher effective return for the same quoted rate. A 12% rate compounded monthly is not the same as 12% compounded annually — the monthly version is actually more expensive (for a borrower) or more lucrative (for a lender).

Why more frequent compounding increases the effective rate
  • With monthly compounding, interest earned in January earns interest in February — before the year is out
  • With annual compounding, that same interest sits idle until the following year before itself earning interest
  • The more frequent the compounding, the more periods interest has to compound on itself within the year
  • In the limit (continuous compounding): EAR = e^r − 1, where e ≈ 2.71828

Growing Perpetuity — Delayed

A perpetuity is a constant stream of cash flows that lasts forever. The first payment occurs at date 1. Despite paying out infinitely, a perpetuity has a finite present value because each successive payment is discounted more heavily than the last — the discount factor shrinks towards zero as time approaches infinity.

Perpetuity Present Value
PV = C / r
C = constant cash flow per period · r = discount rate per period
Assumes first payment at date 1 (end of first period)

The intuition: if you deposit PV = C/r at rate r, you will earn exactly C in interest each period forever — and never need to touch the principal. The cash flows are paid entirely from interest.

Real-world examples of perpetuities
  • Consols — UK government bonds historically issued with no maturity date; pay a fixed coupon forever
  • Preferred shares — fixed dividend payments with no redemption date are often modelled as perpetuities
  • Endowment funds — designed to pay out a fixed percentage of capital annually in perpetuity
  • Land rents — ground rents on long leaseholds can be modelled as perpetuities in valuation
Perpetuity worked example
What is the PV of a perpetuity of £15 per year if the discount rate is 10%?
PV = 15 / 0.10 = £150
Interpretation: £150 deposited today at 10% per year will generate exactly £15 of interest every year forever. The £150 principal is never consumed.
Why perpetuity PV is finite despite infinite payments
  • Each future payment is multiplied by a discount factor: 1/(1+r)ᵗ
  • As t → ∞, the discount factor → 0, so each distant payment contributes almost nothing to PV
  • The infinite sum C/r + C/r² + C/r³ + … converges to the geometric series C/r
  • At r=10%, a payment in year 100 has a PV of C × (1/1.10¹⁰⁰) ≈ C × 0.000073 — essentially zero

A growing perpetuity extends the perpetuity by allowing each cash flow to be larger than the previous one by a constant growth rate g. The cash flows are: C, C(1+g), C(1+g)², … forever. This is the foundation of the Gordon Growth Model for stock valuation — where dividends grow at a constant rate forever.

Growing Perpetuity Present Value
PV = C / (r − g)
C = first cash flow (at date 1) · r = discount rate · g = constant growth rate
⚠ Requires r > g — otherwise PV is infinite (the denominator must be positive)

The constraint that r must exceed g is critical. If growth ever equalled or exceeded the discount rate, the sum of discounted cash flows would be infinite — meaning the asset would have an infinite value today, which is economically impossible in equilibrium.

The Gordon Growth Model — stock valuation application
For a stock that pays a dividend D₁ next year, growing at rate g forever, with required return r:
P₀ = D₁ / (r − g)
This is one of the most important formulas in equity valuation. It shows that stock price rises when dividends increase, required return falls, or the long-run growth rate increases.
Growing perpetuity — worked example
PV of a perpetuity of £15 per year that grows at 5% forever, discounted at 10%?
PV = 15 / (0.10 − 0.05) = 15 / 0.05 = £300
Compare to the flat perpetuity: PV was £150 with no growth. The same first payment of £15 is now worth £300 because each future payment is larger than in the flat case. Growth doubles the value.
Flat perpetuity (g=0)PV = £150
Growing perpetuity (g=5%)PV = £300
Value added by growth+£150
REMEMBER: Present Value (PV) = How much that future income stream is worth today NOT how much you have to invest to recieve X amount in the future. We are comparing two different income streams, not two investments. The present value of a growing perpetuity measures the value today of a stream of cash flows that increase over time, and it can be compared to a flat perpetuity to show how growth increases value.

A common complication is when the first cash flow is delayed — the stream starts not at date 1 but at some later date. The standard formula always gives the PV one period before the first payment. If the first payment is at date 2 instead of date 1, the formula gives PV at date 1, which must then be discounted back one further period to date 0.

Delayed Growing Perpetuity — two steps
Step 1: PV₁ = C / (r − g)
This gives the value ONE period before the first payment
Step 2: Discount back to date 0
PV₀ = PV₁ / (1 + r)
Divide by (1+r) for each period between date 0 and PV₁
Delayed growing perpetuity — worked example
First payment of £15 occurs at date 2 (not date 1). Growing at 5%, discount rate 10%.
Step 1: PV at date 115 / (0.10−0.05) = £300
Step 2: Discount to date 0300 / 1.10 = £272.73
The one-year delay reduces the present value from £300 to £272.73 — a reduction of £27.27, which is exactly the opportunity cost of waiting one year at 10%. Every year of delay costs you 1/(1+r) in present value.
General rule: if first payment is at date n, compute PV at date n−1, then discount n−1 more years

Annuities & Growing Annuities

An annuity is a stream of equal cash flows paid at regular intervals for a fixed number of periods. Unlike a perpetuity, it has a definite end date. Annuities are ubiquitous in finance: mortgages, car loans, bond coupons, pension payments, and lease agreements are all annuities. The growing annuity extends this to cash flows that increase at a constant rate each period.

Annuity Present Value
PV = C × [1 − (1+r)^(−T)] / r
C = constant cash flow per period · r = discount rate per period · T = number of periods
The term in brackets is the annuity factor (AF). Note: AF = (1 − DF) / r where DF = 1/(1+r)ᵀ
Growing Annuity Present Value
PV = C/(r−g) × [1 − ((1+g)/(1+r))ᵀ]
C = first cash flow · g = growth rate · r = discount rate · T = number of periods
Reduces to annuity formula when g=0; reduces to growing perpetuity when T→∞

The annuity formula is simply the difference between two perpetuities: one starting at date 1 (which gives all payments from date 1 to infinity) minus one starting at date T+1 (which removes all payments beyond T). This "perpetuity difference" trick is a powerful way to derive and remember the formula.

Annuity — car loan example
You can afford £400 per month. Interest rate: 7% per year (= 0.5833%/month). Loan term: 36 months. What car can you afford?
PV = 400 × [1 − (1 + 0.07/12)^(−36)] / (0.07/12)
= 400 × annuity factor = £12,954.59
You can afford a car priced at up to £12,954.59. The 36 monthly payments of £400 (total: £14,400) repay both the £12,954.59 principal and £1,445.41 of interest.
Growing annuity — retirement savings example
Retirement plan pays £20,000/year for 40 years, increasing by 3%/year. Discount rate: 10%.
PV = 20,000/(0.10−0.03) × [1−(1.03/1.10)⁴⁰]
= 285,714.29 × 0.9282 = £265,121.57
The retirement plan is worth £265,121.57 at retirement. Despite starting at just £20,000/year, the 3% annual growth significantly increases total value — compare to flat annuity which would give only ~£195,000.

Mortgage Amortisation — How Each Payment Splits

A mortgage is an annuity: a fixed periodic payment that repays both principal and interest over a set term. The clever and important feature is that although the total payment stays constant, the split between interest and principal changes each period. Early payments are mostly interest; late payments are mostly principal. This is called an amortisation schedule.

Mortgage setup — example
Principal (PV)£250,000
Annual interest rate6% p.a.
Semi-annual rate (rₛ)3% per semester
Term10 semesters (5 years)
Constant payment C£29,307.63
Solving for the constant payment C
250,000 = C × [1 − (1.03)^(−10)] / 0.03
→ C = 250,000 / annuity factor = £29,307.63 per semester
How one row of the schedule is calculated
  • Opening Balance — the outstanding principal at the start of the period
  • Interest = rₛ × Opening Balance (3% × outstanding principal)
  • Principal = C − Interest (the remainder of the payment reduces debt)
  • Closing Balance = Opening Balance − Principal repaid
  • Repeat each semester using that period's opening (= previous closing) balance
Semester 1 interest: 0.03 × £250,000 = £7,500 · Principal: £29,307.63 − £7,500 = £21,807.63

Worked Exercises

The following exercises demonstrate the core TVM techniques applied to real decision-making scenarios. Every TVM problem follows the same logic: identify the cash flows, identify the timing, identify what you are solving for, and apply the right formula.

Exercise 1: Sell the Bike?
Exercise 2: Mr. Rogers' Retirement
Exercise 1 — Present Value Comparison
You want to sell your Ducato bike. Two buyers make offers:
Buyer 1£76,200 today
Buyer 2£93,750 in 2 years
Interest rate11% per year
To compare fairly, we must bring both offers to the same point in time. We discount Buyer 2's offer to today using the 11% rate.
Solution — discount both to today
PV(Buyer 1) = £76,200 (already today)
PV(Buyer 2) = 93,750 / (1.11)² = £76,089.60
PV — Buyer 1£76,200.00
PV — Buyer 2£76,089.60
Difference£110.40 in favour of Buyer 1
Decision: Sell to Buyer 1. Despite offering £17,550 less in nominal terms, Buyer 1's offer is worth more in present value because you can invest the money immediately at 11%.
Key lesson — Exercise 1
You can never directly compare cash flows at different points in time. A larger number in the future is not necessarily worth more than a smaller number today. Always bring all cash flows to the same date — today is the most convenient — before comparing. The discount rate is the cost of time.
Exercise 2 — Growing Annuity + FV Compounding
Mr. Rogers saves for retirement:
Current salary$50,000
Annual contribution2% of salary
First contributionIn 1 year (t=1)
Salary growth rate g4% per year
Account interest rate r8% per year
Saving period T40 years
Question: How much will Mr. Rogers have after 40 years?
Solution — two-step approach
STEP 1: First contribution
C₁ = 0.02 × $50,000 × 1.04 = $1,040
Salary at t=1 is 50,000 × 1.04; contribution is 2% of that
STEP 2: PV of growing annuity at t=0
PV₀ = (1,040 / (0.08−0.04)) × [1 − (1.04/1.08)⁴⁰]
= 26,000 × 0.7790 ≈ $20,254.12
STEP 3: Compound PV forward 40 years at 8%
FV₄₀ = 20,254.12 × (1.08)⁴⁰ ≈ $440,011
Mr. Rogers will have ≈ $440,011 at retirement.
Key lesson — Exercise 2
For growing annuity problems requiring a future value: always find the PV first (using the growing annuity formula), then compound that PV forward to the target date. The two-step approach — PV then FV — works for any stream of cash flows regardless of complexity. Also notice how even modest contributions (just 2% of salary) compounded over 40 years accumulate to a very substantial sum — the power of time and compounding.

All TVM formulas — exam reference
  • FV = C₀(1+r)ᵀ — compound a single cash flow forward T periods
  • PV = Cᴛ/(1+r)ᵀ — discount a single future cash flow back to today
  • FV = C₀(1+r/m)^(mT) — FV with m compounding periods per year
  • EAR = (1+r/m)ᵐ − 1 — true annual rate accounting for intra-year compounding
  • PV(Perpetuity) = C/r — constant cash flow forever, first payment at t=1
  • PV(Growing Perpetuity) = C/(r−g) — requires r > g; first payment C at t=1
  • PV(Annuity) = C × [1−(1+r)^(−T)]/r — T equal payments, first at t=1
  • PV(Growing Annuity) = C/(r−g) × [1−((1+g)/(1+r))ᵀ] — growing payments for T periods
  • r = (FV/PV)^(1/T) − 1 — solve for the implied rate of return
  • T = ln(FV/PV) / ln(1+r) — solve for the number of periods
Common mistakes & misconceptions
  • Never compare cash flows at different times directly — always discount or compound to a common date first
  • Period consistency — if payments are monthly, the rate r must also be per month (divide annual rate by 12)
  • Growing perpetuity requires r > g — if g ≥ r the formula gives a negative or infinite value, which is meaningless
  • Perpetuity/annuity formula gives PV one period before first payment — if first payment is at t=2, formula gives PV at t=1; discount one more year back to t=0
  • EAR ≠ quoted rate when m > 1 — always use EAR when comparing products with different compounding frequencies
  • Compounding is exponential, not linear — simple interest gives 150; compound gives 161.05 at 10%/5yrs
  • Higher discount rate = lower PV — a higher r makes the future worth less in today's terms; do not invert this
  • FV then discount ≠ same as PV then compound — they are equivalent only if you use the correct period rate consistently throughout
STEP 1 — Master one-period FV and PV
FV = C₀(1+r) and PV = C₁/(1+r)
Everything in TVM is an extension of these two expressions. FV is compounding forward; PV is discounting back. The interest rate r bridges past and future. Understand why £1 today and £(1+r) in one year are equivalent before moving on.
STEP 2 — Extend to multiple periods and understand compounding
FV = C₀(1+r)ᵀ — the power of exponents
The jump from one period to many is just replacing (1+r) with (1+r)ᵀ. But the behaviour changes dramatically — growth becomes exponential. Practice with different T and r values to develop intuition for how much compounding adds. The "Rule of 72" (years to double ≈ 72/r%) is a useful mental shortcut.
STEP 3 — Understand compounding frequency and EAR
EAR = (1+r/m)ᵐ − 1 — the true cost of borrowing
A 12% rate means very different things depending on whether it is compounded annually, monthly, or daily. EAR standardises all rates to an annual basis for fair comparison. Always ask: what is the compounding frequency? Then compute EAR before comparing.
STEP 4 — Apply perpetuity and growing perpetuity formulas
PV = C/r and PV = C/(r−g) — infinite streams, finite values
The elegance of these formulas is that they collapse an infinite sum into a simple fraction. The growing perpetuity is the foundation of the Gordon Growth Model. Always check: is first payment at date 1? If it's delayed, remember to discount back one additional period per year of delay.
STEP 5 — Master annuities and build amortisation schedules
PV = C × [1−(1+r)^(−T)]/r — finite streams, mortgages, loans
Annuities are perpetuity differences: a T-period annuity = perpetuity starting t=1 minus perpetuity starting t=T+1. Once you can price an annuity, you can price any loan or bond. The amortisation schedule shows the mechanics: constant total payment, shifting mix of interest and principal.
One-line synthesis — Time Value of Money
Every financial valuation — a stock, a bond, a business, a project — is just a collection of future cash flows discounted back to today at an appropriate rate. The time value of money is not a technique; it is the bedrock logic of all of finance. Master the four formulas (FV, PV, perpetuity, annuity) and the ability to rearrange them, and you hold the key to every valuation problem you will ever encounter.
